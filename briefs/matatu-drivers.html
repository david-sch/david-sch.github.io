Policy Brief

Safer Driving for a Price: paying minibus drivers to drive safely

David Schönholzer, Gregory Lane, and Erin Kelley · randomized controlled trial, Nairobi, Kenya

Where traffic laws go largely unenforced, paying drivers directly for safer driving — using the GPS trackers already in most commercial vehicles — measurably reduces dangerous driving at no cost to drivers' earnings. The catch: the gains fade once the payments stop.

The problem

Road traffic injuries are the leading cause of death for people aged 5–29, and low- and middle-income countries bear 92% of road fatalities while owning only 60% of the world's vehicles. In Nairobi, minibuses ("matatus") are involved in a disproportionate share of crashes: drivers are residual claimants who profit from every extra passenger, so they have strong incentives to speed and maneuver aggressively. Conventional regulation depends on enforcement capacity that is often absent.

The intervention

In an RCT with 203 minibuses (77 treated, 126 control), treated drivers began each day with a 600 KES bonus — about 60% of average daily earnings — from which we deducted penalties for unsafe driving recorded by an in-vehicle GPS tracker (speeding, hard braking, sharp turns, over-acceleration). Crucially, each driver was scored against their own baseline, not against other drivers, and no driver could end up worse off than without the scheme. Drivers received a nightly SMS reporting what they earned or lost.

  • Incentives work even with weak enforcement. Pricing unsafe driving cut speeding by 29% and harsh braking by 13%, improving a composite safety index by 0.096 standard deviations.
  • No measurable cost to drivers. Revenue and take-home pay were essentially unchanged; drivers worked modestly longer (about 43 minutes, 6.8%) to protect earnings.
  • No gaming of the system. Granular 30-second tracker data show no shift of unsafe driving to nighttime, off-route, or out-of-metro periods — the safety gains are genuine, not displaced.
  • But the gains are not self-sustaining. Safety reverts toward baseline once payments end. A longer (two-month) exposure cushions the reversal in speeding, but improvements still fade — evidence of only modest habit formation.
  • Plausibly cost-effective. The scheme would need to avert roughly 0.5–1.1 accidents to break even; the point estimate is about 1.9 accidents averted, before counting non-fatal injuries and property damage.

Policy implications

  • A deployable lever for weak-enforcement settings. The approach needs no new infrastructure — it runs on the telematics insurers and fleet owners already install — so insurers, fleet owners, transport authorities, or police could operate it.
  • Benchmark against each driver's own history. Scoring drivers against their own baseline is fairer and rewards improvement on the margin each driver can actually move.
  • Treat it as a standing contract, not a one-off campaign. Because behavior reverts when pricing stops, durable safety gains require keeping unsafe driving priced on a continuing basis rather than running a temporary push.
  • Target the most dangerous routes first. Given finite budgets, continuous pay-for-safety contracts are most valuable on the highest-risk, least-enforced routes.
