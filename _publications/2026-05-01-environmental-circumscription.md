State capacity is associated with long-run development but the causes of early state formation remain debated. A prominent theory in anthropology suggests that states form due to environmental circumscription—high land productivity surrounded by an unproductive periphery. The classic version of the theory posits that circumscription accelerates intergroup conflict and inhibits out-migration, which fosters state formation. We propose an expanded theory that also accommodates cooperation and social stratification within groups. We combine archaeological, historical, and environmental data to test circumscription theory and its underlying mechanisms across the globe. Circumscription is associated with early state formation, and both intergroup conflict and cooperation are at play. Persistent social stratification, by contrast, appears to be primarily a consequence rather than a precursor of the state. The pace of change after the arrival of farming is higher in circumscribed regions than in similar comparison regions. Which pathway dominates depends on context: circumscription can foster state formation through conflict, cooperation, or both.
