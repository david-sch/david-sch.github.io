Local governments provide many essential public goods and services, such as education, policing, and public infrastructure. Using a sample of 1.5 million homes near thousands of local government boundaries, we estimate the value of access to these governments, separately for cities and for school districts. House prices jump discontinuously even when comparing homes on the same residential street, with cities and school districts valued jointly rather than independently. A variance decomposition attributes 12% of house price variation to local governments and 19% to neighborhoods. An equilibrium sorting model reveals substantial heterogeneity in valuations across racial groups.
