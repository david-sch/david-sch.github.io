Information and Strategy in Lemon Markets: Improving Safety in Informal Transit

Erin Kelley, Gregory Lane, and David Schönholzer

Abstract

Road traffic accidents in poorly regulated public transit systems are a leading cause of death in low- and middle-income countries. We collect data on safe driving practices using a newly developed tracking device installed in minibuses operating from one of Kenya's busiest bus stations. We randomly provide some passengers with information on the safest firm, but they do not respond to this private safety information. In the second phase of the experiment, we publicly advertise our tracking efforts with large banners. This leads to a noticeable shift in passenger choice towards the safest firm, prompting some firms to significantly improve their safety. We develop a structural model with firm effort choice in which firms differ in baseline safety and marginal effort costs, and in which the public signal creates firm- and market-specific demand prizes from safety certification. The fitted model rationalizes the heterogeneous firm response we observe in the data and implies monthly net welfare gains of about 803 thousand KES in the observed equilibrium, with most of the gains coming from safer service rather than passenger switching alone.

Introduction

Economists broadly agree that markets work poorly when consumers lack reliable information about product quality (Akerlof, 1970): consumer demand for products falls, markets shrink, and consumer welfare is depressed. Uncertainty about product quality is particularly high in low- and middle-income countries where state capacity is weak and laws that mandate the disclosure of information are difficult to enforce. This has contributed to the proliferation of low-quality products, including medicine (Bennett and Yin, 2018; Björkman Nyqvist et al., 2018), fertilizers (Bold et al., 2017), and grain (Bold et al., 2022). There is some evidence to suggest that providing information to consumers may improve welfare by guiding them toward better choices (Levin, 2001) or incentivizing firms to supply higher quality products (Bai, 2025). Yet the effectiveness of information campaigns likely depends on market structure. This includes perceived quality differences between suppliers, the level of competition these suppliers face (and their incentives to improve), and both customers' and suppliers' expectations of how each side of the market will respond.

In this paper we investigate the extent to which consumer information improves lemon markets, and how this depends on the market dynamics and strategic incentives firms face. We focus on Kenya’s informal public transit sector, which is dominated by small firms who compete for market share. Although the sector accounts for just 10% of all motor vehicles circulating in the country, it is responsible for a disproportionate share of traffic deaths. Macharia et al. (2009) find this to be as high as 70%. The problem is not an isolated one: informal transit plays a central role in making traffic fatalities a leading cause of death in many low- and middle-income countries (WHO, 2020). While the industry is perceived to be exceedingly unsafe, passengers lack reliable information that would allow them to distinguish safer options from the rest.

To this end, we run a randomized control trial (RCT) with more than a thousand passengers (i.e. consumers) at one of Kenya's busiest bus terminals. We equip 52 buses across five firms operating on a major route with safety tracking devices. We provide the safety data to bus managers, allowing them to monitor their drivers’ safety behavior and improve their safety performance. We also use this data to rate the top safety performer each month based on measures of speed and sharp breaking. The RCT then proceeds in two stages.

In the first stage, we randomly approach passengers and provide them with one of three pamphlets. Crucially, we do not share these pamphlets or discuss them with the companies at the station. Passengers in the ‘safety leader’ group receive a pamphlet that discusses road safety, and identifies the safest company operating on the route. Passengers in ‘salience’ group receive a pamphlet that discusses road safety and lists the companies offering their services at this station without identifying the safest one. Finally, the control group receives a small pamphlet that only features an ID key.1 We then monitor which transit company consumers choose. This allows us to see whether the safety information provided in the pamphlets affected passengers’ choice.

In the second stage, we erect large signs across the transit station publicizing our tracking efforts (which we refer to as a “public signal/campaign"), and continue to randomly offer passengers one of the three pamphlets detailed above. The public signal informs passengers that firms can potentially improve safety, and lets firms know that passengers may be aware of their safety performance. We continue to monitor consumers’ choice of transit company, and whether transit companies improve their safety performance. Passengers’ reaction to the safety leader treatment when tracking efforts are publicly revealed identifies how much they value the expected safety difference of the safest bus now that the tracking devices are known to be available. Conversely, how firms react reveals whether the perceived benefits of improving transit quality outweigh the costs to the companies (where the benefits and costs are a function of the cost of improving, the number of competitors, and the quality of services they provide).

We document three key findings. First, we find that the ‘safety leader’ treatment passengers do not respond to the safety information they receive privately before the public signal is in place. The behavior of passengers who receive the pamphlet that identifies the safest bus is the same as those who do not. This suggests that passengers do not perceive there to be large differences between bus companies when it comes to safety, and therefore they have no reason to switch based on this margin (relative to other features of the bus that may be driving their choices).

Second, we find that passengers do respond to safety leader information when they receive it with the public signal. In our preferred specification, passengers who receive the safety-leader pamphlet after the banners are installed are 10.9 percentage points more likely to choose the top safety performer, nearly doubling its market share. This suggests that the same information becomes more valuable once passengers expect firms to compete on the safety margin.

Third, we find that the public campaign improved transit safety among the lower-performing companies. On average, high-speeding alerts fall by about 25%, and the largest safety gains are concentrated among the initially less safe firms. We rationalize these demand and supply responses with a structural model in which firms draw baseline safety and marginal effort costs, passengers respond to certification, and the public signal creates firm- and market-specific demand prizes from winning. In the fitted model, the public signal induces heterogeneous effort responses with a catch-up pattern rather than uniform improvement or pull-away.

There may be other reasons beyond the scope of the model that could explain the results, but we do not find evidence for these alternative explanations. First, passengers may perceive private information to be more trustworthy after the introduction of the public signal. The experiment was designed to make the information credible (Bai, 2025): enumerators dressed professionally, displayed laminated photo ID badges, and explained that the project was supported by the National Transport Safety Authority and by international universities. Furthermore, in a sub-experiment where we subsidized passengers to choose the safest bus, we found that consumers were equally likely to trust the subsidy offer and switch to the safer bus both before and after the public campaign. This suggests that the public campaign did not affect passengers’ trust in our efforts. Second, the public signal may have increased the salience of safety. However, we do not see passengers who receive the ‘salience treatment’ pamphlet (which makes safety salient) changing their behavior before the public signal. Third, passengers may be subject to an interviewer demand effect after the public signal. However, their stated preferences for safety remain unchanged, which minimizes concerns of interviewer demand effects. Fourth, passengers might worry about the social implications of their choice, but since they receive information privately, their actions are difficult for others to interpret. Fifth, while passengers might coordinate their choice of the safer bus under the public signal, the serial correlation of choices among consecutive passengers remains low and insignificant. Finally, while the safety improvements we observe could be due to mean reversion, our safety index is relatively stable in the three months before and after the introduction of the public signal.

This model provides a foundation for welfare analysis of consumer information in markets with strategic firms. Consumer surplus in the fitted model can be decomposed into a reallocation term, an intrinsic-quality term that captures safer service even for non-switchers, and an entropy / variety term. We use the randomized subsidy to convert passenger demand responses into KES, and we recover producer effort costs structurally from the public-period first-order condition.

In the observed equilibrium, the fitted model implies monthly net welfare of about 803 thousand KES. Consumer surplus rises by about 1.151 million KES per month, producer effort costs are about 318.8 thousand KES, and campaign costs are 30 thousand KES. Most of the gain comes from intrinsic quality improvements rather than from passenger switching alone.

A strength of our framework is that it also organizes policy counterfactuals along economically meaningful margins. We use the fitted model to study stronger passenger demand for safety, threshold policing of unsafe service, and changes in market structure. In the current calibration, stronger safety demand raises welfare, the simple policing rules we study reduce welfare, and the market-structure counterfactual is hump-shaped. We treat the fitted covariance between baseline safety and cost as a mechanism object that helps explain catch-up, rather than as a central policy counterfactual on its own.

This paper makes three primary contributions. First, we contribute to the literature on lemon markets in low-income countries. In high-income countries, inefficiencies in these markets are typically addressed through government regulation and inspection. However, in low-income countries with weak state capacity, fewer solutions have been identified and studied. Bai (2018) shows that providing tamper-proof technologies to firms encourages them to offer higher-quality goods, thereby fostering consumer trust. (Björkman Nyqvist et al., 2018) and (Bennett and Yin, 2018) find that the entry of large firms can encourage other producers to improve their product quality. Our study introduces a novel approach to addressing inefficiencies in lemon markets by combining consumer information with public signals that emphasize the benefits of offering higher-quality products. We demonstrate that such a strategy can prompt firms to respond strategically, leading to overall welfare gains.

Second, we contribute to a small body of research at the intersection of market regulation and consumer product information. For instance, (Jin and Leslie, 2003) examine the introduction of hygiene grades for restaurants in Los Angeles County, finding improvements in service quality and an increase in demand for high-quality establishments. Similarly, (Barahona et al., 2020) analyze the effects of a warning label mandate on unhealthy foods, estimating both demand and supply responses. Our study builds on this literature by using a randomized control trial to estimate consumer welfare gains in a low-income country. Moreover, we show how experimental demand responses, supply responses, and a structural model with heterogeneous firms can be combined to evaluate policy counterfactuals over information, policing, and competition.

Finally, we contribute to a growing literature on road safety in low- and middle-income countries. Road traffic accidents contribute to a staggering loss of life, and limit economic development (Odero et al., 2003; Cervero and Golub, 2007; Raynor and Mirzoev, 2014). There is emerging literature on the impact of potential solutions. For instance, (Habyarimana and Jack, 2011; Habyarimana and Jack, 2015) found that placing stickers in Kenyan minibuses encouraging passengers to report dangerous driving reduces average speeds. Our study expands on this method by directly engaging passengers with minibus safety ratings before they board, potentially moving the market towards a safer equilibrium. Furthermore, we observe that providing information to passengers can motivate bus companies to operate more safety, addressing some of the inefficiencies typical in markets with asymmetric information. Crucially, we show how these consumer information campaigns may benefit from complementary public signals that incentivize improvements from the supply side.

Context: Informal Transit in Kenya

According to the WHO an estimated 1.35 million people are killed annually in road accidents and as many as 50 million individuals are injured worldwide (WHO, 2020). More than 90% of these deaths occur in low and middle-income countries, which have less than 60% of the world’s vehicles. In Kenya alone, approximately 3,000 to 13,000 people die each year as a result of reckless driving (WHO, 2015). These road accidents often involve public transportation vehicles, which are called matatus in Kenya. In many low-income countries including Kenya, the public transportation sector is dominated by private minibuses that are notoriously unsafe: drivers often speed, stop suddenly, and perform other dangerous maneuvers in order to collect more passengers and arrive at their destinations more quickly. According to one study, matatus account for 11% of registered vehicles but 70.2% of passenger casualties (Macharia et al., 2009). As a point of comparison, buses in the US account for 1% of registered vehicles and 0.4% of casualties (BTS, 2016).

One reason why minibus companies undersupply safe rides is that passengers cannot observe how safe a bus is before they board. This information asymmetry between firms and consumers means that companies have weak incentives to provide safe rides as they cannot hope to capture extra revenue by supplying this unobserved quality, even if it is demanded by passengers.2 This leads to a scenario where there is potentially unmet demand for safety, a scenario that can be thought of as a typical lemons market. This status quo jeopardizes not only the safety of passengers on board, but also other vehicles on the road, and pedestrians.

Governments and international institutions are continuously looking for ways to address road safety. This can be challenging in low-income countries where state capacity is weak and regulations are difficult to enact and enforce. Only 28 countries, representing 7% of the world's population, have implemented laws that address all five road risk factors (speed, drunk driving, helmets, seat-belts and child restraints). Less than 35% of low- and middle-income countries have policies in place to protect road users, despite experiencing the highest fatality rates in the world. In Kenya, the government passed the Michuki rules in 2003 requiring that buses install speed limiters, safety belts, and exhibit valid licenses (Michuki, 2003). To date, this limited set of regulations are poorly enforced by the Kenyan police service, which is notoriously corrupt.

An alternative to government regulation is to empower customers with information. There have been some attempts to do this in Kenya. Ma3 route is a mobile/web/SMS platform in Nairobi that crowd-sources for up to date transportation data, and provides users with information on traffic, matatu directions and driving reports. Similarly, (Habyarimana and Jack, 2015) launched the Zusha Road Safety Campaign by placing stickers inside matatus that encourage passengers to speak up against bad driving. In a similar spirit, we implement an experiment in Kenya's public transit industry to directly alleviate information asymmetries between minibus firms and their customers in an effort to improve road safety.

Experimental Design and Data

Design of Experiment

Study site. We conducted our experiment in one of the largest bus terminals in Kenya, operating near Nairobi's central business district (see Appendix Figure A2). The terminal provides service between Nairobi and Kisumu, one of the country's most important and busiest long-distance bus routes, which is notoriously unsafe. The terminal serves both regular business passengers that transit between Nairobi and Kisumu several times a year, as well many passengers who infrequently travel between the two locations.

The study location was well suited to the study's requirements. It was a well defined location, with two clear entry points and less congested than other stations in the vicinity, which meant that we could more easily intercept passengers (see Appendix Figure A2). We worked with five different companies (commonly referred to as SACCOS) and we fit a minimum of six buses in the SACCO’s fleet (of approximately 50 buses) with our GPS tracking device. After completing the installations of the tracking devices, we collected one month of tracking data for each bus. We then used this information to reliably compare the safety performance of one company to another based on sharp breaking and speeding alerts.

Safety measurement technology. The intervention required the ability to collect information about the safety of the minibuses in the sample. Kenya, like most low-income countries, lacks reliable data on safety and road accidents. Hence, we developed a new monitoring system for matatus that can pick up safety behavior with high accuracy. The system was also considerably cheaper, more flexible and more user-friendly than traditional tracking devices. The physical tracking units were procured from a company in the United States. They featured a GPS and a 3-axis accelerometer for motion sense, tilt and impact detection. The device captured the vehicle's location and forward/backward/lateral/vertical acceleration at 30 second intervals. The device was also calibrated to generate alerts for every instance of vehicle speeding and sharp braking. These safety alerts were calculated by an internal algorithm built into the device with threshold parameters as inputs (which were calibrated to the Kenyan road conditions to capture context-appropriate levels of unsafe driving). Further processing of the system data on the server provided additional measures of interest including the total number of kilometers traveled that day, the total time the matatu was running, and a safety index (from aggregating the day’s safety alerts on sharp breaking and speeding). This information served two purposes. First, it was conveyed to minibus managers, who could use the information to monitor and address unsafe driving. Second, it was used to assign a Top Safety Performer badge to the company that operated most safely that month.

Information treatments. Our field team intercepted passengers as they entered the bus terminal area, but before they had an opportunity to purchase a ticket for a specific SACCO. We successfully intercepted approximately 30% of the individuals we approached, resulting in a sample size of 1,186 passengers during the main study period. We randomly assigned passengers to one of three arms that differed in the amount of information we provided.3 In the control group, passengers received a pamphlet with an ID key printed on it, but nothing more. In the salience group, passengers received a pamphlet that contained a message about the safety of the matatu industry, a picture of a matatu after an accident, and a list of the five SACCOs operating from the terminal (see Figure 1). Finally, passengers assigned to the safety leader group, received the same pamphlet as the salience group with one notable exception: one of the five SACCOs appeared prominently with the message “Top Safety performer" alongside it. The enumerator carefully explained what this meant and how the title was awarded. Once the pamphlets were discussed with the subject, enumerators asked them to proceed to buying a bus ticket of their choice and to show it to a second enumerator stationed further down the road where the buses were leaving from, which allowed us to measure their actual firm choice. Before passengers left to choose their bus, enumerators tore off the bottom of the pamphlets where the safety information was displayed. This served the purpose of keeping the safety information private from the SACCO managers. Passengers received 50 KES (0.50 USD) for showing their ticket to the second enumerator.

The three information treatment arms were designed to identify two effects. First, comparing the control group to the salience group identifies whether priming passengers about matatu safety affects their choice of firm. Second, the comparison of the safety treatment group to the salience group identifies the impact of revealing new information about which firm is in fact the safest option on the route. To the extent that passengers use this new information to update their priors about safety, we would expect this treatment to increase the probability that passengers choose the firm marked as “Top Safety performer".

Public signal. Three months into delivering pamphlets to passengers we went “public" with our campaign. In particular, we printed two large signs and placed them at the entrance of the bus terminal. The signs read “Sacco's on Mfangano-Kisumu are now tracked for safety, enjoy the benefits of safer transit, and check out the Top Safety Performer" (see Figure 2). Everything else about the experiment remained the same, and the timing of the treatment was chosen so as to not coincide with other events that could have affected demand or supply. The signs alerted SACCOs that this information was being delivered publicly to their customer base, which could affect their reputation and ultimately their demand. Before the sign was revealed, we made sure to visit each participating firm and inform them that we would begin to advertise the safety system at the bus station, and explain that passengers would be informed about the Top Safety Performer.

Subsidy. We also introduced a sub-experiment where passengers were cross randomized into an additional “subsidy treatment". This helps to assign monetary valuations to the behavior changes we observe in response to the pamphlets, which we also use for model estimation purposes. We selected half of the respondents to receive a 100 KES ($1 USD) subsidy should they purchase a ticket from the company that was awarded the Top Safety performer rating. The other half of the respondents did not receive any additional incentives beyond the 50 KES that they were awarded for completing our surveys. When the subsidies were provided to passengers in the control and salience group, enumerators were careful not to tell passengers why this particular bus was being subsidized.

Data and Descriptive Statistics

Data. We use data from the tracking device to track changes in driving behavior over time by each company. Moreover, we administered a series of passenger surveys to capture changes in passenger behavior. We first administered a baseline survey to passengers before handing them their assigned pamphlet. This survey collected information about their demographics, their experience riding matatus on this particular route; their preferences for various matatu characteristics including speed, comfort, safety, and style; and their beliefs about which of the five SACCOS was the best along each of these dimensions.

Experimental Results

We run the following regressions as our preferred specification to understand the impact of our treatments on passenger bus choices.

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} D_i & =\alpha+\beta_{1}\text{Salience}_{i}+\beta_{2}\text{SafetyLeader}_{i}\\ & \quad+\beta_{3}\text{Salience}_{i}\times\text{Public}_{t(i)}+\beta_{4}\text{SafetyLeader}\times\text{Public}_{t(i)}+\mathbf{X}_{i}'\gamma+\varepsilon_{i} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

where $D_{i}$ is an indicator equal to 1 if passenger $i$ selected the Top Performing bus company; $\mathbf{X}_i$ are controls for individual characteristics; and $\varepsilon_{i}$ is an error term. Specifically, $\mathbf{X}_i$ contains the day of week by time of day when the interview took place (e.g. Monday morning or Wednesday evening), gender, age, education, and travel frequency of the respondent. The treatment indicators are $\text{Salience}_{i}$ for our safety salience treatment; and $\text{SafetyLeader}_{i}$ for those passengers randomized into learning about the Top Safety Performer. Last, $\text{Public}_{t(i)}$ is an indicator equal to one if the interview was conducted on a day after the public signal was activated. To test robustness, we also run specifications without interview timing controls and passenger characteristic controls.

Next, to estimate the effect of the public treatment on firm safety performance, we run the following regression:

\begin{align} Y_{m,t} = \alpha_m + \beta \text{Public}_{t} + \varepsilon_{m,t} \end{align}

where $Y_{m,t}$ is bus $m$ on day $t$ safety outcome, $\alpha_m$ is a bus fixed effect, and $\text{Public}_{t}$ is an indicator that takes the value of one if the public signal has been launched. We cluster standard errors at the bus level.

Effects of Public Signal

Demand side.

After several months of delivering these pamphlets to passengers, we went “public" with the safety campaign. We placed two large, visible signs at the entrance of the bus terminal indicating that SACCOS on the route were being monitored for safety (see Figure 2). Rows 3 and 4 of Table 2 shows how the effect of our treatments in the post-public period (days where the public signs were on display) change from the private information period. In all specifications, we see that the public signal increased the impact of the safety leader arm meaningfully. In our preferred specification in Column 4, passengers that know who the top safety performer is are 10.9 percentage points (nearly 100 percent) more likely to choose that company. This represents an important shift from passengers' reactions to the same information when the signs were not displayed on the street. This result suggests that the signs themselves are changing the way passengers interpret the information we provide about the Top Safety Performer in the pamphlet.

Supply side.

The results indicate that passengers find the safety information in the pamphlet more informative once the campaign is public. This may be because passengers now expect bus companies to compete on safety, as it has become an observable characteristic. Similarly, companies may find it more enticing to improve their safety now that they realize customers know they are being tracked. We investigate whether companies improve their safety after the campaign goes public. Table 3 reports the estimates of equation ? on the three safety measures we track as well as the overall safety index. We see that all measures show meaningful and statistically significant improvements in safety in the public period. Starting with column 1, average speed fell by 1.33 kilometers per hour, which is a 3 percent reduction. Likewise, column 2 and 3 show that the daily number of high speeding and sharp braking events were reduced by 0.12 (25% decrease) and 0.01 (33%) respectively. Combining all three metrics into a safety index, column 4 shows that the average safety metric improved by 0.164 standard deviations. Figure 4 plots these safety improvements visually. All four plots show a visually detectable improvement from the pre-period to the public period, however the improvement in braking is more gradual than the other metrics.

Possible Mechanisms for Response to Public Signal

Market equilibrium. Our preferred explanation for the demand and supply side response to the public signal is that it changes the market equilibrium by making safety certification demand-relevant. We provide a full explanation in the model section (Section 5 and Appendix 12). In the fitted structural model, each firm faces a firm- and market-specific demand prize from winning certification and chooses effort in response to that prize. Passengers understand that the public signal changes firms' incentives, so the same safety certification becomes more informative about current service quality once the campaign is public. Before this, passengers likely did not believe that firms were actively working to improve safety, so they discounted the safety signal.

There are other possible interpretations for the demand and supply response to the public signal. Here, we present these alternatives and explain why we believe they cannot account for the behavior changes we observe in the data.

Credibility. The banners might have increased the credibility of the safety leader treatment, but we believe this explanation is unlikely for a few reasons. First, we provided the pamphlet information in a credible manner: enumerators dressed professionally, displayed laminated ID badges with photos, and explained that the project was approved by the National Transport Safety Authority and funded by reputable international universities. Second, 30% of passengers switched to the safest bus when offered a subsidy (before the public signal), showing they trusted our promise to provide cash after purchasing the ticket. If they trusted us to pay the subsidy, it's likely they also trusted the information about the safest bus.

Social signaling. Passengers may have chosen the safety-certified bus to show others that they cared about safety once this attribute was being advertised publicly. However, we believe this behavior is unlikely in the context of our experiment. Other passengers do not observe which treatment was provided to the respondent, so they cannot know if someone received the safety information. Additionally, since the majority of passengers did not receive the safety leader information, there is no way to publicly signal that one is making the safe choice. Finally, even if passengers could signal to each other, we would expect these pressures to be weaker in a context where almost everyone is a stranger and does not have repeated interactions. Moreover, it is not clear that a preference for safety over other attributes, such as speed or comfort, would be considered a socially preferred choice.

Interviewer demand. Passengers might have also felt more pressure to “please" the enumerator by choosing the safe bus company under the public signal. While this is possible, we would expect this behavior to be reflected in the number of people reporting bus safety as the most important attribute when choosing a bus at baseline. However, our checks show no change in the proportion of passengers expressing safety as their primary concern once the public campaign is launched. Passenger salience. The unveiling of the safety banners might have made passengers and firms value safety more. If salience influenced passenger choice, we would expect passengers who received the safety salience pamphlet (before the public campaign) to have chosen the bus company they believed was the safest, but we did not observe this behavior. Similarly, the tracking data we provided to firms highlighted the available safety information for managers and we do not see changes in their behavior before the campaign went public (see Appendix Figure A1). Therefore, it is unlikely that the public banners significantly increased the salience of safety for passengers or firms.

Coordination. While the safety banner may have helped passengers coordinate on choosing the winning firm, the demand for each firm is hard for any single passenger to observe, as the ticket purchase locations are out of view, making it difficult to know which firm an individual chose after the transaction. Moreover, if coordination increased due to the public signal, we would expect the serial correlation of passenger ticket purchases to increase during the public period. We tested this by regressing passenger bus choice on the choices of previous passengers that day in the public period. We found no evidence of autocorrelation, with a point estimate of -0.026 (t = -0.52) on the lagged choice of bus.

Information Effects and Market Structure

We now present a structural model of competition among the firms in our setting. The model is designed to do three things. First, it provides an economic interpretation of why the public signal changed passenger responses to the same private safety information. Second, it links the demand reward from certification to the supply-side incentive to improve safety. Third, it provides the objects needed for welfare analysis and policy counterfactuals.

Model Setup

There are $J$ firms competing for passenger demand. Firm $j$ begins with a baseline safety level $\alpha_j$ and marginal effort cost $c_j$, drawn from a joint distribution $F_{\alpha,c}$. After observing its type, the firm chooses a continuous safety effort $\mu_j \ge 0$. Realized safety on a given day is

\begin{equation} q_j = \alpha_j + \mu_j + \xi_j, \end{equation}

where $\xi_j$ is an idiosyncratic day-level shock. Equation 2 separates persistent safety differences across firms from strategic effort and residual noise.

Passengers choose among firms after observing the experimentally assigned information. In the background demand system, passenger $i$'s utility from firm $j$ in market state $x_t$ is

\begin{equation} U_{ijt} = \delta_j(x_t) + \mathbb{E}[q_j \mid \mathcal{I}_{it}] + \varepsilon_{ijt}, \end{equation}

where $\delta_j(x_t)$ collects firm- and market-specific demand shifters such as brand appeal, availability, or wait time; $\mathcal{I}_{it}$ is the information available to passenger $i$ at the time of choice; and $\varepsilon_{ijt}$ is an idiosyncratic taste shock. The key experimental distinction is between the private regime, in which passengers see the winner label but firms do not internalize any demand consequence from it, and the public regime, in which both sides understand that certification can shift demand.

This difference matters because the reward from winning is no longer a single common scalar. Instead, the estimated demand system implies a firm- and market-specific certification prize,

\begin{equation} \Delta D_j(x_t) = s_j^1(x_t) - s_j^0(x_t), \end{equation}

where $s_j^1(x_t)$ is the predicted demand share when firm $j$ is certified and $s_j^0(x_t)$ is the predicted share absent certification. Certification can therefore be more valuable for some firms than for others, even in the same market, because brand position and choice-set composition shift how much demand is at stake.

Equilibrium without Public Signal

The no-public benchmark remains simple. When firms do not believe passengers can observe and respond to safety certification, they do not internalize a certification prize and therefore have no strategic reason to adjust effort. Under that benchmark, the winner label primarily updates passengers about baseline safety rather than about endogenous effort.

Equilibria with Public Signal

Once the campaign becomes public, firms understand that winning the safety tournament can increase demand. Let $H(q)$ denote the probability that a firm with realized safety $q$ wins certification, and let $h(q) = H'(q)$ be the corresponding slope. Conditional on a realized demand state $x_t$, firm $j$ chooses $\mu_j$ to maximize

\begin{equation} \pi_j(\mu_j; \alpha_j, c_j, x_t) = H(\alpha_j + \mu_j)\Delta D_j(x_t) - c_j \mu_j. \end{equation}

The first term is the expected demand reward from certification; the second is the real resource cost of safer service.

For an interior optimum, the first-order condition is

\begin{equation} c_j = \Delta D_j(x_t)\, h(\alpha_j + \mu_j^*). \end{equation}

Equation 6 is the core supply-side condition in the paper. A firm raises effort until its marginal cost equals the marginal increase in win probability times the demand reward from winning. Relative to the earlier binary model, the main difference is that both the prize and the desired effort target can vary across firms and market states.

This formulation also clarifies what the catch-up versus pull-away language means in this model. Let \[ m(\alpha \mid x) = \mathbb{E}[\mu_j^*(\alpha,c;x)\mid \alpha,x] \] denote average equilibrium effort among firms with baseline safety $\alpha$ in demand state $x$. If $m(\alpha \mid x)$ declines with $\alpha$, lower-baseline firms exert more effort and the equilibrium exhibits catch-up. If it rises with $\alpha$, safer firms pull further ahead. In the present model, that slope depends on two objects: how sharply the win probability changes with realized safety, and how the cost distribution shifts with baseline safety. A more positive relationship between baseline safety and marginal cost tends to favor catch-up; a sufficiently negative relationship can generate pull-away.

Model Interpretation

The reduced-form evidence fits this structural formulation well. Before the campaign becomes public, passengers do not respond to private safety information, which is consistent with the idea that the winner label conveys little beyond baseline safety. After the public campaign begins, the same safety information has a much larger demand effect, which is consistent with passengers inferring that the winner label now contains information about endogenous effort as well.

The firm-level supply response points in the same direction. In the current estimates, the average certification prize is $\widehat{\Delta D}=0.0855$ and the implied public-regime willingness to pay is $31.85$ KES per passenger. At the same time, both the reduced-form public-signal estimates and the structural mean-effort estimates in Appendix Table A5 show that the lowest-baseline firms account for the largest public-period effort responses. In other words, the empirical pattern is not that all firms shift safety by the same amount, nor that the already safest firms uniformly pull away. The fitted model instead points to a catch-up pattern in which lower-baseline firms face incentives to move toward the winner cutoff when certification becomes demand-relevant.

Model limitations. The model remains deliberately partial equilibrium. We abstract from price adjustment, changes in total market size, entry and exit, and longer-run reputation dynamics across routes. Those omissions are important for external validity, but they are not the margins that appear to drive the short-run response in this setting. The data show very little price variation across firms during the study period, so the main active margin is safety effort rather than fares. Within that scope, the model allows for heterogeneity in baseline safety, heterogeneity in marginal cost, and firm- and state-specific demand rewards from certification.

Welfare Analysis

The structural model provides a disciplined way to move from the reduced-form effects of the experiment to welfare. The central idea is straightforward. The public signal changes the demand reward from certification, firms respond by adjusting effort, passengers reallocate toward firms they expect to be safer, and those two forces jointly determine consumer gains, producer costs, and net welfare.

Calibration

The welfare calculation combines four estimated ingredients. First, the demand estimation recovers the response to certification in the public regime, including the subsidy-normalized willingness to pay for the safety signal and the firm-state prize object $\Delta D_j(x_t)$. Second, the estimated winner-gap distribution determines how realized safety translates into win probabilities. Third, the supply-side estimation recovers baseline safety, public-period effort, cost draws, and the joint distribution $F_{\alpha,c}$. Fourth, the empirical distribution of observed market states provides the environment over which the welfare objects are averaged.

Welfare Estimates

Producer-side welfare is the negative of the real resource cost of effort:

\begin{equation} \Pi = -\mathbb{E}_X \left[\iint c\,\mu^*(\alpha,c;X)\, dF_{\alpha,c}(\alpha,c)\right]. \end{equation}

Consumer surplus follows the standard log-sum expression from the demand system. If \[ v_j^{\mathcal{I}}(X) = \delta_j(X) + \mathbb{E}[q_j \mid \mathcal{I}], \] then

\begin{equation} \mathrm{CS}(\mathcal{I}) = N\gamma \, \mathbb{E}\left[ \ln\left(\sum_{j=1}^J \exp\left(v_j^{\mathcal{I}}(X)/\gamma\right)\right) \right]. \end{equation}

This expression admits a useful decomposition into three parts:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \Delta \mathrm{CS} ={}& \underbrace{\text{reallocation}} _{\text{passengers switch toward safer firms}} \\ &+ \underbrace{\text{intrinsic quality}} _{\text{firms raise safety even for non-switchers}} \\ &+ \underbrace{\text{entropy}} _{\text{changes in effective variety}}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Net welfare combines consumer surplus, producer cost, and the resource cost of running the intervention:

\begin{equation} \Delta W = \Delta \mathrm{CS} + \Pi - \tau. \end{equation}

This accounting is more useful than the earlier discrete formula because it no longer requires summarizing the entire equilibrium with a small set of binary type shares. Instead, welfare is evaluated directly under the fitted distribution of baseline safety, marginal cost, and observed demand states.

Under the observed equilibrium, the fitted model implies monthly net welfare of about $802.6$ thousand KES. The consumer-surplus gain is $1.151$ million KES per month, producer-side effort costs are about $318.8$ thousand KES, and campaign costs are $30$ thousand KES. Figure 7 breaks the consumer gain into its three components: roughly $225.6$ thousand KES comes from passenger reallocation, $1.086$ million KES comes from intrinsic quality improvements, and the entropy term is about $-159.9$ thousand KES. Most of the welfare gain in the current run therefore comes from firms supplying safer service, not just from consumers switching across existing options.

The same fitted model is also the basis for the policy counterfactuals in Section 7. Before turning to those exercises, we first summarize uncertainty around the observed-equilibrium welfare estimate.

Sensitivity Analysis

There are two distinct uncertainty exercises in the current draft. The first is statistical uncertainty around the observed-equilibrium welfare estimate. The current bootstrap yields a 95% interval of approximately $[384.3,\ 1418.4]$ thousand KES for monthly net welfare. This interval is still wide, but it remains centered on a meaningfully positive welfare effect.

The second exercise is structural sensitivity to how baseline safety and marginal cost covary in the fitted distribution. Appendix Figure A4 varies the correlation parameter $\rho_{\alpha,\log c}$ and traces out the implied mean-effort curve. More negative dependence moves the model toward pull-away, because safer firms are also relatively low cost and therefore have stronger incentives to pull further ahead. More positive dependence moves the model toward catch-up, because lower-baseline firms are relatively less disadvantaged on the cost margin and therefore respond more strongly to the public prize. In this way, the counterfactual is best read as a mechanism diagnostic for the fitted distribution of types, not as a central policy experiment.

Policing unsafe service

The second counterfactual studies a simple threshold penalty for low realized safety. The benchmark rule sets the penalty to zero above a threshold $\bar q$ and increases it linearly below that threshold. In the benchmark calibration, $\bar q$ is the mean pre-public safety level and the penalty slope is chosen so that the sanction at the reference shortfall corresponds to 20% of baseline producer earnings.

Table 5 shows that monthly consumer surplus falls by about $29.3$ thousand KES, producer effort cost rises by about $32.8$ thousand KES, and net welfare falls by about $62.1$ thousand KES. Market structure

The third counterfactual varies the number of firms allowed to operate in the market, holding total passenger demand fixed and drawing entrant types from the fitted joint distribution. Figure 9 shows a hump-shaped relationship between market structure and welfare. Simulated net welfare peaks around $J=3$ at roughly $1.043$ million KES per month, which is about $240.1$ thousand KES above the observed market. Welfare then declines as the number of firms rises; by $J=10$, monthly net welfare falls to about $393.7$ thousand KES, or $408.8$ thousand KES below the observed market.

The economic force is straightforward. Moving from five firms to a somewhat more concentrated market strengthens the demand prize from certification and therefore the incentive to improve safety. But when the market becomes too crowded, the prize is diluted across more firms and equilibrium effort falls. The market-structure counterfactual therefore reinforces the paper's broader point that information policy and competition cannot be evaluated separately.

Conclusion

In this paper, we present findings from a randomized control trial where informal transit passengers receive information about bus safety. We make two contributions. First, we show that passengers value this information only when they expect firms to provide safer services in response to a public signal that broadcasts their ability to track buses. Additionally, we demonstrate that firms use their tracking devices to supply safer transit services after the introduction of the public signal. We interpret these findings through a structural model in which firms differ in baseline safety and marginal effort costs, and in which the public signal makes certification demand-relevant.

Our second contribution is using this model to estimate the welfare effect of the information intervention under the public signal. We decompose consumer surplus into reallocation, intrinsic quality, and entropy / variety components, and combine these with producer effort costs and intervention costs to measure net welfare. The same framework then lets us evaluate counterfactual changes in passenger demand for safety, policing of unsafe service, and market structure.

What are the implications of these results for optimal policy in an informal transit network? First, demand-side information is most valuable when passengers respond strongly to safety and firms expect those responses to matter for demand. Second, supply-side policing is not automatically welfare improving: in our current local calibration, the threshold sanctions we study induce additional effort costs without generating enough passenger surplus to offset them. Third, more competition is not always better in this environment: the simulated market-structure frontier is hump-shaped because adding firms reduces the demand prize from certification and therefore weakens effort incentives. These results suggest that the relevant policy question is not only whether consumers are informed, but also how information, regulation, and market structure interact to shape firms' incentives to supply safety.

There are a number of important open questions for future research. The effects we demonstrate in this paper are short-run and confined to a local equilibrium. In particular, our welfare calculations hold total passenger volume fixed, so they likely understate total gains if safer transit attracts riders from outside the market. It would be valuable to extend the analysis to general-equilibrium effects across the wider transport network, including demand expansion, entry of safer firms, and exit of low-quality firms.

Structural Model

This appendix develops a structural model of competition among matatu firms after the safety-tracking intervention. Firms compete to be recognized as the safest operator on a route, passengers observe that certification with different degrees of credibility depending on the information regime, and firms can respond by changing safety effort. The model is designed to organize those strategic responses in a way that maps cleanly to the experiment while allowing passengers to distinguish firms on dimensions other than safety certification alone.

The main purpose of the model is to connect three objects: how much passengers value safety information, how costly it is for firms to improve realized safety, and what those two forces imply for welfare. The demand prize from winning is not forced to be a single scalar $\Delta D$. Instead, certification can be more valuable for some firms than for others because brand appeal, passenger beliefs, timing, or choice-set composition can shift how much extra demand a winner captures.

Environment

Each firm $j\in\{1,\dots,J\}$ draws baseline safety $\alpha_j\in\mathcal A=[\underline\alpha,\overline\alpha]$ and marginal cost $c_j\in[0,\bar c]$ from joint distribution $F_{\alpha,c}$ with density $f_{\alpha,c}$ and correlation parameter $\rho$. After observing $(\alpha_j,c_j)$, the firm chooses continuous safety effort $\mu_j\in[0,\bar\mu]$.

The realized safety index that determines the tournament outcome is \[ q_j=\alpha_j+\mu_j+\xi_j,\qquad \xi_j\sim G_{\xi}, \] where $q_j$ is firm $j$'s quality on the relevant day, $G_{\xi}$ is the cdf of the idiosyncratic shock, and $g_{\xi}$ is the associated density. The additive structure separates persistent safety differences ($\alpha_j$), strategic effort ($\mu_j$), and residual day-level noise ($\xi_j$).

Passengers may also observe firm- or state-specific demand shifters $X_j$, such as brand perceptions, scheduling convenience, or other alternative-specific attributes. Let $\delta_j(X_j)$ denote the associated mean-utility shifter. Passenger $i$ observes information set $\mathcal I_i$ and draws an i.i.d. taste shock $\varepsilon_{ij}\sim\mathrm{EV}(0,\gamma)$ for each firm. The passenger chooses the bus that maximizes \[ U_{ij}=v_{ij}(X_j)+\varepsilon_{ij},\qquad v_{ij}(X_j):=\delta_j(X_j)+\mathbb E[q_j\mid\mathcal I_i]. \] The scale parameter $\gamma$ governs how strongly passengers respond to perceived quality differences. Because the content of $\mathcal I_i$ changes across regimes, passenger beliefs about expected quality can also change across regimes. We consider four experimentally relevant information sets:

\begin{align*} \mathcal I_i^{\text{priv},0} &= \{X_j\}_{j=1}^J, \\ \mathcal I_i^{\text{priv},1} &= \{X_j\}_{j=1}^J \cup \{w_t\}, \\ \mathcal I_i^{\text{pub},0} &= \{X_j\}_{j=1}^J \cup \{\text{public campaign active}\}, \\ \mathcal I_i^{\text{pub},1} &= \{X_j\}_{j=1}^J \cup \{w_t,\text{ public campaign active}\}, \end{align*}

where $w_t$ denotes the certified winner in market state $t$. Let $X$ denote the random market-state vector and $x$ a realized market state.

Under $\mathcal I_i^{\text{priv},0}$, passengers can condition only on baseline demand shifters and prior beliefs about firm safety. Under $\mathcal I_i^{\text{priv},1}$, they additionally observe which firm won the safety tournament, but they understand that firms did not choose effort in response to passenger information, so the winner label mainly updates beliefs about fixed safety differences. Under $\mathcal I_i^{\text{pub},0}$, passengers know the campaign is active and therefore know that firms may have strategic incentives to improve safety, but they do not observe the winner label for that specific choice occasion. Under $\mathcal I_i^{\text{pub},1}$, passengers both know the campaign is active and observe the certified winner. That distinction is empirically important because the demand prize $\Delta D_j(x)$ is identified from the difference between public-period demand with and without the winner label, holding the realized market state fixed.

Firm Optimization in the Public-Signal Regime

Fix a firm with baseline safety $\alpha$ and effort $\mu$. Its probability of being certified as the winner is \[ p(\alpha,\mu)=\Pr\!\bigl[q_j\ge\max_{k\neq j}q_k\mid \alpha_j=\alpha,\mu_j=\mu\bigr]=H(\alpha+\mu). \tag{A.1} \] Here $H$ is the winner-probability function obtained after integrating over opponents' types and shocks, and $h:=H'$ is its slope. Only the sum $\alpha+\mu$ enters because the tournament rewards realized safety, not whether safety comes from a stronger baseline or from additional effort.

Let $\Delta D_j(x)$ denote the incremental demand captured by firm $j$ when it is certified, conditional on the realized market state $x$, as defined in Subsection ?. Normalizing the profit of not winning to zero, a firm with marginal cost $c$ chooses $\mu$ to maximize \[ \pi_j(\alpha,c;\mu,x)=p(\alpha,\mu)\,\Delta D_j(x)-c\mu. \] The first term is the expected demand prize from certification; the second is the resource cost of producing safer service. Relative to the discrete model, the key difference is that the reward from winning can now depend on who the firm is and which rivals are present.

FOC and inverse supply. For an interior optimum $\mu_j^*(\alpha,c;x)\in(0,\bar\mu)$,

\begin{equation} c=\Delta D_j(x)\,h\!\bigl(\alpha+\mu_j^*(\alpha,c;x)\bigr). \end{equation}

Equation 11 is the marginal-benefit-equals-marginal-cost condition. An extra unit of effort is valuable only insofar as it raises the probability of winning, and that marginal win probability is exactly $h(\alpha+\mu)$. The difference in this version is that the marginal return is scaled by a firm- and market-specific prize $\Delta D_j(x)$ rather than by a common prize.

When $h$ is strictly monotone on the relevant support, the FOC can be inverted as \[ \mu_j^*(\alpha,c;x)=\psi_j(c;x)-\alpha,\qquad \psi_j(c;x):=h^{-1}\!\bigl(c/\Delta D_j(x)\bigr),\qquad \partial\psi_j(c;x)/\partial c<0. \] In the implemented logistic specification, however, \[ h(q)=\sigma^{-1}\frac{e^{-q/\sigma}}{(1+e^{-q/\sigma})^2} \] is symmetric and single-peaked at $q=0$, so the inverse is not globally single-valued. Let $r:=c/\Delta D_j(x)$. If $r\ge 1/(4\sigma)$, the FOC has no interior solution because the required marginal return exceeds the peak of $h$. If $00$ for $q<0$ and $h'(q)<0$ for $q>0$. Therefore the lower root $q_j^-(c;x)$ is a local minimum of profits, while the upper root $q_j^+(c;x)$ is a local maximum. The economically relevant interior policy rule is therefore the upper branch, \[ \boxed{\mu_j^*(\alpha,c;x)=q_j^+(c;x)-\alpha,} \tag{A.3} \] provided this choice is feasible and yields higher profits than the zero-effort corner $\mu=0$. This is the branch-selection logic used in the Matlab implementation.

Aggregate Effort

Average effort among firms with baseline safety $\alpha$, conditional on demand state $x$, is \[ m(\alpha\mid x)=\mathbb E[\mu_j^*(\alpha,c;x)\mid\alpha,x] =\mathbb E[\psi_j(c;x)\mid\alpha,x]-\alpha. \tag{A.4} \] The term $\mathbb E[\psi_j(c;x)\mid\alpha,x]$ captures how the cost distribution shifts with baseline safety once we condition on the demand environment. The subtraction of $\alpha$ captures the mechanical fact that firms that start safer need less extra effort to reach the same realized quality target.

prop[Catch-up versus pull-away] Under Assumption ?, \[ \frac{\partial m(\alpha\mid x)}{\partial\alpha} =\frac{\partial T(\alpha\mid x)}{\partial\alpha}-1, \qquad T(\alpha\mid x):=\mathbb E[\psi_j(c;x)\mid\alpha,x]. \] If $\partial T(\alpha\mid x)/\partial\alpha<1$ ($>1$) for all $\alpha$, the equilibrium exhibits catch-up (pull-away), conditional on $X$. Sufficient primitive conditions are:

  • [(C-1)] $c\!\perp\!\alpha$ or $\rho>0$ $\Rightarrow$ catch-up;
  • [(P-1)] a sufficiently large left shift of $F_{c\mid\alpha,X}$ for higher baseline safety $\Rightarrow$ pull-away.

prop

Economically, catch-up means initially less safe firms exert more effort because they have the most to gain from moving toward the winner cutoff. Pull-away means initially safer firms also tend to be sufficiently low-cost that they widen the gap once incentives become public. Allowing $\Delta D_j(x)$ to vary adds a second channel for heterogeneity: even two firms with the same cost draw may exert different effort if winning is more valuable for one of them in the realized demand environment.

Demand Premium

Let $V_{j1}(X)$ denote firm $j$'s systematic utility when it is certified and $V_{j0}(X)$ its systematic utility when it is not certified. These objects bundle together baseline demand shifters $\delta_j(X_j)$ and the expected quality passengers assign to firm $j$ under the relevant information regime. With type-I extreme-value tastes, firm $j$'s demand share when it is certified is \[ s_j^1(X)= \frac{\exp\!\bigl(V_{j1}(X)/\gamma\bigr)} {\exp\!\bigl(V_{j1}(X)/\gamma\bigr)+ \sum_{k\neq j}\exp\!\bigl(V_{k0}(X)/\gamma\bigr)}. \] Its share absent certification is \[ s_j^0(X)= \frac{\exp\!\bigl(V_{j0}(X)/\gamma\bigr)} {\sum_{k=1}^J \exp\!\bigl(V_{k0}(X)/\gamma\bigr)}. \] The firm-specific demand prize from certification is therefore \[ \Delta D_j(x)=s_j^1(x)-s_j^0(x). \tag{B.1} \] This object is the market reward for winning the certification tournament in a particular demand environment. It is larger when passengers perceive a wider quality gap between the winner and the field, when the winner's baseline demand position is stronger, or when $\gamma$ is small so that passengers are more responsive to quality information.

Welfare

Producer-side resource cost. Because certification primarily reallocates riders across firms, the producer-side welfare term comes from the real resource cost of effort:

\begin{equation} \Pi=-\mathbb E_X\!\left[\iint c\,\mu_j^*(\alpha,c;X)\; dF_{\alpha,c}(\alpha,c)\right]. \end{equation}

The negative sign reflects that higher effort improves safety but uses real resources. The outer expectation reminds us that producer costs are averaged over the empirical distribution of market states $X$.

Consumer surplus. Let $N$ denote the number of passenger decisions in the market and let $v_j^{\mathcal I}(X):=\delta_j(X_j)+\mathbb E[q_j\mid\mathcal I]$ denote the perceived utility index of firm $j$ under information regime $\mathcal I$ and state $X$. Up to the additive constant $N\gamma\gamma_E$ (where $\gamma_E$ is Euler's constant, which cancels in regime comparisons), expected ex-ante consumer surplus is

\begin{equation} \text{CS}(\mathcal I) =N\gamma\; \mathbb E\!\Biggl[ \ln\!\Biggl( \sum_{j=1}^{J}\exp\!\bigl(v_j^{\mathcal I}(X)/\gamma\bigr) \Biggr) \Biggr]. \end{equation}

Decomposition. Write \[ \pi_j^{\mathcal I}(X) =\frac{\exp(v_j^{\mathcal I}(X)/\gamma)} {\sum_{k=1}^{J}\exp(v_k^{\mathcal I}(X)/\gamma)} \] for the logit choice probability of firm $j$ under regime $\mathcal I$. Then

\begin{equation} \text{CS}(\mathcal I) =N\underbrace{\Bigl(\sum_j \pi_j^{\mathcal I}(X)\, v_j^{\mathcal I}(X)\Bigr)} _{\text{average traveled quality}} +N\gamma\underbrace{\Bigl(-\!\sum_j \pi_j^{\mathcal I}(X)\ln \pi_j^{\mathcal I}(X)\Bigr)} _{\text{choice entropy}}. \end{equation}

The first term captures expected riding quality under the equilibrium allocation of passengers. The second term is the standard logit “love-of-variety” component.

Let $\Delta x:=x^{\text{pub}}-x^{\text{base}}$ denote the change from the baseline regime to the public-signal regime. Then

\begin{align} \Delta\text{CS} &=N\sum_j\mathbb E_X\!\left[ (\pi_j^{\text{pub}}(X)-\pi_j^{\text{base}}(X))\, v_j^{\text{pub}}(X) \right] \quad\text{(reallocation / information)} \notag\\ &\quad{} +N\sum_j \mathbb E_X\!\left[ \pi_j^{\text{base}}(X)\, \bigl(v_j^{\text{pub}}(X)-v_j^{\text{base}}(X)\bigr) \right] \quad\text{(intrinsic quality gain)} \notag\\ &\quad{} +N\gamma\, \mathbb E_X\!\left[ \Delta\Bigl(-\!\sum_j\pi_j(X)\ln\pi_j(X)\Bigr) \right] \quad\text{(entropy / variety)}. \tag{B.3} \end{align}
  • Reallocation benefit: passengers who switch firms after the public signal are steered toward options they believe are safer.
  • Intrinsic quality gain: even passengers who would not have switched gain when firms raise realized safety through higher effort.
  • Entropy term: the public signal can either widen or narrow effective choice depending on how concentrated demand becomes in each market state.

Estimation Blueprint

Step 0 – Pre-processing. Construct a scalar safety index $q_{jt}$ from the observed driving measures using the Anderson inverse-covariance procedure. Collapse to firm-day means and label the daily winner on each route-day.

Step 1 – Reduced-form demand with observed heterogeneity. Estimate a conditional logit using long-form passenger-by-alternative data. A convenient specification is \[ \begin{aligned} u_{ijt} ={} & a_j+\lambda'X_{ijt} +\beta_{\text{subsidy}}\text{subsidy}_{ijt} \\ & +\beta_{t1}\bigl(\text{treat1}_t\cdot\text{certified}_{ijt}\bigr) +\beta_{t2}\bigl(\text{treat2}_t\cdot\text{certified}_{ijt}\bigr) \\ & +\beta_{pt1}\bigl(\text{public}_t\cdot\text{treat1}_t\cdot\text{certified}_{ijt}\bigr) \\ & +\beta_{pt2}\bigl(\text{public}_t\cdot\text{treat2}_t\cdot\text{certified}_{ijt}\bigr) +\varepsilon_{ijt}. \end{aligned} \] Here $a_j$ are firm-specific constants or fixed effects, and $X_{ijt}$ can include observed alternative-specific demand shifters such as availability, wait time, survey beliefs, or other convenience attributes. We normalize $\gamma=1$ for estimation. In the current Matlab implementation, the key public-signal certification effect is the total coefficient $\beta_{t2}+\beta_{pt2}$, and this is the object used when constructing the public-regime certification prize.

For each observed market state $x_t$, compute the predicted demand prize from certification as \[ \hat\Delta D_j(x_t) =\hat s_{jt}^{\,1}(x_t)-\hat s_{jt}^{\,0}(x_t). \] If $\hat\beta_{\text{subsidy}}$ is the coefficient on the 100 KES subsidy, the ratio \[ 100\cdot\frac{\hat\beta_{t2}+\hat\beta_{pt2}}{\hat\beta_{\text{subsidy}}} \] gives a money-metric valuation of certification in the public regime holding $x_t$ fixed.

Step 2 – Noise distribution $G_ $.} Let $d_t$ denote the latent difference between the top and second-best firm-day safety indices on day $t$, and let $z_t:=d_t d_t 0$ denote the observed nonnegative winner--runner-up gap. Under a logistic specification with scale $$, \[ f_{\sigma}(d)=\sigma^{-1}\frac{e^{-d/\sigma}}{(1+e^{-d/\sigma})^2}, \qquad F_{\sigma}(d)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-d/\sigma}}. \] Estimate $$ by truncated-logistic maximum likelihood using \[ \mathcal L(\sigma)=\prod_t \frac{f_{\sigma}(z_t)}{1-F_{\sigma}(0)}. \]

Step 3 – Baseline safety $\hat\alpha$. Before the public signal, firms do not strategically adjust effort, so $q_{jt}^{\text{pre}}=\alpha_j+\xi_{jt}$. In the Matlab pipeline we estimate both a firm-mean fixed-effects baseline and a random-effects REML baseline, and then carry one selected $\hat\alpha_j$ object forward to later steps. The active implementation selects the fixed-effects baseline by default.

Step 4 – Realized effort $\hat\mu$. In the public period, \[ \hat\mu_{jt}=q_{jt}^{\text{pub}}-\hat\alpha_j. \] Subtracting baseline safety from realized public-period quality converts observed safety performance into a direct measure of the firm's strategic response to the campaign.

Practical Extensions

  • Nonparametric $F_{\alpha,c}$: kernel-smooth $m(\alpha\mid X)$ and deconvolve $c$ via 11.
  • General $G_{\xi}$: replace the logistic with a sieve density estimator.
  • Equilibrium test: the sign of $\partial m(\alpha\mid X)/\partial\alpha$ (Proposition ?) distinguishes catch-up from pull-away before turning to welfare.

Outcome. The pipeline recovers the fundamental primitives, allows certification to have firm- and state-specific demand consequences through $\Delta D_j(X)$, and delivers welfare effects under information design in a version of the model that does not force a common demand prize across firms. In the current draft, the main policy takeaways come from the demand, tax, and market-structure frontiers, while the $\rho_{\alpha,\log c}$ sweep is used to illustrate how the fitted supply-side heterogeneity generates catch-up versus pull-away.
