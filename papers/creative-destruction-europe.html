Abstract

Competition between states is widely credited for Europe's long-run economic development, yet the process involves both creative and destructive forces. We provide the first empirical decomposition of how territorial competition affected city growth over the second millennium. Using new data on the universe of European state boundaries matched with city population records, we combine event study and panel decomposition methods to estimate the impact of boundary changes and recover unobserved state quality. We decompose the effect of competition into three channels: switching costs from regime change, between-state sorting as cities move to better rulers, and within-state reform as incumbents improve governance over time. Switching is costly but beneficial over the long-run. We find no evidence that cities sort systematically according to their prior trajectory. State quality explains a sizeable share of city outcomes, and its explanatory power roughly doubles when allowed to vary over time. The bulk of improvement comes from within-state reform to stave off challengers in more competitive environments, though sorting across durable states contributes as well. A structural model suggests that the European state system operated close to the growth-maximizing rate of entry of new states.

Introduction

Over the course of the second millennium, European cities changed hands thousands of times as states conquered, merged, fragmented, and dissolved. A longstanding tradition in economics and the social sciences holds that this competition between states was central to Europe's long-run economic development (North and Thomas, 1973; Jones, 1981; Hall, 1986; Tilly, 1990; Diamond, 1997; Kennedy, 2010; Acemoglu and Robinson, 2012). Yet the process of territorial competition involves both creative and destructive forces: cities may gain access to better governance when a high-quality state takes over, or when competitive pressure spurs incumbents to reform, but they also suffer disruption from conflict and regime change. How large are these opposing forces, and does the creative or destructive side of state competition dominate? Despite the prominence of this question, systematic empirical evidence has been lacking—partly because historical state boundaries are difficult to observe at scale, and partly because the quality of historical states is unobserved, particularly for the many states that were eventually pushed out of the market for governance.

In this paper, we provide the first empirical decomposition of how territorial competition affects city growth. We separate the effect into three channels: switching costs from conflict and regime change, between-state sorting as cities move from lower- to higher-quality rulers, and within-state reform as incumbents improve governance over time. To measure these channels, we combine newly available data on the universe of boundary changes of all European states over the second millennium (Reed, 2016) with city population data from (Buringh, 2021), and adapt panel decomposition methods from labor economics (Abowd et al., 1999) to recover unobserved state quality from the history of cities and their rulers.

We present four main results. First, city population losses from switching between states are large but transitory, whereas benefits from improved governance are longer-lasting. Second, unobserved state quality explains a sizeable share of the variation in city outcomes—and allowing state quality to vary over time roughly doubles its explanatory power, indicating that states reformed or deteriorated substantially across the period. Third, the bulk of state improvement over time comes from within-state reform rather than from replacement of worse states by better ones. Fourth, quality-based selection operates primarily at the entry margin: when cities change hands, higher-quality entrants are far more likely to be the new ruler than lower-quality ones.

We interpret these results through the lens of creative destruction among competing states. Section 2 develops a conceptual framework in which the entry of challenger states can improve governance but imposes costs on cities in the process. At one extreme, if there is little entry, cities may be spared the losses from conflict, but incumbent states may ossify.1 At the other extreme, frequent entry of low-quality challengers imposes high costs with little improvement in governance—destructive competition, as argued by (Cosandey, 1997).2 Between these extremes, the framework distinguishes classical creative destruction, in which better states displace worse ones, from escape competition, in which competitive pressure spurs incumbents to reform. Our evidence suggests that the European state system operated primarily in the escape-competition regime, close to the growth-maximizing rate of entry.

Event studies of city populations around boundary changes reveal that switching imposes an immediate population decline of roughly 39%, consistent with the disruption of conquest and regime change. This cost is transitory: populations recover within one census interval. By contrast, when a city switches to a different state—controlling for the cost of switching itself—the population gain is approximately 65% and persistent, remaining at a similar magnitude one period after the transition. Panel regressions that progressively add fixed effects show that state quality absorbs roughly a third of this benefit, indicating that much of the gain from a change in governance reflects the quality of the new ruler.

To measure state quality directly, we adapt the AKM decomposition from labor economics (Abowd et al., 1999), treating cities as workers and states as firms. In the baseline specification with time-invariant state effects, unobserved state quality accounts for 5% of the variance in log population and 8% of the variance in decadal growth. Allowing state effects to vary across census intervals (Lachowska et al., 2023) more than doubles these shares—to 12% and 21%, respectively—and a Wald test strongly rejects time-invariance ($p < 0.001$). States reformed and deteriorated substantially over the millennium, and ignoring this time-variation understates the role of governance in urban growth.

Decomposing changes in state quality into the three channels of the conceptual framework, we find that within-state reform is the dominant positive force: the average switching city experiences cumulative improvement from incumbent reform large enough to nearly triple its population over the course of its history. Between-state sorting is negative in aggregate, and switching costs reduce city size by a further 20%, but the net effect remains strongly positive. Durable transitions—where both the origin and destination state persist—do exhibit mildly positive sorting; the entire negative sorting effect is concentrated among transient transitions where one state quickly dissolves. Within-state reform increases with competition intensity, consistent with the escape-competition mechanism.

Finally, a conditional logit model of destination choice reveals that quality-based selection operates primarily at the entry margin. When a city changes hands, newly entering states—those appearing in the system for the first time—are disproportionately likely to be the new ruler, and this pattern is sharply quality-sensitive: higher-quality entrants are far more likely to displace incumbents than lower-quality ones. The conditional logit, however, can only study selection among observed transitions; for the majority of city-periods where no switch takes place, challenger identity and quality are unobserved. To overcome this, we estimate a structural model that parameterizes the contest between incumbents and challengers through a quality-sensitive contest success function. By modeling state quality as a latent AR(1) process, the structural model can simulate challenger quality even for non-switchers, recovering the overall quality sensitivity of contests from the full dynamics of state quality trajectories. The estimates confirm that contests are quality-biased and that the European state system operated close to the growth-maximizing rate of entry.

We provide evidence against several alternative interpretations. The event studies show no pre-trends in city population before switching events, inconsistent with cities switching systematically based on their prior growth trajectories. A leave-out test grouping cities by the magnitude of their change in state quality confirms this: cities moving to better versus worse states show no significant differential pre-trends ($p = 0.40$ for log population, $p = 0.38$ for growth). These patterns are consistent with the view that changes in territorial control were driven largely by geopolitical and military considerations rather than by city-level economic conditions (Hoffman, 2015). That said, the estimated state effects likely combine treatment and selection to some degree.

Contributions to literature.

Our work contributes to the large literature on the causes of Europe's ascent.3 Previous work has argued that competition improves governance (Tilly, 1990) or that it causes excessive destruction (Cosandey, 1997; Rosenthal and Wong, 2011), but has not separately estimated these channels. We provide the first quantitative assessment, finding that the net effect of territorial competition was positive but driven primarily by within-state reform—competitive pressure spurring incumbents to improve their governance—with a more modest contribution from the direct replacement of worse states by better ones.4 Our view of institutions is expansive, allowing for features ensuring stability and security to be relevant besides inclusivity [see also][who documents that politically autonomous cities initially grew faster but eventually stagnated as merchant guilds enacted barriers to entry]Stasavage_2014.

Our findings also add to the literature studying the long-run impact of state capacity on economic development (Bockstette et al., 2002; Dell, 2010; Michalopoulos and Papaioannou, 2013; Michalopoulos and Papaioannou, 2014; Cox, 2017; Dell et al., 2017; Dincecco et al., 2022). Much of this literature documents that exposure to higher-quality states has persistent benefits. We complement these findings by showing how competitive pressure may have produced such states in the first place, and by quantifying the costs that the competitive process itself imposes on cities.5

Finally, our work connects to the literature on state capacity and conflict (Mann, 1984; Tilly, 1990; Besley and Persson, 2010; Gennaioli and Voth, 2015). A central thesis in this literature is that external threats drive the development of state capacity, which in turn promotes economic development (Queralt, 2019). Our decomposition provides direct evidence for this mechanism: states that face territorial competition improve their governance over time, and this within-state reform is the dominant source of improvement in the quality of governance experienced by European cities.

Conceptual Framework

Setup

Consider a city whose log size is $\log Y = \alpha + \psi_j + S\beta$, where $\alpha$ is the city's baseline size and $\psi_j$ is the quality of state $j$ in control of the city. We use “quality” expansively: $\psi_j$ summarizes the full environment that a state provides for urban development, including security, property rights, fiscal capacity, and the broader institutional conditions that enable or hinder economic activity. In the empirical analysis, $\psi_j$ is identified from the association between states and city outcomes, so it captures the net effect of all state-level attributes rather than any single policy channel.

An incumbent state $j=I$ provides public goods, guarantees internal peace, and sets rules for economic exchange. A challenger state $j=C$ with quality drawn from $F$ may take over the city through conquest and expose the city to its institutions instead.6

Since our primary interest is in the effect of state competition, we do not specify the motivation of the Challenger to take over the city, which could be driven by animosity between rulers, for example over religious matters, or by the desire to rule the city. We simply assume that whether the Challenger succeeds in conquering the city is a Bernoulli random variable $S$ with mean $p(\psi_C, \psi_I)$ for a given draw of $\psi_C$, which places no restrictions on the correlation structure between the respective state qualities and the Challenger's success probability. If the Challenger succeeds in the conquest, the city suffers a proportional loss of population $\beta < 0$ due to destruction or looting in the course of the change in governance.7

Static expected city size

Given these assumptions, the expected city size is

\begin{align} E[\log Y] &= \alpha + E[\psi_j] + \bar{p}\,\beta \nonumber \\ &= \alpha + \psi_I + \left\{(E[\psi_C | S = 1] - \psi_I) + \beta \right\}\bar{p} \end{align}

where $\bar{p} \equiv P(S=1) = E_{\psi_C}[p(\psi_C,\psi_I)]$ is the marginal switch probability (integrating over the distribution of challenger quality) and $E[\psi_C | S = 1]$ is the average quality of the Challenger conditional on its victory against the Incumbent.8 Equation (?) summarizes the conditions through which state competition trades off potential increases in the quality of states against the costs of war. The first two terms, $\alpha + \psi_I$, set the baseline city size in the absence of any competition from other states (that is, when $\bar{p} = 0$).

The effect of competition is captured in two components. First, the terms in curly brackets capture net benefits (or net costs) of state competition: the potential improvement in governance quality—the difference between the quality of the victorious Challenger and the Incumbent—reduced by the cost $\beta$ of switching. The extent of positive (or negative) selection is a product both of the distribution of Challenger quality and the correlation structure of qualities with the Challenger's success probability.9 Second, the Challenger success function $p(\psi_C,\psi_I)$ scales any net benefits or costs. This function can be biased towards (or against) quality-improving states: in a strongly quality-biased environment, even small net benefits are magnified by war fortunes skewed towards high-quality Challengers.10

The static framework thus illustrates how there can be both too little or too much state competition: if potential net benefits are large but few high-quality challenges succeed, more challenges would be welfare-improving; if net benefits are small or negative, less competition would be better. However, by treating state quality $\psi_j$ as fixed, this analysis can only rationalize between-state sorting—replacement of worse states by better ones—as the channel through which competition benefits cities. We now extend the framework to allow state quality to evolve over time.

Dynamic extension: within-state reform

Suppose the incumbent's quality may evolve between periods. In period $t{-}1$, the city is governed by incumbent $I$ with quality $\psi_{I,t-1}$. In period $t$, three things happen: (i) the incumbent's quality evolves to $\psi_{I,t} = \psi_{I,t-1} + \Delta\psi_I$, where $\Delta\psi_I$ captures institutional reform, fiscal consolidation, or decay; (ii) a challenger $C$ with quality $\psi_{C,t}$ appears (drawn from $F$); and (iii) the challenger takes over the city with probability $p = p(\psi_{C,t}, \psi_{I,t})$, imposing switching cost $\beta < 0$.

The only new element relative to the static model is $\Delta\psi_I \equiv \psi_{I,t} - \psi_{I,t-1}$: within-state improvement of the incumbent. Setting $\Delta\psi_I = 0$ recovers the static framework exactly.

Taking the difference between periods $t$ and $t{-}1$, the expected change in city size decomposes into three channels:11

\begin{equation} E[\Delta \log Y] \;=\; \underbrace{\Delta\psi_I}_{\text{within-state reform}} \;+\; \underbrace{\bigl(E[\psi_{C,t} \mid S{=}1] - \psi_{I,t}\bigr) \cdot \bar{p}}_{\text{between-state sorting}} \;+\; \underbrace{\beta \cdot \bar{p}}_{\text{switching cost}} \end{equation}

The first channel, within-state reform, captures the incumbent's improvement (or deterioration) over time. This occurs regardless of whether a switch takes place, and is absent from the static model. The second channel, between-state sorting, is the quality gap between the victorious challenger and the incumbent at the moment of transition, scaled by the probability of switching. This is positive if challengers who win tend to be of higher quality than the incumbent; it is the only channel present in the static framework. The third channel, the switching cost, is the same $\beta \cdot \bar{p}$ term as before.

When is competition beneficial? From equation (1), city growth is positive when

\begin{equation} \Delta\psi_I > -\bigl(E[\psi_{C,t} \mid S{=}1] - \psi_{I,t} + \beta\bigr) \cdot \bar{p}. \end{equation}

In the static case ($\Delta\psi_I = 0$), this reduces to the requirement that the quality advantage of the victorious challenger exceeds the switching cost—competition is beneficial only if between-state sorting is sufficiently positive. In the dynamic case ($\Delta\psi_I > 0$), competition can be beneficial even when between-state sorting is negative, as long as within-state reform is large enough to compensate. A state system with frequent, quality-destroying switches can still be growth-enhancing if the competitive environment drives incumbent states to reform.

Escape competition

The decomposition in equation (1) treats within-state reform as exogenous. But reform may itself be a response to competitive pressure. To formalize this, we parameterize the switching probability through a Challenger Success Function (CSF):

\begin{equation} p = \Lambda\bigl(\pi_0 + \pi_1 \cdot (\psi_{C,t} - \psi_{I,t})\bigr), \end{equation}

where $\Lambda(\cdot)$ is the logistic function. The intercept $\pi_0$ governs the baseline switching rate: when $\pi_1 = 0$, every challenger has the same probability $\Lambda(\pi_0)$ of displacing the incumbent regardless of quality. The slope $\pi_1$ governs how sensitive the contest outcome is to the quality gap.

When $\pi_1 > 0$, an incumbent that improves its governance (raising $\psi_{I,t}$) directly reduces its displacement probability: the argument of $\Lambda$ falls by $\pi_1 \cdot \Delta\psi_I$, lowering $p$. Reform is therefore not only about governing better but about survival. This is the state-competition analog of the “escape competition” mechanism in the industrial organization literature [in the spirit of][]Aghion_etal_2005: the threat of displacement drives incumbent improvement. The larger $\pi_1$, the more reformed governance reduces the risk of displacement, and the stronger the incentive to reform. If $\pi_1 = 0$, contests are random, reform offers no protection against displacement, and the escape-competition channel shuts down entirely. Hence $\pi_1 > 0$ is a necessary condition for escape competition to operate.12

The three-channel decomposition and the CSF generate two testable predictions. First, if escape competition operates, within-state reform should be increasing in the intensity of competition (measured by the number of switches a city experiences). Second, escape competition requires quality-biased contests ($\pi_1 > 0$), a parameter whose identification requires modeling the full contest process, since challenger quality is unobserved for non-switchers (see Section 7).

State competition as creative destruction

Our framework gives rise to the interpretation of state competition over territory as a form of creative destruction. In the standard Schumpeterian model [see][for an overview]Aghion_etal_2012, the average quality of firms improves through three margins: reallocation of market share to better entrants, exit of worse incumbents, and innovation by existing firms. All three forces have analogs in competition between states, but the dynamic extension adds a distinction within the third margin: within-state reform may be merely incidental, or it may respond to competitive pressure, as in the escape competition framework of (Aghion et al., 2005). If it does, then competition improves governance not only by replacing worse states with better ones but also by spurring incumbents to reform.

The framework admits qualitatively distinct regimes of state competition, depending on the magnitudes of the three channels in equation (1) and the parameters of the CSF. Under classical creative destruction, between-state sorting is the dominant positive force: higher-quality challengers displace lower-quality incumbents, and the quality gains outweigh switching costs. This is the only regime the static model can rationalize. Under escape competition, within-state reform is the primary engine of improvement: incumbents facing competitive pressure improve their governance, and these gains may compensate even if sorting at the moment of transition is negative. Finally, under destructive competition, switching costs and negative sorting dominate, reform is insufficient to compensate, and competition is on net harmful to cities.

Which regime obtains depends on the magnitudes of the three channels in equation (1)—within-state reform $\Delta\psi_I$, the sorting gap $E[\psi_{C,t} \mid S{=}1] - \psi_{I,t}$, and the switching cost $\beta$—together with the quality sensitivity of contests $\pi_1$. If sorting is strongly positive and switching costs are small, classical creative destruction may suffice to make competition beneficial. If within-state reform is large, it may compensate even when sorting is negative. And $\pi_1$ determines whether the escape-competition channel operates at all: when $\pi_1 > 0$, reform offers a survival advantage that incentivizes further improvement; when $\pi_1 = 0$, contests are random and escape competition shuts down. The framework is deliberately agnostic about the balance of these forces.

The empirical analysis that follows is organized to measure each of these objects in turn. We begin with event studies of city populations around boundary changes to establish that switching has real consequences for cities and to characterize the nature of those consequences—separating short-run costs from longer-run benefits (Section 4). We then estimate state effects in a panel decomposition, providing a first measure of the sorting gap: the difference in estimated quality between the states a city leaves and the states it joins (Section 6). By allowing state effects to vary over time, we can separate within-state reform from between-state sorting, directly measuring the first two channels in equation (1). Finally, we estimate the contest technology structurally to assess whether $\pi_1 > 0$, exploiting the dynamics of state quality trajectories to overcome the missing-data problem that arises when quality gaps are unobserved for non-switchers (Section 7).

Data and Historical Background

We combine two data sources: the Centennia Historical Atlas (henceforth: Atlas) by (Reed, 2016) and city population data by (Buringh, 2021). The former dataset is newly available and marks a significant improvement in the quality of European state boundary data, as detailed below. The latter dataset provides a comprehensive panel of European city populations at century intervals from 1000 to 1500 and half-century intervals from 1500 to 2000, covering over 2,000 cities.1314

The Centennia Historical Atlas

The Atlas consists of state boundary data covering Europe, Western Asia, the Middle East and North Africa, although most changes take place in Europe. Instead of a single static map, it provides ten sets of boundaries each year for every year between AD 1000 and AD 2003, resulting in a total of 10,030 maps (roughly 5.2 weeks per map). The goal of the Atlas is to depict de facto territorial control by states, as opposed to claims to territory. The underlying data were assembled from the consensus of the historical cartography community, with discretion where the consensus reflected claims rather than actual control and a consistent judgement across regions and time aimed at depicting “boots-on-the-ground” power.15

Defining the state.

A state is defined as the entity holding the best claim of de facto power over a given territory—the entity most likely to have the capacity to extract taxes, set rules, and hold the monopoly of violence. In practice, the line between sovereignty and subordination is imprecise: many entities, particularly within the Holy Roman Empire, exercised meaningful governance authority while remaining nominally subject to a higher sovereign. The Atlas resolves such ambiguities by identifying which entity holds the strongest overall claim to de facto control at a given time and place.

States have continuity over time as long as they neither cease to exist nor absorb territory of sufficient size and independence that the old identity becomes too restrictive; for example, France continues to exist after absorbing Burgundy and Brittany. Likewise, England continues to be essentially the same state after the union with Scotland and refashioning itself as the United Kingdom, whereas Germany constituted a genuinely new supra-territorial entity despite its dominance by Prussia. The de facto criterion also applies in reverse: when a supra-national entity's authority degrades, its constituent territories become the relevant states. The Holy Roman Empire is the key example: by the mid-sixteenth century, many of its constituent territories—Bavaria, Bohemia, Saxony, Switzerland, and others—had achieved de facto independence, a process the Atlas recognizes by progressively reclassifying cities from the Empire to specific governing territories (see Appendix ? for the full set of cleaning steps). Formal sovereignty followed later, codified at the Peace of Westphalia in 1648. Cities switching between states thus encompass a range of historical mechanisms—military conquest, dynastic succession, negotiated settlement, and popular revolt—all coded based on which entity holds de facto control. We incorporate data from (Brecke, 1999) to distinguish between wartime and peacetime switches.16

State boundaries have been coded in great detail, sometimes carving out individual cities with special status from larger bodies. Figure 2 illustrates the matched data through two city histories that foreshadow the two effects we estimate formally below. Prague is governed by Bohemia through the medieval period, then passes to the Luxembourgs and subsequently to Austria and the Habsburg dynasty. During the Thirty Years War, Swedish forces briefly occupy the city, and Prague's population declines from roughly 60,000 in 1600 to 54,000 by 1650—a disruption associated with switching itself rather than a permanent change in the governing state. These two examples illustrate a cost of switching itself, and a benefit from switching to a higher-quality state.

While survival is highly variable—some states exist all the way to 2000, others last only years or decades—the rate of entry is surprisingly constant, perturbed by occasional lulls and bursts. Event Study Analysis of Switching Cities

With this background, we investigate how city populations respond to switching, with two goals: first, to demonstrate that switching imposes systematic costs and benefits amenable to causal interpretation; and second, to validate the identification assumptions underlying the decomposition in Section 6. Let $\mathbf{J}(i,t)$ denote the state governing city $i$ at census date $t$, and let $S_{it}$ count the number of switches per decade in the interval from $t-1$ to $t$.

Cost of Switching

To trace out the dynamics of city populations around switching, we estimate a (Sun and Abraham, 2021) interaction-weighted event study:

\begin{equation} y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \psi_{\mathbf{J}(i,t)} + \sum_{s\in \{-4,\ldots,-2,\,0,1,2\}} \mathbf{1}[t = e_i ^{\text{Switch}} + s]\lambda_{s} + \varepsilon_{it} \end{equation}

where $y_{it}$ is log population, $\alpha_i$ a city fixed effect, $\gamma_t$ a period effect, $\psi_{\mathbf{J}(i,t)}$ a state effect, and $e_{i}^{\text{Switch}}$ the first period in which city $i$ experienced switching ($S_{it} > 0$). The reference period is $s = -1$. By controlling for state quality on both sides of the switch, the $\lambda_s$ coefficients isolate the costs of switching itself—war, occupation, administrative upheaval—separately from any difference between the old and new state.

The left panel of Figure 5 shows results for cities experiencing up to three switching periods. Pre-trend coefficients ($\hat\lambda_{-4}$ through $\hat\lambda_{-2}$) are close to zero, supporting the assumption that switching cities were not on divergent trajectories beforehand. At the time of switching, cities experience an immediate and substantial population loss of approximately 39% ($\hat\lambda_0 = -0.50$, $p < 0.01$). This effect is transitory: by one period later, population has recovered to pre-switch levels, consistent with the finding of (Davis and Weinstein, 2002) that long-run city size is robust to large temporary shocks.

Benefit of Switching to Another State

To estimate the benefit of switching when it brings a different state to power, we estimate:

\begin{equation} y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \mathbf{1}[S_{it} > 0]\beta + \sum_{s\in \{-4,\ldots,-2,\,0,1,2\}} \mathbf{1}[t = e_i^{\text{AnotherState}} + s]\mu_{s} + \varepsilon_{it} \end{equation}

where $e_{i}^{\text{AnotherState}}$ is the first period in which $\mathbf{J}(i,t) \neq \mathbf{J}(i,t-1)$. This specification differs from equation (4) in three ways: it drops state effects $\psi_{\mathbf{J}(i,t)}$ so as to pick up quality differences between the old and new state; it controls for any switching to net out disruption costs; and the event of interest is switching to a different state rather than switching per se.

The right panel of Figure 5 shows the results. Pre-trends are again flat. Upon switching to another state, cities experience a large and sustained population gain of approximately 65% ($\hat\mu_0 = 0.50$, $p < 0.01$), which persists one period later ($\hat\mu_1 = 0.51$). The gain fades at the binned endpoint ($\hat\mu_2 = 0.09$), likely reflecting heterogeneity across later periods rather than a true reversal.

The benefits documented in these event studies may arise from at least three sources: a state quality effect, in that the new state may provide a more favorable environment for growth; a match effect, reflecting idiosyncratic affinities between cities and states; or a change-in-governance effect, whereby the transition itself breaks up political rents tied to the old regime. We now quantify these components using panel regressions.

Econometric Framework

The event studies and panel regressions in Section 4 established that switching between states imposes short-run costs, while switching to a different state yields sustained population gains, a substantial share of which is absorbed by state fixed effects. However, these reduced-form estimates cannot tell us which states provide favorable environments for growth, nor can they decompose the variance of city outcomes into its city, state, and period components. To recover individual state effects and quantify their contribution, we turn to panel decomposition methods from labor economics. This section presents the model, discusses identification, evaluates threats through diagnostic tests, and introduces time-varying extensions.

The AKM Decomposition in the City-State Setting

We adapt the AKM decomposition (Abowd et al., 1999; Card et al., 2013; Kline et al., 2020) to our setting, with cities playing the role of workers and states the role of firms. Cities governed by multiple states over their history reveal state effects, just as workers switching between employers reveal firm pay premiums. The state effect $\psi_j$—the main object of interest—measures which states are associated with larger or faster-growing cities.

A key difference from the standard AKM setting is the temporal mismatch between outcomes and assignments: population is observed at census dates 50–100 years apart, yet political boundaries change sub-annually. A city may be governed by several states within a single inter-census interval, so we cannot assign a unique state to each observation as in the labor setting. Instead, we need continuous measures of exposure that reflect both the number and the timing of different rulers within an interval—since governance late in the period has a more direct effect on the census-date population stock than governance early in it.

These considerations lead to a specification with continuous exposure weights. Define the discounted share of the inter-census interval ending at $t$ that city $i$ spends under state $j$ as: \[ w_{ijt}(\delta) = \frac{\sum_{s} \delta^{t - s}\,\mathbf{1}\{J(i,s)=j\}}{\sum_{s} \delta^{t - s}}, \qquad \sum_j w_{ijt}(\delta) = 1, \] where the sums run over all calendar years $s$ in the interval and $\delta \in [0,1]$ is a geometric discount factor. When $\delta = 0$, only the end-of-period ruler matters (snapshot assignment); when $\delta = 1$, all years receive equal weight. For growth, each year's contribution enters symmetrically, so only $\delta = 1$ is economically meaningful; for log population in levels, $\delta < 1$ downweights earlier rulers. We estimate $\delta$ via profile likelihood; in our data, $\hat{\delta} = 1$ and results are robust across values of $\delta$ (Appendix Figure A8).

Our baseline specification is:

\begin{equation} y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{j=1}^{J} w_{ijt}(\delta)\,\psi_j + \mathbf{1}[S_{it} > 0]\,\beta + \mathbf{1}[\mathbf{J}(i,t) \neq \mathbf{J}(i,t-1)]\,\eta + \varepsilon_{it} \end{equation}

where $y_{it}$ is log population of city $i$ at census date $t$; $\alpha_i$ is a city fixed effect capturing permanent geographic and locational advantages; $\gamma_t$ is an interval fixed effect capturing continent-wide trends; $\psi_j$ is the state effect, the main object of interest; $\mathbf{1}[S_{it}>0]\,\beta$ controls for the cost of any boundary change during the interval, with $\beta$ expected to be negative based on the event study evidence; and $\mathbf{1}[\mathbf{J}(i,t) \neq \mathbf{J}(i,t-1)]\,\eta$ captures any benefit from ending up under a different state that is not absorbed by the additive state effects.

Identification

State effects are identified only within a connected set of cities and states linked by boundary changes. Each city that switches between two states acts as an edge in a bipartite network linking them; $\psi_j$ is identified up to a common normalization for any group of states connected through such edges. Due to the long time horizon and the frequency of territorial changes, 129 of the 346 states in our data belong to a single connected component, covering the vast majority of city-period observations. Figure 6 visualizes this network, and Table 1 shows that summary statistics of cities and states in the connected set.

The key identifying assumption is exogenous mobility: the idiosyncratic error $\varepsilon_{it}$ in equation (7) must be mean-independent of the full history of state assignments. Formally, we require $E[\varepsilon_{it} \mid \mathbf{J}_i] = 0$ for all $i, t$, where $\mathbf{J}_i = \{J(i,s)\}_{s}$ denotes city $i$'s complete assignment sequence. Crucially, this condition is not violated by the general tendency for cities to move to higher-$\psi$ states, which we document below; nor by city size or age affecting switching frequency; nor by state characteristics such as location or military capacity influencing conquest probability. What would violate the assumption is if unobserved, time-varying city-level shocks simultaneously drive both the assignment to states and the outcome. The event studies in Section 4 provide direct support: the absence of pre-trends in the periods before switches is inconsistent with such confounding.

Three setting-specific concerns deserve comment. First, the entry and exit of states does not threaten identification as long as new states are not created to serve specific cities; a minimum of 10 city-period observations per state excludes single-city entities from the identified set. Second, static assortative matching—large cities tending to be governed by powerful states—is fully compatible with identification; only dynamic sorting, in which cities on differential growth trajectories systematically attract higher- or lower-$\psi$ states, would bias the estimates.

If present, $\hat{\psi}_j$ conflates match quality with state effects. Appendix Figure A5 provides a complementary visual test: the surface of mean residuals across deciles of $\hat{\alpha}_i$ and $\hat{\psi}_j$ is approximately flat, with no systematic diagonal pattern that would indicate positive assortative matching.

If state transitions are triggered by temporary shocks—wars, plagues, famines—that independently affect city outcomes, $\hat{\psi}_j$ may capture the shock rather than the state effect. The same mover diagnostic in Appendix Figure A6 addresses this concern: if transitory shocks drove mobility, cities about to experience state transitions would show abnormal residuals before the transition occurs. The flat pre-event profiles render this unlikely. Additionally, the switching-cost control $\beta \cdot \mathbf{1}[S_{it}>0]$ absorbs any mean disruption from the transition itself.

Overall assessment. Our data—thousands of cities switching across hundreds of states—cannot match the statistical power of labor applications with millions of workers and tens of thousands of firms. The estimated state effects likely combine treatment and selection to some degree. But violations appear modest by all available tests, and the state effects remain informative as a summary measure of the institutional and economic environment that states provide to cities, akin to how firm effects in labor economics capture both causal wage premiums and sorting.

Time-Varying Extensions

The baseline model assumes that a state's effect on city outcomes is constant across the entire sample period. This may be too restrictive if institutional quality changes at discrete moments of revolution, reform, or dynastic succession. We therefore consider two extensions that allow state effects to vary over time.

TV-AKM: piecewise-constant state effects. The first extension allows $\psi_j$ to vary across census intervals while remaining constant within each:

\begin{equation} y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{j=1}^{J} w_{ijt}(\delta)\,\psi_{j,t} + \mathbf{1}[S_{it} > 0]\,\beta + \mathbf{1}[\mathbf{J}(i,t) \neq \mathbf{J}(i,t-1)]\,\eta + \varepsilon_{it} \end{equation}

The baseline model is nested as the restriction $\psi_{j,1} = \psi_{j,2} = \cdots = \psi_{j,K}$ for all $j$, testable via a Wald statistic. Identification requires the weighted city-state graph to be connected within each interval separately, a more demanding requirement than for the baseline model. As a result, 9 states that lack within-interval variation drop out of the connected set. Because many state-period cells contain few observations, we apply empirical Bayes shrinkage to the raw $\widehat{\psi}_{j,t}$ estimates following (Armstrong et al., 2022); details are in Appendix 14.

The TV-AKM retains the baseline assumptions of exogenous mobility and additive separability, but also exploits a new identification channel: stayers. In the baseline AKM, only switching cities identify state effects, because $\psi_j$ differences out for cities that remain under the same state throughout. With time-varying effects, a city that stays under state $j$ across consecutive intervals $k$ and $k+1$ identifies the change in that state's quality: first-differencing eliminates $\alpha_i$, while $\gamma_t$ is common across cities, leaving $\Delta y = (\gamma_{k+1} - \gamma_k) + (\psi_{j,k+1} - \psi_{j,k}) + \Delta\varepsilon$ [eq. 16]Lachowska_etal_2023. This is how the model identifies within-state improvement—the core quantity in the decomposition of Section ?. Concretely, the rapid growth of English cities during the early industrial revolution is attributed in part to a rising $\psi_{\text{Britain},t}$, reflecting the security environment, property rights protections, and conditions for free exchange of ideas that the British state provided—consistent with our expansive definition of state quality.21 The stayer channel is both a strength—more information, a larger identified set than rolling-window approaches—and a vulnerability, since the baseline AKM does not require it. We present both specifications throughout for this reason.

AR(1)-AKM: smooth evolution of state effects. Historical change can also be gradual: bureaucratic capacity builds slowly, fiscal systems erode over decades, and wars impart shocks that decay over time. More importantly, imposing additional structure on the evolution of state effects allows us to assess more precisely the extent to which the churn and competitive turnover among states—the central mechanism in our conceptual framework—translated into sustained improvements in the environment for urban growth. As a further extension, we model $\psi_{j,t}$ as a latent AR(1) process, $\psi_{j,t} = \rho\,\psi_{j,t-1} + u_{j,t}$, estimated via EM combined with a Kalman smoother (Shumway and Stoffer, 1982; Durbin and Koopman, 2012). This specification nests the baseline model (as $\rho \to 1$ and $\sigma_u \to 0$) and captures smooth trajectories—such as Spain's gradual decline or Prussia's late acceleration—that neither the baseline nor TV-AKM can accommodate. Results from all three specifications are compared in Section 6.

The two extensions play complementary roles. The TV-AKM provides a descriptive decomposition of realized quality trajectories into within-state reform, between-state sorting, and switching costs, following (Lachowska et al., 2023). Because it places no restrictions on how $\psi_{j,t}$ evolves, it lets the data identify which regime—classical creative destruction, escape competition, or destructive competition—obtains empirically. The AR(1)-AKM, by contrast, is a structural estimator that imposes smooth dynamics via the Kalman smoother. This additional structure enables two things TV-AKM cannot provide: first, it estimates $\psi_{j,t}$ even for states that have no cities in a given period—which is essential for constructing the counterfactual “what if this city had remained under its incumbent?”—and second, it recovers the contest technology parameter $\pi_1$ by simulating the contest process for all city-periods—not only those where switching occurred—thereby exploiting information from non-switchers that the conditional logit of Section ? cannot use (Section 7).

Creative Destruction Decomposition

The three-channel decomposition from Section ?—within-state reform, between-state sorting, and switching costs—can now be measured empirically using the time-varying state effects from the TV-AKM specification. For each city-period transition, we decompose the total change in state quality into a within-state component (how much the incumbent state improved between periods) and a between-state component (the quality gap between the new and old state at the moment of transition), with the switching cost estimated from the first stage. Summing across all switch events in each city's history and averaging across switching cities yields the aggregate decomposition in Figure 8(a).

Within-state reform is by far the dominant positive channel: cumulating the within-state component at each transition, the average switching city experiences a total within-state improvement of $0.52$ log population. On aggregate, between-state sorting is negative ($-0.33$), and switching costs account for $-0.12$, yielding a net effect of $0.08$: states improve from within more than enough to compensate for the costs of transitions.

However, the aggregate between-state component masks important heterogeneity. We classify each transition as durable—both the origin and destination state can be tracked at least one census interval after the switch—or transient, meaning at least one of the two states cannot be tracked beyond the moment of transition, typically because it dissolved or was absorbed shortly afterward. Of 3,033 switch events with identified cross-terms, 1,901 (63%) are durable and 1,132 (37%) are transient. As Figure 8(a) shows, the entire negative between-state effect is concentrated in transient transitions ($-0.37$), while durable transitions exhibit mildly positive sorting ($0.04$).

The distinction matters for interpretation. Transient transitions involve states that hold a city briefly before dissolving—border oscillations, brief conquests, or fragments of collapsing polities. In these episodes, $\psi_C$ is poorly defined: the challenger barely exists as a going concern, and the low estimated state effect reflects the absence of governance capacity rather than a meaningful quality comparison. Such episodes are better understood as extended disruptions—conceptually closer to the switching cost $\beta$ in equation (1) than to the sorting channel. For durable transitions, by contrast, both origin and destination persist long enough for their quality to be tracked, and the mildly positive between-state gap indicates that cities tend to sort toward states of equal or higher quality when the transition leads to a lasting change in governance.

This decomposition reveals that the European state system exhibits elements of both regimes from Section ?. Escape competition is the dominant channel: within-state reform is large and positive across all levels of competition intensity, and increases significantly with the number of switches (Figure 8(b); slope $= 0.056$, SE $= 0.015$), substantially exceeding the costs of transitions. But classical creative destruction also operates at the margin: among durable transitions, higher-quality challengers do displace incumbents, consistent with the quality-based selection documented in Section ?. The negative aggregate between-state effect reflects not the absence of beneficial sorting, but the high cost of political instability—the many transient transitions that impose disruption without delivering lasting governance improvements. We return to the structural implications of this finding in Section 7.

Structural Model of State Competition

Model and Estimation

This section estimates a structural model that simulates the contest process for all city-periods, recovering the quality sensitivity parameter $\pi_1$ and computing the welfare effect of switching, holding estimated quality paths fixed. We model state quality as an AR(1) latent process, $\psi_{j,t} = \rho\,\psi_{j,t-1} + u_{j,t}$, as introduced in Section ?. The persistence parameter $\rho$ and the innovation variance $\sigma^2_u$ are estimated jointly with the city and period fixed effects via an EM algorithm combined with a Kalman smoother (Shumway and Stoffer, 1982; Durbin and Koopman, 2012).

Switching between states is governed by the contest success function from equation (3): \[ P(S_{it} > 0) = \Lambda\bigl(\pi_0 + \pi_1 \cdot (\psi_{C,t} - \psi_{I,t})\bigr), \] where $\psi_{I,t}$ is the incumbent state's quality and $\psi_{C,t}$ is the quality of a randomly drawn challenger from the set of contemporaneous states. The switching cost $\beta$ is estimated from the first stage. The contest parameters $(\pi_0, \pi_1)$ are estimated via Simulated Method of Moments (SMM), matching two moments: (i) the observed switching frequency and (ii) the mean quality gain per switch. We conduct inference via bootstrap.

Discussion

Both approaches point to a net positive effect of state competition. The TV-AKM shows that within-state reform more than compensates for negative sorting and switching costs at all levels of competition intensity. The AR(1) welfare accounting, which holds quality paths fixed and varies only the reallocation of cities across states, confirms that the direct gains from switching exceed its costs. Both testable predictions of the escape competition framework are confirmed: within-state reform increases with competition intensity (via TV-AKM), and contests are quality-biased with $\pi_1 > 0$ (via AR(1)-AKM and SMM).

The two specifications play complementary roles in reaching this conclusion. The TV-AKM measures the three channels of equation (1) descriptively, decomposing realized trajectories without parametric assumptions. But it requires both the incumbent and the challenger to have estimated state effects in the same interval—which is not possible when the incumbent has lost all its cities before the transition occurs. The AR(1) specification goes further: because the Kalman smoother extrapolates $\psi_{j,t}$ for states that currently have no cities, it can construct the counterfactual “what would city size have been under continued incumbent rule?”\ Moreover, the SMM simulation evaluates the contest for all city-periods—including those where the incumbent prevailed—recovering $\pi_1$ from the full distribution of outcomes rather than only from observed transitions.24

The finding that cities switch close to the optimal rate reinforces the creative destruction interpretation. Competition in the European state system was beneficial not only because it brought better challengers to power, but because it would have intensified the pressure for incumbent reform. This is the distinctive prediction of escape competition: the welfare gain comes not from the transitions themselves, but from the threat of transition.

Conclusions

Competition between European states imposed substantial short-term costs on cities but generated meaningful long-term gains through improved governance. We decompose the effect of territorial competition into three channels—switching costs, between-state sorting, and within-state reform—and find that escape competition, not classical creative destruction, was the dominant mechanism. Within-state reform accounts for the bulk of state quality improvement, increasing with competition intensity, while between-state sorting is negative in aggregate. The net effect is positive: the cumulative gains from incumbent reform more than compensate for the costs of transitions and the negative sorting at the moment of switching.

The three-channel decomposition in Figure 8 reveals the mechanism underlying these gains. Within-state reform—the improvement of incumbent states over time—is by far the dominant positive channel, more than compensating for the costs of transitions. On aggregate, between-state sorting is negative at the moment of transition, but this is entirely driven by transient transitions involving states that dissolve shortly after taking control. For durable transitions, sorting is mildly positive, indicating that classical creative destruction operates alongside escape competition: when a transition leads to a lasting change in governance, cities tend to sort toward states of equal or higher quality.

The structural AR(1) model in Section 7 complements this descriptive decomposition by showing that, despite the contemporaneous negative sorting pattern, the counterfactual welfare effect of switching is positive: cities are better off having experienced transitions than they would have been under continued incumbent rule. Contests are quality-biased ($\hat{\pi}_1 > 0$), with higher-quality challengers more likely to displace incumbents, particularly at the entry margin. The model-implied optimal switching rate is very similar the observed rate, indicating that the European state system was close to the growth-maximizing level of competition. Together, the two approaches identify the European state system as operating primarily in the escape competition regime: competition was beneficial chiefly because the threat of displacement drove incumbent reform, with classical creative destruction contributing at the margin through durable transitions that brought higher-quality challengers to power.

In light of Europe's ascendancy over other world regions, this tradeoff seen within Europe may have acted globally across a much wider domain. The structural model's finding that Europe was slightly below the optimal switching rate puts the continent's experience in perspective: China and South Asia present two cases that may have been exposed to insufficient and excessive creative destruction, respectively. Due to China's early unification under a single state after the establishment of the Qin dynasty and especially the Han dynasty around 200 BC, the Chinese state faced only infrequent threats from other states (Finer, 1997; Rosenthal and Wong, 2011). As a result, Chinese cities were exposed to much less upheaval than European ones, but in turn the Chinese state may have ossified and the quality of governance may have failed to improve—precisely the absence of the escape competition mechanism we document for Europe.

South Asia presents the opposite case. The subcontinent experienced nearly one hundred “supra-regional” or “pan-Indian” states (Schwartzberg, 1978), with no city besides Delhi serving as a reliable center of political power—in stark contrast to Europe's durable capitals. The extreme variability in the number and location of states, perhaps facilitated by the subcontinent's open geography, may have led to repeated conquest and looting of cities, delaying capital deepening—a state system where competition was destructive rather than creative. European colonization profoundly affected India's subsequent trajectory, but the instability of South Asian urban hierarchies and the destructiveness of territorial conquest are pre-colonial phenomena, visible well before the late eighteenth century. (Dincecco et al., 2022) document a complementary pattern at a different margin: exploiting within-India variation in pre-colonial conflict exposure, they find that areas with moderate local conflict experienced greater state-making and better long-run outcomes. This is consistent with the inverted-U logic of our framework: at the local level, moderate competitive pressure can drive institutional improvement through the escape competition channel, even within a state system whose aggregate level of instability may have been excessive.

Ultimately, whether a state system can benefit from competition depends crucially on its tendency to engender states that are both high quality and highly competitive in the contest with existing states. While this seems to have been true in Europe's second millennium overall, there is no guarantee for this to be true in other places or at other times—for instance, ancient Greece may offer a cautionary example, where states with seemingly high quality institutions succumbed to more powerful states that were detrimental to long-run growth. Understanding the features of a state system in which high quality states survive and thrive is an important avenue for future research in political economy.
