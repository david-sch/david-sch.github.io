David Schönholzer

Abstract

Local governments provide many essential public goods and services, such as education, policing, and public infrastructure. But how much households value these public goods compared to neighborhood amenities remains unclear. We develop a simple equilibrium sorting model that endogenously generates price and racial sorting discontinuities at local government boundaries. We then use a sample of 1.5 million homes in thousands of neighborhoods that straddle local government boundaries to isolate these discontinuities. We find that households value access to specific local governments even when comparing homes across boundaries on opposite sides of the same residential street, especially at boundaries that delineate both cities and school districts. Variance decompositions show that households value local government choice similarly to neighborhood choice. Structural estimates of the model suggest substantial heterogeneity of valuations across racial groups, with peer preferences playing an important role. JEL classification: H4, H7, R2. \\ Keywords: Local governments, public goods, peer valuation, boundary RD.

Introduction

Local governments provide various essential services to households, such as education, public safety, and infrastructure. A rich history of research in economics attempts to estimate the value of these local public goods, going back to studies by (Oates, 1969), (Brueckner, 1979) and (Epple and Sieg, 1999), and for schools by (Card and Krueger, 1992), (Bogart and Cromwell, 1997), and (Black, 1999). How much households value these public goods has important implications for household sorting and the efficiency of public goods provision (Samuelson, 1954; Tiebout, 1956; Bewley, 1981; Hoxby, 1999), but compelling evidence on the valuation of publicly provided goods and the local governments that provide them remains elusive.

Estimating these valuations is challenging for at least two reasons. First, it is hard to distinguish between households valuing access to local governments from valuation of desirable neighborhoods. Household sorting plays an important role even on a relatively small geographic scale, which has put into question whether comparing house prices across local government boundaries can serve as a reliable way to estimate the valuation of local public goods. Indeed, these boundaries often exhibit discontinuities in both prices as well as characteristics of nearby residents (Bayer et al., 2007; Boustan, 2013; Gibbons et al., 2013). Second, even if it were possible to isolate local government valuation, it is unclear to what extent these preferences differ across different types of households (e.g. by race/ethnicity) and different types of local governments (i.e. school districts and cities). Such heterogeneous preferences are crucial to understand how public policy affects household sorting and segregation in urban areas, and they are important inputs for models in urban economics and local public finance.

This paper makes progress on these challenges by combining a large dataset of millions of individual property sales records with new methods, guided by a simple sorting model, to elicit preferences for thousands of local governments. We begin by developing an equilibrium model of residential sorting near a boundary, with two goals in mind. First, the model helps us to interpret price and racial sorting discontinuities jointly. Specifically, we show that average group-specific preferences for one local government over another can be identified as the weighted sum of the price discontinuity and a racial sorting discontinuity between any two jurisdictions, with weights determined by price elasticities. Second, under the assumption that household preferences exhibit discontinuities at local government boundaries, the model provides a structural foundation to identify and estimate group-specific preferences for local governments. These preferences can then be decomposed into observable characteristics of local governments and their residents.

To provide evidence for the basic assumption of the model—that households value which local government they reside in, even when holding neighborhood amenities fixed—we turn to a newly assembled dataset of 1.5 million single-family homes near thousands of local government boundaries. We estimate both price and racial discontinuities across these boundaries, controlling for housing characteristics and street fixed effects. This amounts to a comparison of similar homes on opposite sides of the same residential street but in different local governments.1 We then demonstrate that these discontinuities can be used to decompose house prices into unobserved local government and neighborhood valuations. This decomposition allows us to compare the relative contributions of local governments and neighborhoods to house price variation, benchmarking our earlier boundary discontinuities across local governments.

These analyses yield several new insights into the valuation of local governments. First, we show that prices and some racial shares jump discontinuously at local government boundaries even when comparing houses on opposite sides of the same residential street. We use a hold-out sample to order the sides of each boundary into lower and higher-average price sides. The price discontinuity between these two sides, estimated on the remaining sample, is about \$18,000, of which \$2,300 cannot be explained by housing or neighborhood characteristics. This suggests that about 13% (i.e. \$2,300/\$18,000) of housing costs are due to access to local governments. These discontinuities are especially large when city and school district boundaries coincide—in fact, more than twice as large than the sum of those at city-only and district-only boundaries—, suggesting that municipal services (such as zoning or policing) and high-quality schools are highly complementary. While it is possible that unobservable house characteristics jump at the boundary, they would have to be largely uncorrelated with observable house characteristics to have a large effect on this estimate.

Turning to racial discontinuities, in contrast to earlier findings, there is no indication that White or Black homebuyers sort discontinuously on one side; however, Asian households exhibit substantially higher willingness to pay for access to high-quality local governments and are more likely to sort on that side of the boundary. Hispanics are slightly more likely to sort on the lower-price side. Although there is no discontinuity for White and Black homebuyers, we estimate considerable sorting for these households within local governments, ameliorating concerns about measurement error in race driving the result.

We then disaggregate the price discontinuities of thousands of connected boundary neighborhoods and decompose them into a neighborhood component, a local government component, and their covariance. To this end, we adapt methods from labor economics typically used to decompose variation in wages into firm and worker components (Abowd et al., 1999), commonly referred to as AKM, but using data on houses across space instead of workers over time. Specifically, fixed effects for neighboring houses on either side of a boundary identify neighborhood effects, and fixed effects for local governments connected by neighborhoods identify local government effects. We find that local governments explain a similar share of house price variation as neighborhoods and that their covariance is often negative, implying that households in some areas trade off better local governments against better neighborhoods. We then correlate the estimated local government valuations with observable characteristics of local governments that speak to the role of public goods versus that of peers. Both student achievement of school districts and the crime clearance rates of cities are correlated with valuations, but these relationships are much weaker after the inclusion of peer characteristics.

While these variance decomposition results allow us to distinguish between neighborhood and local government valuation, they cannot identify preferences that are heterogeneous across groups. To this end, we return to the equilibrium sorting model and estimate the structural preference parameters via maximum likelihood. We find considerable heterogeneity in preferences, primarily driven by an own-race preference of most racial groups, but also by a preference for affluent peers for some groups. This suggests that at least some of the segregation observed across local governments is due to household preferences for certain peers, above and beyond their immediate neighbors.

It is worth highlighting an important limitation. The approach of this paper is suitable to estimate the value of the excludable portion of local public goods. But many public services provided by local governments may be non-excludable. For example, crime and pollution spill across boundaries, and local government policies to combat these problems may be of considerable value to households. In this sense, estimates provided here are a lower bound of the total valuation of local governments. However, the excludable portion of local public goods, including access to high-quality schools and police departments, is important in itself, and providing estimates of their value can be useful for theory and policy.

Links to existing literature. This paper builds on a long literature studying how local public goods and property taxes capitalize in house prices (Oates, 1969; Brueckner, 1979; Brueckner, 1982; Deller, 1990; Berglas, 1984; Brueckner and Lee, 1989), often using boundary discontinuities (Black, 1999; Bayer et al., 2007; Boustan, 2013; Gibbons et al., 2013; Turner et al., 2014). The contribution to this literature is threefold: first, due to the large sample of house sales available in this paper, it is the first to use the across-the-street comparison to study how differences in public goods and property taxes are capitalized in the housing market, holding other components of neighborhood quality fixed. Second, it estimates valuation separately for thousands of cities and school districts, providing a comprehensive assessment of the overall value of local governments. Earlier studies often value only a single public good such as schooling. Moreover, studies using fine-grained micro data are often constrained to a single region, whereas this study estimates public goods valuation across hundreds of metro areas. Third, the sorting model developed here identifies valuation of public goods and peers even when households sort discontinuously at the boundary, as is often the case.

A number of studies propose methods to capture unobserved neighborhood quality, amenities, and household sorting for valuation of local public goods (Gyourko and Tracy, 1991; Kahn, 1995; Bogart and Cromwell, 1997; Bayer et al., 2007; Caetano, 2019; Albouy et al., 2020; Laliberté, 2021). (Bogart and Cromwell, 1997) are the first to exploit the fact that city and school district boundaries do not always overlap to estimate school valuation separately from city valuation. The approach presented here is most similar to (Laliberté, 2021), who uses boundary discontinuities in student achievement across school attendance zones in Montreal combined with a student-mover design to separate neighborhood effects from school effects. In contrast, we show that connected sets of boundary discontinuities can be used directly to decompose neighborhood and local government valuation and to study how they correlate with observable characteristics. This methodology has wide applicability across many settings in local public finance, urban economics, development, and history.

Finally, this paper connects to a large literature on household valuation of peers (de Bartolome, 1990; Benabou, 1993; Durlauf, 1996; Epple and Romano, 1998; Sethi and Somanathan, 2004; Calabrese et al., 2006; Bayer et al., 2022). (Rothstein, 2006) and (Abdulkadiroglu et al., 2020) provide evidence that households primarily value peers attending the same school rather than a school's capacity to improve student achievement. Our findings show that this is true even when households choose a residential location (rather than school choice conditional on residence) and when holding neighbors fixed (unlike Bayer et al., 2022), and that city governance amplifies this valuation. Given the lack of evidence of the valuation of city governance, their public goods, and their peer composition, these estimates help to fill an important gap in local public finance.

Section 5 presents boundary discontinuity estimates using the across-the-street design, followed by Section 6 describing the house price decomposition into a local government and neighborhood component. Section 7 conducts the structural estimation and Section 8 concludes.

Background: Local Governments in the U.S.

The bulk of local public goods in the U.S. in terms of expenditures are provided by school districts and municipalities (henceforth “cities”). Unlike in most countries in the world, the vast majority of school districts and cities are governed separately in the United States, with some school districts providing services to multiple cities, such as the Los Angeles Unified district to 26 cities in the region; and sometimes a city is serviced by multiple school districts, such as Phoenix or San Jose, each having multiple elementary school districts offering education services.

The top panel of Figure 1 shows the spatial distribution of all 19,539 cities and 13,193 school districts in the United States. As we can see, the distribution differs markedly by region: In New England, there are often more school districts than cities, while in the South there are relatively more cities and generally a sparser distribution of local governments. Major metropolitan areas are often densely packed with dozens of local governments clustered around a central city.

Local governments provide a wide variety of services (see Figure A1). As shown in Appendix Table A1, both cities and school districts vary enormously in size, demographic characteristics, and per capita expenditures. School districts are primarily responsible for K-12 education provision to any household in their jurisdiction. Cities have a broad portfolio of responsibilities. Their largest expenditure item and their most important responsibility is public safety, including police and fire protection. On average, cities spend about \$300 per capita on police protection and \$100 per capita on fire protection. Local services include a number of other items too, such as street maintenance, utilities, parks and recreation, libraries, sewerage maintenance, and solid waste management.

Many of the public goods provided by school districts and cities are excludable to non-residents who do not live in the jurisdiction. While school districts sometimes have programs for non-residents to join (Bergman, 2018), residency in the district is usually required to attend a school in the district (Center, 2005). Similarly, access to services such as police, fire protection, and emergency medical services are tightly linked to residence as well: calls to 9-1-1 are routed to the jurisdiction associated with the address. Zoning, regulation, and other local ordinances are also sharply tied to residence, although they may exert local externalities in the neighborhood (Turner et al., 2014).

If a household highly values any of these excludable services, it is thus usually required to reside in the associated jurisdiction. The extent to which excludable public goods are important is then reflected in a sharp difference in capitalization of local governments at the boundary. In contrast, other local public goods and neighborhood amenities are not excludable. As a result, a household can still enjoy these amenities even when living nearby but outside of the jurisdiction, and they would be capitalized smoothly across local government boundaries.

A Simple Model of Residential Sorting Across Local Governments

We develop a simple framework to make the distinction between local governments and neighborhoods precise, drawing on ideas from (Bilal and Rossi-Hansberg, 2021). On the supply side, we consider a residential neighborhood that straddles a local government boundary with smoothly varying neighborhood quality but a different bundle of public goods (and peers with whom to share them) on either side of the boundary. For simplicity, we assume that strict zoning regulation limits supply responses on either side. On the demand side, we are interested in identifying the valuation of both neighborhoods and local governments. Hence, we allow for households to sort endogenously both within and across each of the local governments on either side of the boundary. In equilibrium, patterns of household sorting and prices are pinned down by the combination of locational amenities (neighborhood and local government valuation) and household preferences.

Environment. Consider a single neighborhood with locations $s \in \mathcal{S} = [-0.5, 0.5]$ near a boundary at $s = 0$ separating two local governments. Each location has a fixed unit of uniform housing supply.2 Households are divided into groups $g\in \mathcal{G} = \{1,...,G\}$. Each group makes up a share $m_g$ in the neighborhood. We assume that each household makes a single residential choice according to the following indirect utility function: $U_g(s) = V_g(s) + \sigma_g \varepsilon_g(s)$. The systematic component of utility is given by \[ V_{g}(s)=\mu_{g}(s)+\psi_{g}(s)-p(s), \] where $\mu_g(s)$ captures group-specific average valuation of amenities that varies smoothly across the neighborhood; $\psi_g(s)$ is group-specific average valuation of local governments, which is constant within local governments but may jump at the boundary; and $p(s)$ is the equilibrium price for housing at $s$, which applies to households irrespective of their group membership. Households may value a local government $\psi_g(s)$ directly because of the local public goods it provides, or indirectly because of strategic complementarities of sharing the local government with a particular set of households residing there in equilibrium. The stochastic component of utility, $\sigma_g \varepsilon_g(s)$, consists of an identically and independently distributed type-I extreme value random variable $\varepsilon_g(s)$ and a dispersion parameter $\sigma_g$ that captures the relative weight of idiosyncratic drivers of residential choice not captured in neighborhood amenities, local governments, or prices.3

Equilibrium. An allocation of households across the neighborhood and a price for each location are a sorting equilibrium if the following two conditions hold. First, households choose locations such that they maximize their utility. Under utility maximization, the density of households of group $g$ across the neighborhood, denoted by $f_g(s)$, is

\begin{equation} f_{g}(s)=\frac{\exp\left(V_{g}(s)/\sigma_{g}\right)}{\int_{\mathcal{S}}\exp\left(V_{g}(\ell)/\sigma_{g}\right)d\ell}, \end{equation}

as shown in Appendix 15. The second condition for equilibrium is that the housing market clears in the neighborhood, which requires $\sum_{g\in \mathcal{G}}m_{g}f_{g}(s)=1$ at every location. The left-hand side is the demand for housing at a location across all groups, taking into account the size of each group, whereas the right-hand side is the unit supply of housing at each location. Intuitively, if demand exceeds supply at some location, then the price at this location has to rise to dissuade some households from choosing to live there.4

Figure 6 illustrates the relationship between preferences, prices, and residential choice in a sorting equilibrium. Three groups have preferences for amenities and local governments as shown in Panel A. In particular, households in Group 1 and 2 have a preference for the local government on the right of the boundary, whereas those in Group 3 have a preference for the one on the left. Panel B shows the corresponding equilibrium price schedule, which exhibits a discontinuity at the boundary. Finally, Panel C shows the equilibrium sorting behavior of the three groups. In line with their preferences, households in Group 2 and 3 sort discontinuously on the side of the boundary of their preferred local government, but interestingly, those in Group 1 sort on the left side even though they prefer the government on the right. The reason for this is that households in Group 2 have such a strong preference for the right government that many of those in Group 1 are not willing to pay the resulting market premium to live there.5

Identification. The illustration in Figure 6 shows how preferences yield equilibrium prices and sorting behavior. But the econometrician solves the reverse problem: using observed prices and sorting behavior to infer preferences. To see how preferences are identified from observed prices and sorting patterns, define for any variable $x(s)$ its boundary discontinuity $\Delta x \equiv x(0) - \lim_{s\rightarrow 0^-} x(s)$. Then, we can express a group's preference for the government on the right of the boundary over the one on the left as

\begin{equation} \Delta \psi_g = \sigma_g \Delta \log f_g + \Delta p. \end{equation}

We can see that these preferences are identified as the weighted sum of the price discontinuity $\Delta p$, which applies to all groups in the same way, and the group-specific sorting discontinuity $\Delta \log f_g$, which captures a group's willingness to pay a premium above and beyond the market price.6 The weight on the sorting discontinuity is given by the dispersion parameter $\sigma_g$: if a group's preferences are more idiosyncratic (i.e. larger $\sigma_g$), then systematic sorting implies a larger willingness to pay to live in one government over another. In contrast to local government preferences, the dispersion parameter $\sigma_g$ is identified by variation in residential choices and prices away from the boundary.7

Example: Piedmont-Oakland. It is useful to see Equation (2) in action, using an example from our data (described below). Piedmont, CA is an affluent city and school district in the San Francisco Bay Area whose residents are mostly White and Asian, whereas neighboring Oakland is much larger and more diverse, both in terms of household income and race. Figure 7 shows the price and sorting discontinuities near the boundary between the two local governments. The panel on the left shows that prices for similar housing jump by about \$172,000 at the boundary, corresponding to the $ p$ term in Equation (\ref{eq:disconts}). The right panel shows that White households sort discontinuously on the less expensive Oakland side, whereas Asian households are more likely to reside on the more expensive Piedmont side. Transforming these discontinuities in White and Asian shares at the boundary into densities and taking the difference of their logs yields sorting discontinuity estimates of $-0.154$ for $ f_White$ and $0.215$ for $ f_Asian$, respectively. Finally, price variation relative to variation in the (log) densities of the two groups yields estimates of \$123,000 for $\sigma_{\text{White}}$ and \$103,000 for $\sigma_Asian$.8

Putting these numbers together according to Equation (2), we get preference estimates of White households for Piedmont over Oakland of \$153,000 and for Asian households of \$194,000. Even though White households prefer Piedmont just like Asian households, the stronger preference of Asian households and the resulting higher price to live there dissuades some White households from residing in Piedmont, leading to a discontinuous drop of White households when crossing from Oakland into Piedmont.

Data

To put this model to the test, we combine two primary datasets. First, we use fine-grained local government boundaries from the Census TIGER/Line database. Second, we combine these boundaries with a large sample of individual property assessment records from ATTOM. We supplement these data with standard records from the American Community Survey (ACS), from the Stanford Education Data Archive (SEDA), and Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR). We describe each of the primary datasets in turn.

Local government boundaries. Local governance in the U.S. consists of various entities including counties, school districts, incorporated towns and cities, townships, and special districts. We focus on school districts as well as incorporated towns and cities (jointly referred to as cities) as the two most important layers of local governance as they make up the bulk of local public employment, revenue and expenditures.

To measure the extent of these two types of jurisdictions, we use shapefiles from the Census's TIGER/Line place boundaries database. These consist of detailed boundaries for each place (i.e. cities and unincorporated Census Designated Places, CDPs), for a total of more than 19,000 cities.9 School district boundaries have been prepared by the NCES's EDGE school district boundaries program, which also draws on the TIGER/Line database. It combines elementary school districts and unified school district boundaries that jointly cover all of the U.S. with more than 13,000 districts.10

City-district pairs. We use these boundaries to create city-district pairs, which we simply call local governments. The territorial extent of such a local government is a subset of an overlapping city and district territory, and in case city and district boundaries do not coincide, the local government is a strict subset. This means that neighboring local governments are often in the same city but different school districts or vice versa. This is illustrated in Figure 2: the blue boundary in the Northwest separates the City of Cupertino in the North from the City of Saratoga in the South. The green boundary in the South divides the Cupertino Unified School District (CUSD) in the North from the Saratoga Unified School District (SUSD) in the South. Hence local government .5pt-1pt 1 consists of the Cupertino city-district pair; local government .5pt-1pt 2 of the City of Saratoga but the Cupertino school district; and local government .5pt-1pt 3 combines the Saratoga city-district pair.

ATTOM assessor records. ATTOM offers a comprehensive database of individual property assessment records drawing primarily on county assessor records and covering a total of 118 million properties over 2003-2016, with near-universal coverage of properties for a little more than half of all states. These records have been standardized and cleaned across jurisdictions, allowing us to focus on comparable properties across the country. We restrict our sample to single family residential properties since multi-unit residential properties and commercial properties are harder to compare. For each property, we have information on the sales price at the time of the most recent transaction, the most recent property tax payment, its geocoded location, age, lot size, indoor square footage, the number of rooms, the number of bathrooms, and the number of stories. We also know the last names of the owner of each property, which allows us to probabilistically infer their race and ethnicity using the Bayesian procedure in [][see Appendix ? for details]Diamond_etal_2019. To this end, we merge property records with census data assigning probabilities to last names according to race and ethnicity into five categories: White, Black, Asian, Hispanic, and Unknown. The latter category applies when no posterior probability of any race reaches 80%.

These steps result in a dataset of approximately 25 million single family homes across the 30 states shown in the bottom panel of Figure 1. As shown in Table 1, the average house price in 2010 dollars is about \$235,000 and has been last sold in 2007. Around two-thirds of buyers have White surnames, 9\% have Hispanic surnames, 5\% each have Black or Asian surnames, and for about 15\% of surnames there is no clear race/ethnicity. Houses have been built on average in 1977, have 2.25 bathrooms and 3.69 rooms in total. Neighborhood characteristics from the census show slightly higher shares of all racial/ethnic categories other than Asian, in part due to the 15\% of unknown buyer race/ethnicity. Average household income is \$66,900 in the neighborhood, with around 20% of household heads being college educated.

Boundary sample and Neighborhoods

Next, we link these 25 million houses to the nearest local government boundary, restricting the matching to properties within 500 meters (0.31 miles) of any boundary. We include only houses that are near boundaries with a sufficient number of houses on either side of the boundary. Specifically, we require at least 100 houses on each side of the boundary, with further restrictions on sufficient density of houses as detailed in Appendix ?. We define neighborhoods as 500-meter segments along these boundaries (the white diamonds in Figure 2) and associate each house with the nearest one of these neighborhoods. Neighborhoods with no properties in the immediate vicinity (100m), which may be due to a physical barrier running along the boundary, are dropped together with their associated houses. To ensure neighborhood continuity across boundaries, we drop boundary segments that cross a highway or other major roadway and keep only those that coincide with residential streets, as can be seen in the center of Figure 2. This means that neighborhoods running across major roadways such as 8 Mile Road in Detroit are excluded. This definition of neighborhoods guarantees that there is a substantial number of neighborhoods that cut across local government boundaries. If we were to use census blocks or tracts instead, we would have very little variation in local governments within census units.

This results in a boundary sample of 1.47 million properties, which are slightly more expensive and less likely to have been bought by a buyer with a White surname. Since there are many more houses near boundaries in dense urban areas, houses have on average substantially smaller lot sizes and are slightly older, although they have a higher average number of rooms. They are in neighborhoods that are less White, substantially more Hispanic and Asian, and have slightly higher than household income at around $71,100.

Clusters of connected local governments. As each local government may border several other local governments, they form metropolitan clusters in the form of a spatial network of connected local governments. These clusters arise entirely endogenously based on the availability of ATTOM property records across states as well as sufficient density of houses near boundaries. As shown in the bottom panel of Figure 1, they are similar to standard definitions of metropolitan areas such as Consolidated Metropolitan Statistical Areas (CMSAs) as defined by the census, but diverge in important ways. First, large CMSAs often have multiple clusters within them that are not connected. For example, there are two large clusters with more than 20 connected local governments each on Long Island. In total, there are 444 clusters with an average number of 4.43 local governments. The largest cluster is Los Angeles with 73 connected local governments.

As an example of a cluster, see Figure 3, the cluster around San Jose, CA (and the second-largest in the country). We can see that most of Silicon Valley is connected through residential neighborhoods as demonstrated in the Cupertino-Saratoga example in Figure 2, which can be found in the Southwest on the map in Figure 3. The red boundaries show connected local governments that are separated by stacked city-district boundaries, such as the boundary between Palo Alto and Los Altos in the West. Blue boundaries separate cities, with either side of the boundary belonging to the same school district; and green boundaries separate districts, with either side receiving services from the same city.

After connecting local governments through houses near boundaries, there are just over a thousand cities and school districts in the data, as shown in columns 4-6 in Table A1. These cities and school districts are substantially larger than the average city and school district across the country. They are also more diverse, have higher average household income, higher share of college-educated residents, and higher revenue and expenditures, as one would expect from local governments in high-density urban areas.

Discontinuities at Local Government Boundaries

We now estimate discontinuities in both prices and racial composition across local government boundaries holding neighborhood characteristics fixed, in line with the underlying assumption in the model. Hence, we narrow the comparison to similar houses bought by similar buyers located close-by on the same street but on opposite sides of a local government boundary.

Empirical Approach: Stacked Boundary Discontinuities

We begin by stacking all boundaries in our data on top of one another after orienting them such that the side with the lower average price is on the left and the side with the higher average price on the right. To avoid sorting boundary sides on the outcome of interest, we only use prices of houses in the 100 meter bin farthest from each boundary as a hold-out sample and subsequently estimate discontinuities on the stacked data from the remaining 400 meters closest to the boundary. Consequently, while house prices increase mechanically as we move from the left to the right, this setup allows us to estimate the extent to which house prices jump discontinuously at a local government boundary, in contrast to a gradual change in neighborhood quality and corresponding house prices. Concretely, we run

\begin{equation} p_{in} = \alpha_n + \psi D_{in}+g_n(S_{in})+X_{in}'\gamma+\epsilon_{in}, \end{equation}

where houses $i=1,...,I_n$ are nested in $n = 1,...,N$ neighborhoods. $S_{in}$ is the distance to the nearest boundary and $p_{in}$ is the house price at the time of last transaction. $\alpha_n$ are fixed effects for streets as well as neighborhoods. We also include cluster-by-year-of-transaction fixed effects throughout to account for different house price trends across metro areas. $g_n(\cdot)$ is a neighborhood-specific smooth function capturing local variation in neighborhood quality, and $X_{in}$ are housing characteristics, which include age, lot size, interior square feet, and fixed effects for the number of rooms, bathrooms and stories. The parameter of interest is $\psi$, the size of the average discontinuity in house prices at the boundary going from the lower to the higher average-price side, which is indicated by $D_{in} = \mathbf{1}[S_{in} \geq 0]$.

We show results for both nonparametric (Calonico et al., 2014) as well as quartic polynomial regression discontinuity estimates, to ensure results are robust to the estimation approach. All standard errors are clustered on the level of the boundary, allowing for arbitrary spatial correlation near the boundary.

Price Discontinuities

Table 1 presents a simple comparison of houses on the low and high average-price side of local government boundaries. Houses on the high side sell on average for \$284,900, which is \$20,600 higher than on the low side. Buyers on the high side are slightly more likely to be White or Asian, and slightly less likely to be Black or Hispanic. Houses on the high side are slightly newer and larger. Average neighborhood characteristics are also quite different, with households on the high side more likely to be non-minority, college educated, and high income. Since it is unclear how much of the simple price difference is due to gradually changing neighborhood characteristics or local government characteristics, we now go beyond this broad comparison using the methodology described in the previous subsection to zero in on the local discontinuity at the boundary.

Discontinuities in Race/Ethnicity

The evidence provided so far suggests there are significant discontinuities in prices across boundaries, comparing houses on the same street bought by a household of the same race in the same year. We now investigate to what extent households of a different race/ethnicity sort across jurisdictions differently. Figure 5 shows the share of homebuyers of a certain race/ethnicity near a boundary using the same estimation approach as in Figure 4, again including neighborhood, cluster-by-year, and street fixed effects as well as property controls.

Surprisingly, there is little evidence of racial sorting across the boundary for White and Black households. The share of buyers with a White surname increases gradually from about 55% to 57% at the boundary, and then continues growing smoothly up to about 58% at 400 meters into the high average-price side. The share of Black households exhibits the exact reverse pattern: it drops smoothly from just under 6% to a little over 5% near the boundary, from where on it continues to drop smoothly to below 5%.13 In contrast, the share Hispanic and Asian households exhibit a substantial discontinuity at the boundary. The share of Asian buyers falls gradually from about 8.5% to 8% moving towards the low side of the boundary. At that point, it jumps discontinuously to more than 9% and continues growing to about 10%. Hispanic buyers exhibit the reverse pattern: Their share starts off at around 15%, gradually dropping to about 14% as we approach the boundary. It then drops and remains constant at just under 13%.

One concern is that Asian and Hispanic surnames are easier to identify than White and Black surnames, which may be driving the difference in discontinuities. But the slopes of White and Black shares are quite steep, suggesting that we are able to identify substantial sorting of White and Black households within local governments according to neighborhood quality. If differential noise were responsible the lack of jumps for White and Black shares, then identifying neighborhood sorting would be more difficult as well. To further address this concern, Appendix Figure A2 shows very similar estimates based on Census block data, which rely on self-reported race rather than race inferred from surnames.

Comparing Local Government and Neighborhood Valuation

Stacking boundaries ordered by the average price in the 400-500m bins from the boundary allows us to detect a discontinuity, provided that the discontinuity is in the direction of the average price change. But this assumption may not be satisfied, and ordering boundaries in this way removes a lot of interesting heterogeneity across local governments. In particular, we are interested in how local government valuation compares to the valuation of neighborhoods, local public goods, and peers who co-reside in the local government.

We can avoid ordering boundaries by exploiting the fact that the sample of neighborhoods form a bipartite network of local governments in space connected by mutual boundaries, as shown in Figure 3. It turns out that this data structure can be organized along the same lines as two-way fixed effects models estimating unobserved worker and firm fixed effects in labor economics [henceforth AKM]Abowd_etal_1999, whereby we use neighborhoods “moving” across local governments through space instead of workers moving across firms over time. As we demonstrate in Appendix 13, the conditions for separate identification of unobserved neighborhood and local government effects are identical to AKM.

We can implement this strategy empirically through the regression

\begin{equation} p_{in}=\alpha_{n}+\psi_{j(i,n)}+g\left(S_{in}\right)+X_{in}'\gamma+\epsilon_{in} \end{equation}

where $\alpha_n$ is the average valuation of neighborhood $n$, and $\psi_{j(i,n)}$ is the average valuation for local government $j = j(i,n)$ associated with house $i$ in neighborhood $n$, depending on which side of the boundary house $i$ is located. $X_{in}$ are the same housing characteristics as in equation (3) as well as state-by-year fixed effects. $g(S_{in})$ are separate quartic polynomials on either side of the boundary. Identifying assumptions are discussed in detail in Appendix 13. This decomposition allows neighborhood and local government valuations to be correlated, but it assumes they are additively separable, which we can test by comparing this model to a saturated model (see Section ?).

We can then decompose the variance in log house prices into

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \text{Var}(p_{in}) & =\text{Var}(\alpha_{n})+\text{Var}(\psi_{j(i,n)})+\text{Var}(X_{in}'\gamma)+\text{Var}(\epsilon_{in})\\ & \quad+2\text{Cov}(\alpha_{n},\psi_{j(i,n)})+2 \text{Cov}(\psi_{j(i,n)},X_{in}'\gamma)+2 \text{Cov}(\alpha_{n},X_{in}'\gamma) \end{aligned} \end{equation}

where we are particularly interested in the contributions of the neighborhood component $\text{Var}\left(\alpha_{n}\right)$ and the local government component $\text{Var}(\psi_{j(i,n)})$. These components tell us how much of the variation in house prices is explained through differences in neighborhood and local government valuation. In addition, $\text{Cov}(\alpha_{n},\psi_{j(i,n)})$ indicates the extent to which homebuyers seem to treat neighborhoods and local governments as complements or substitutes, depending on whether it is positive or negative. Since plug-in estimates as in AKM or (Card et al., 2013) are known to be biased, we also present estimates using the leave-one-out estimator developed by [henceforth KSS]Kline_etal_2020, which estimates bias-corrected variance components with unrestricted heterogeneity.

In addition to estimating variance components using equation (5), we can also investigate how the estimated local government effects correlate with observed characteristics of corresponding cities and school districts. While these estimates are not causal, they demonstrate what aspects of local governments are valued in equilibrium. We study this by running \[ \widehat{\psi}_{j}=\delta_{h(j)} + Z_{j}'\beta+r_{j}, \] where $\delta_{h(j)}$ are city or district fixed effects, $Z_j$ is a vector of observable city and school district characteristics, and $r_j$ is an error term. Including city or district fixed effects restricts the comparison to cities in the same school district or vice versa.

These correlations are not causal, but they are informative about which characteristics are associated with household valuation in equilibrium. We study local government characteristics that help us to distinguish between the direct valuation of public goods and the indirect valuation of local governments through peers. Finally, output characteristics such as school achievement or crime clearance rates represent the idea that households value the quality of public services, which in itself may be a product both of government inputs and peers.

Structural Estimation of Demand Model

While the variance decompositions in the previous section allow us to speak to the relative value of local governments and neighborhoods, they do not account for the joint determination of prices and sorting behavior captured in the model. Hence, we now estimate the structural parameters of the model separately for each of the 1,744 boundaries in our dataset for which there are a sufficient number of households of at least two groups. We proceed as follows.

Residualize and smooth prices. Since the model assumes that all location amenities other than local governments are smooth at the boundary, we residualize prices with respect to observable characteristics, in a process similar in spirit to (Rossi‐Hansberg et al., 2010). Concretely, we take real house prices values in 2010 dollars $\tilde{p}(s)$ and regress them on a vector $\mathbf{X}(s)$ consisting of boundary points, year of sale, and the same hedonic controls as those used in previous sections (lot size, indoor square footage, age, and fixed effects for the number of rooms, the number of bathrooms, and the number of stories), separately for each boundary.

Next, we take the residuals of this regression and regress them on a quadratic polynomial of the distance to the boundary and an indicator for being on the right side of the boundary, again for each boundary separately. The predicted values of this regressions are then the values for $p(s)$ used in the model estimation. Results are qualitatively similar but noisier if we use real house prices instead of residualized smooth prices instead.

Restricted MLE Estimator. Define $y_g(s)$ as an indicator for the household at location $s$ being in group $g$, and assume $\mu_{g}(s)$ is a polynomial with a vector of coefficients $\phi_{g}$. The log likelihood of the parameter $\theta=\left\{ \Delta\psi_{g},\phi_{g}\right\} _{g\in\mathcal{G}}$ can then be written as: \[ \mathcal{L}(\theta)=\sum_{g=1}^{G}\int_{\mathcal{S}}y_{g}(s)\log f_{g}(s)ds \] with restriction that $\sigma_{g}=H(p(s),f_{g}(s))>0$ as explained in the next paragraph. In principle, it would be possible to include $\sigma_g$ for all $g$ in $\theta$ as part of the estimands (in the sense that $\sigma_g$ is identified from variation in prices, as in Brancaccio et al., 2020). But in practice, there are two issues with doing so. First, estimating $\sigma_g$ freely in this maximum likelihood almost always yields a negative estimate for $\sigma_g$ for at least one group, which would suggest that this group's demand curve for housing would slope up (recall that $\sigma_g$ is the negative inverse of the price semi-elasticity). Simply restricting the search domain such that it is positive often leads to implausibly large estimates of $\sigma_g$ for at least one group. Second, Monte Carlo simulations show that when group membership $y_g(s)$ is measured with noise, estimates for $\sigma_g$ tend to be biased.

Hence, we restrict $\sigma_g$ by making the assumption that the variation in $\mu_g(s)$ is small relative to the variation in $p(s)$. Using only variation from within local governments such that $\psi_g(s)$ is constant, we can write \[ \sigma_g^2 = \frac{\text{Var}(p(s))}{\text{Var}(\log f_g(s))}, \] where we estimate $f_g(s)$ using the posterior probability that the household at $s$ belongs to group $g$ and bounding the probability from below at $10^{-5}$. The variances are taken over all $s$ within local governments by subtracting the means of $p(s)$ and $\log f_g(s)$ on either side of the boundary. This produces magnitudes for $\sigma_g$ as shown in Appendix Table A5 that are economically plausible.

Conclusion

This paper provides precise estimates of house price and racial sorting discontinuities at local government boundaries, comparing only houses on the same street. We show that there is substantial heterogeneity in the implied preferences for local governments, both across racial groups and types of governments. We briefly discuss implications for efficiency and policy as well as areas for future research.

A fundamental debate in local public finance since its inception as a field is the question whether public goods can be efficiently provided. (Samuelson, 1954) worried that the externalities embedded in public goods would lead to freeriding and underprovision, whereas (Tiebout, 1956) suggested that household sorting across local governments would restore efficiency. However, the pervasive existence of premiums to live in one local government over another goes against the notion of efficient public goods provision (Brueckner, 1982). Furthermore, if peer characteristics play an important role in residential choice, then efficient Tiebout sorting is unlikely, justifying the intergovernmental transfers to local governments that benefit less from such peer valuation.

The findings in this paper also highlight the potential benefits and tradeoffs of designing place-based policy to target local governments as opposed to neighborhoods. For instance, our estimates suggest Black households seem to struggle accessing high-quality neighborhoods, while Hispanic households seem to have less opportunities to access better school districts and cities. Policy may target incentives or support along these dimensions.

Finally, an important area of future research suggested by the findings of this paper is the role of non-excludable local public goods. Much of what local governments do could have substantial spillovers on adjacent neighborhoods and local governments. Quantifying the share of non-excludable public goods in household valuation makes for an interesting problem for future research to tackle.

Data Preparation

Data preparation involves four steps: the raw assessor records from ATTOM, the boundary data from various sources as described in the main text, the spatial match of ATTOM and boundary data, and addressing noisy boundaries. We describe each of these steps in turn.

Preparation of Raw Data from ATTOM

We proceed state by state. We keep only properties categorized as single-family residences (codes “R” and “VRES” in ATTOM). We keep only properties whose sales value is between \$5,000 and \$10,000,000 and sold between 1990 and 2017. We then winsorize sales prices at the 1st and 99th percentiles by year of sale and drop any properties without a sales price. Residential addresses have been geocoded by the Fisher Center for Real Estate & Urban Economics at UC Berkeley.

Turning to housing characteristics, we drop properties without year-built information. We replace year built by “effective” year-built whenever year built is missing or outside the 1950-2017 range. The building age is then the difference between the year of assessment and the year built, and we drop properties with negative age. We keep only properties whose lot size in square feet is between 100 and 1,000,000 and again winsorize at the 1st and 99th percentiles, dropping properties with no lot size information. We proceed the same for interior square feet, again winsorizing at the 1st and 99th percentiles after dropping properties outside the 100-1,000,000 range. We only keep properties with at least 0.25 and at most 20 bathrooms; at most 50 rooms; and at least 1 and at most 5 stories.

Race/Ethnicity Classification of Buyers

To classify buyers by race/ethnicity, we proceed along the lines of (Diamond et al., 2019), although we use only 2010 Census genealogy information without the supplementary information from NamePrism. Their procedure builds in turn on a methodology developed by (CFPB, 2014).

In Step 1, using the data on the frequency of each surname with at least 100 counts in the Census and self-reported race/ethnicity, we first compute each surname's share of respondents in the six race/ethnicity categories (White, Black, American Indian and Alaskan Native, Asian/Pacific Islander, two-or-more races, and Hispanic). We impute missing shares by assigning nationwide means per race so that shares add up to one for each surname. We then keep only the four main categories of interest (White, Black, Asian, and Hispanic). We then merge surname race/ethnicty shares to the surnames in ATTOM, either to the primary entry of buyer surname, or, if there is no match, to the secondary buyer.

In Step 2, we compute a posterior probability of the race/ethnicity of the buyer by using Bayes' rule and data on the distribution of the four race/ethnicity categories across Census blocks. Concretely, we compute: \[ \Pr \left( r |g,s\right) = \frac{\Pr \left( r|s\right) \Pr \left( g|r\right)}{\sum_{r'\in R} \Pr \left(r'|s\right) \Pr \left(g | r'\right)}, \] where $\Pr \left( r|s\right)$ is the probability that a buyer is of race/ethnicity $r$ if their surname is $s$, and $\Pr \left( g|r\right)$ is the probability of living in Census block $g$ if their race/ethnicity is $r$. $R$ denotes the four race/ethnicity categories. In this way, we update probabilities to reflect the geographic distribution of race/ethnicity categories across Census blocks. As in (Diamond et al., 2019), we then assign a race/ethnicity category if its posterior probability is above 80%.

Preparation of Boundary Data

As described in the main text, we use 2010 TIGER/Line place boundaries as a basis of city boundaries, and NCES EDGE school district boundaries for school districts. For place boundaries, we restrict to the class “C” (cities). Because not all areas of the U.S. belong to a city, we use 2010 TIGER/Line county boundaries to assign territories without cities to their corresponding county as the primary entity providing local services. For school districts, we use both elementary school districts and unified school districts (which together cover the United States). We keep only boundaries that fall within the boundaries of a 2010 Core-Based Statistical Area (CBSA), which excludes boundaries in rural parts of the U.S. where boundaries are unlikely to have sufficient density of housing nearby.

We then create fragments of unique city-district pairs by finding the intersection of each city territory with each school district territory in each state, keeping all non-empty intersections. Territories not included in this set of intersections are residual district territories, which are subsequently added to the data, and for which we assign the county as the corresponding “city”.

Dealing with Boundary Noise

TIGER/Line boundary data are known to be unsuitable for high-precision measurement applications such as engineering problems or property transfers (Bureau, 2016). As can be seen in the Northeast of Figure 2, this can result in inaccurate assignment of properties to each boundary side for properties that are very close to the boundary.16 We interpret these caveats by the Census Geography division as limited precision at scales below 30 feet (10 meters).

To deal with this kind of boundary noise, we drop properties whose centroids are very close to the boundary, where “very close” is defined as having a parcel size and a centroid distance to the nearest boundary such that the parcel centroid is less than half of a lot width from boundary, assuming that a parcel's lot width is twice as large as its lot length. The logic is that if the centroid were any closer than that, the boundary would intersect with the lot, and so there would be no clear assignment to either side of the boundary. This procedure identifies 0.7% of properties (10,356 out of 1,481,681) that are too close to a boundary to be reliably assigned to one side and thus subsequently dropped.

Details on Empirical Framework

This appendix discusses how we use the dataset of houses near boundaries to identify and estimate the valuation of non-excludable local public goods provided by cities and school districts. We begin with the simple case of a single boundary; we then describe how to stack those boundaries to estimate a discontinuity in house prices; and finally, we present the two-way fixed effects model that we use to separately estimate neighborhood and local government fixed effects.

One Boundary

Consider a single neighborhood indexed by the distance $s\in\left[-0.5,0.5\right]$ in kilometers from a boundary located at $s=0$. The boundary may either coincide with a residential street or adjacent parcels, ensuring there is some continuity in neighborhood character across the boundary. Without loss of generality, we use the index $j=0$ for the local government on the left of the boundary with $s<0$, and $j=1$ for the one on the right. Local governments provide a bundle of public goods, some of which are excludable. We denote by $\psi_j$ the average valuation of excludable public goods provided by local government $j$ and we normalize the valuation of the local government on the left by setting $\psi_0 = 0$. Hence, $\psi_1$ is the premium the average household is willing to pay to get access to the excludable public goods provided by $j=1$ rather than $j=0$.

Individual houses $i = 1,...,I$ have a distance $S_{i}\in \left[-0.5,0.5\right]$ to the boundary. We assume that a government has a constant and additively separable effect of $\psi_{j}$ on house prices in the neighborhood, such that the potential home price $p_{i}(j)$ if $i$ were to receive excludable public goods by $j$ is given by \[ p_{i}(j)=\alpha + \mu\left(S_i \right)+\psi_{j}+\xi_{i} \] where $\alpha$ is the mean house price value in the neighborhood. $\mu(s)$ captures all amenities other than excludable public goods that affect house prices at $s$ in the same way, such as hilltop views, distance to downtown, or non-excludable public goods such as access to parks. We assume that $\mu(s)$ is continuous in $s$, capturing that any amenities other than access to the excludable bundle of public goods would be similar on opposite sides of the same residential street or for adjacent parcels. While we impose continuity, $\mu (s)$ does not have to be differentiable, allowing for an abrupt change in the slope of spatial trends of house prices near the boundary.

The term $\xi_{i}$ captures property-specific characteristics, such as lot size or the number of rooms, some of which may be unobserved. We assume that, conditional on observable characteristics, unobservable house characteristic do not change discontinuously at the boundary.

The assumption that local government valuation is additively separable from other amenities is not innocuous: for example, it excludes the possibility that the valuation of excludable public goods differs on opposite ends of the same school district. This assumption also does not allow match effects between neighborhood quality and government quality. We can test this assumption by comparing the additively separable model to a match model with unrestricted government-neighborhood interactions. The average valuation of being in $j=1$ as opposed to $j=0$ at some distance $s$ of the boundary can be expressed as a function of average potential house prices: \[ \psi_{1}=\mathbb{E}\left[p_{i}(1)-p_{i}(0)|S_{i}=s\right]. \] Under the stated assumptions, this parameter is locally identified at the boundary akin to standard regression discontinuity designs (Lee and Lemieux, 2010). Let $D_{i}=\mathbf{1}\left[S_{i}\geq0\right]$ be a dummy for being in $j=1$ as opposed to $j=0$. The observed house price is given by $p_{i}=D_{i}p_{i}(1)+\left(1-D_{i}\right)p_{i}(0)$. Under the assumption that $\mathbb{E}\left[p_{i}(j)|S_{i}=s\right]$ is continuous around zero we can identify the LATE of being governed by $j=1$ at the boundary: \[ \psi_{1}=\lim_{s\downarrow0}\mathbb{E}\left[p_{i}|S_{i}=s\right]-\lim_{s\uparrow0}\mathbb{E}\left[p_{i}|S_{i}=s\right]. \] The key identification assumption is that assignment to local governments is by law discontinuous, while other neighborhood amenities and non-excludable public goods are continuous. One threat to this assumption is that households sort along unobservable characteristics across the boundary, and that households may have different valuations that are correlated with the unobservable characteristics. This threat may be less relevant in our case, for a number of reasons. First, since we are comparing houses on the same street, it is unlikely that households value neighborhoods conditional on which side of the boundary they are on. Second, using surnames of buyers as a way to classify race and ethnicity as proposed by (Diamond et al., 2019), we can directly control for one important dimension of heterogeneity. Third, in the work by (Bayer et al., 2007), which explicitly accounts for sorting, mean valuation of school quality is not dramatically different than in standard hedonic regressions with a similar set of fixed effects.

Many Stacked Boundaries

Instead of estimating boundary discontinuities separately for individual pairs of local governments, we begin by stacking all boundaries in our data on top of one another after orienting them such that the side with the lower average price is on the left and the side with the higher average price on the right. Consequently, while house prices increase mechanically as we move from the left to the right, this setup allows us to estimate the extent to which house prices are on average indeed discontinuous at a local government boundary, and how much of the increase is due to continuous change in neighborhood quality. Concretely, we run

\begin{equation} p_{in} = \alpha_n + \psi_{\text{high}}D_{in}+g(S_{in})+X_{in}'\gamma+\epsilon_{in}, \end{equation}

where houses $i=1,...,I_n$ are nested in $n = 1,...,N$ neighborhoods. $p_{in}$ is the house price at the time of last transaction. $\alpha_n$ is a neighborhood fixed effect, for which we use street fixed effects as well as boundary points placed every 500 meters along each boundary. We also include cluster-by-year-of-transaction fixed effects throughout to account for different house price trends across metro areas. $\psi_{\text{high}}$ is the size of the average discontinuity in house prices at the boundary going from the lower to the higher average-price side, which is indicated by $D_{in} = \mathbf{1}[S_{in} \geq 0]$. We allow substantial flexibility for the shape of $g(s)$, using both local linear regressions as well as quartic polynomials. $X_{in}$ captures observable characteristics of the house and the buyer, including the house age, lot size, interior square feet, and fixed effects for the number of rooms, the number of bathrooms, and the number of stories. We also include fixed effects for the likely race or ethnicity of the buyer as proxied by surname.

Spatial Network of Connected Boundaries

Stacking boundaries ordered by average price allows us to detect a discontinuity, provided that the discontinuity is in the direction of the average price change. However, a lot of potentially useful information is lost by organizing the data in this way. Specifically, as discussed in Section ?, the sample of neighborhoods form a spatial network of local governments connected by mutual boundaries. It turns out that this data structure can be organized along the same lines as two-way fixed effects models estimating unobserved worker and firm fixed effects in labor economics [henceforth AKM]Abowd_etal_1999, whereby we use neighborhoods “moving” across local governments over space instead of workers moving across firms over time. As we now demonstrate, the conditions for separate identification of unobserved neighborhood and local government effects are identical.

Identification. Consider again many neighborhoods $n=1,...,N$, each of which cuts across exactly two of $J+1$ possible local governments. We assume for now that every local government shares a boundary with at least one other one. Under this assumption, the difference between any pair of local governments is identified, including non-adjacent pairs. To see this, consider two neighborhoods $n=1,2$ and three local governments, $A$, $B$, and $C$, with the first neighborhood cutting across the $A$-$B$ boundary and the second across $B$-$C$. Note that $A$ and $C$ do not border each other. However, letting property $i$ in neighborhood $n$ be at distance $S_{in}$ from the boundary, we can identify the relative premium of $A$ over $C$ by computing

\begin{align*} \psi_{A}-\psi_{C} & =\left(\psi_{A}-\psi_{B}\right)+\left(\psi_{B}-\psi_{C}\right)\\ & =\lim_{s\downarrow0}\mathbb{E}\left[p_{i1}|S_{i1}=s\right]-\lim_{s\uparrow0}\mathbb{E}\left[p_{i1}|S_{i1}=s\right]+\lim_{s\downarrow0}\mathbb{E}\left[p_{i2}|S_{i2}=s\right]-\lim_{s\uparrow0}\mathbb{E}\left[p_{i2}|S_{i2}=s\right]. \end{align*}

Identification of pairwise differences between $J+1$ local government effects is equivalent to identifying $J$ local government effects after a normalization such as $\psi_{0}=0$, whereby we need to normalize one LG effect per metro cluster. Hence, each $\psi_{j}$ captures the premium relative to the baseline local government. As will become clear now, the identification argument is the same as in AKM.

Let the mapping $j\left(\cdot,\cdot\right):\left\{ 1,...,\max_n I_n\right\} \times\left\{1,...,N\right\}\rightarrow\left\{ 0,...,J\right\} $ allocate any house $i$ in neighborhood $n$ to one of $J+1$ local governments. Just like in the single-boundary case, we can then identify the difference between two adjacent local governments at the boundary limit by comparing two houses $i$ and $i'$ in the same neighborhood on opposite sides of a boundary: \[ \psi_{j(i,n)}-\psi_{j(i',n)}=\lim_{s\downarrow0}\mathbb{E}\left[p_{in}|S_{in}=s\right]-\lim_{s\uparrow0}\mathbb{E}\left[p_{i'n}|S_{i'n}=s\right]. \]

We can implement this strategy empirically through the regression

\begin{equation} p_{in}=\alpha_{n}+\psi_{j(i,n)}+f\left(S_{in}\right)+X_{in}'\gamma+\varepsilon_{in} \end{equation}

where we have now written local government valuation as fixed effects. For a given set of assignments $D_{in}(j) = \mathbf{1}[j(i,n) = j]$ of properties to local governments, it holds that $\psi_{j(i,n)}=\sum_{j=1}^{J}\psi_{j}D_{in}(j)$. We gain from this formulation the insight that this is in fact a two-way fixed effects model along the lines of AKM.

The key mean independence assumption to identify $\alpha_n$ and $\psi_j$ is then: \[ \mathbb{E}\left[\varepsilon_{in}|X_{11},...,X_{IN},\alpha_{1},...,\alpha_{N},\psi_{1},...,\psi_{J},j(1,1), ...,j(I,N)\right]=0. \] Assuming that errors are conditionally mean independent rules out that, conditional on house and buyer observables as well as neighborhood and local government fixed effects, house prices gradually increase as opposed to jump as we cross a boundary.17 The assumption is supported by the evidence that there are indeed such discontinuities as shown in the stacked design in equation (6), as long as there are no unobservables that change discontinuously as we cross the boundary. We allow errors to be correlated within neighborhoods by clustering standard errors by neighborhood.

Variance decomposition. Having shown how to separately identify neighborhood and local government effects, we can then decompose the variance in log house prices into

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \text{Var}\left(p_{in}\right) & =\text{Var}\left(\alpha_{n}\right)+\text{Var}\left(\psi_{j(i,n)}\right)+\text{Var}\left(X_{in}'\gamma\right)+\text{Var}\left(\varepsilon_{in}\right)\\ & \quad+2\text{Cov}\left(\alpha_{n},\psi_{j(i,n)}\right)+2 \text{Cov}\left(\psi_{j(i,n)},X_{in}'\gamma\right)+2 \text{Cov}\left(\alpha_{n},X_{in}'\gamma\right) \end{aligned} \end{equation}

where we are particularly interested in the contributions of the neighborhood component $\text{Var}\left(\alpha_{n}\right)$ and the local government component $\text{Var}\left(\psi_{j(i,n)}\right)$. These components tell us how much of the variation in house prices is explained through differences in neighborhood valuation as well as differences in local government valuation. In addition, $\text{Cov}\left(\alpha_{n},\psi_{j(i,n)}\right)$ indicates the extent to which home buyers seem to treat neighborhoods and local governments as complements or substitutes, depending on whether it is positive or negative.

One complication specific to our setting is that, unlike in typical administrative worker-firm datasets, there is no single connected set that makes up the bulk of the observations, but rather a large number of fairly small unconnected sets of metro clusters. We proceed in two ways to address this. First, we estimate the model on each metro cluster separately. Second, we residualize house prices with respect to metro clusters and set each of the largest fragments in terms of city population to be the reference local government.18

Estimates of variance components in AKM are known to suffer from bias due to a small number of connections of firms by moving workers (Andrews et al., 2008). The same phenomenon is likely to be an issue here, as a small number of neighborhoods may connect each adjacent pair of local governments. We address this issue by using leave-one-out corrected estimates of variance components developed by (Kline et al., 2020).

Linear projection of estimated local government effects. We can also investigate how the estimated local government effects correlate with observed characteristics of corresponding cities and school districts. While these estimates are not causal, they demonstrate what aspects of local governments are valued in equilibrium. We study this by running \[ \widehat{\psi}_{j}=\delta_{h(j)} + Z_{j}'\beta+r_{j}, \] where $Z_j$ is a vector of observable city and school district characteristics and $r_j$ is an orthogonal error term by construction.

Discontinuity in Other Local Government Characteristics

In addition to different bundles of public goods, local governments also offer different property tax rates. Hence, to account for the difference in housing cost beyond the transaction price on either side of a boundary, we also have to account for differences in annual property tax payments. The complete household willingness to pay to live in a local government is then the sales price plus the present-discounted value of property tax payments.

Appendix Figure A3 present discontinuities in property taxes paid in 2016 across the stacked boundaries of each type, controlling for the same set of observables and fixed effects as before. On average, these discontinuities are about \$28-\$84. Unsurprisingly, the patterns of average property taxes as a function of distance to the boundary are very similar to the patterns of house prices, as we would expect if all houses were assessed at a similar rate. The implied property tax rate would be just over 1%.19 This is slightly lower than the average rate of around 1.5% (Harris et al., 2013), which suggests that property tax rates are somewhat lower on the high-price side. Assuming households discount all future property tax payments at a rate of 0.95, the implied willingness to pay to live on the high-price side rises by \$560, for a total of about \$2,900, or 14% of the difference across the two sides of the boundary.20

Other differences in local government characteristics. Appendix Table A3 summarizes how cities and districts on the low and high house-price side of a boundary differ besides taxes. Despite higher prices, city revenue and expenditures per capita are similar on either side. This is primarily due to higher intergovernmental transfers to the low-price side as well as lower property tax rates on the high-price side. Perhaps surprisingly, public employment per capita is almost 10% higher on the low-price side across most government functions including police and administration. They also better paid: public employees on the low-price side make about 6% higher monthly salaries. Turning to demographics, low-price cities are on average substantially larger, with households that have much lower income and are less likely to be White or Asian, as opposed to Black or Hispanic. Their police departments also have lower crime clearance rates than their high-price side neighbors, although there is no difference on average in how restrictive land use regulation is.

Model Derivations

Derivation of Equation (eq:density ). The group density $f_g(s)$ can be derived as follows. Partition $\mathcal{S}$ into $T$ equally sized bins $b_t$, where $h$ is the width of each bin and $t=1,...,T$ such that $hT = 1$. Define $U_{g,t}$ as the average utility for $g$ in $b_t$ with $U_{g,t} \rightarrow U_g(s)$ as $h \rightarrow 0$ and $T \rightarrow \infty$, and define $V_{g,t}$ similarly. We can then derive the probability $P_{g,t} = \Pr(U_{g,t} > U_{g,t'})$ using standard results from discrete choice logit [p. 85]Train_2009 as \[ P_{g,t} = \frac{\exp(V_{g,t}/\sigma_g)}{\sum_{t'=1}^T \exp(V_{g,t'}/\sigma_g)}. \] Let $F_g(s)$ be the cumulative density function of $P_{g,t}$ up to bins that are no greater than $s$, and let $f_g(s)$ be the corresponding probability density function, whenever defined. Then, as $h \rightarrow 0$ and $T \rightarrow \infty$, we have that $P_{g,t} \overset{p}{\rightarrow} f_g(s)$ using the continuous mapping theorem and the definition of the Riemann integral.

Illustration in Figure fig:model_illustration . For the illustration, we set the following parameter values: group sizes are $m_1 = 0.5$, $m_2 = 0.3$, and $m_3 = 0.2$. Letting $\psi_{g,j}$ for $j\in \{L,R\}$ be group $g$'s valuation of the government to the left or the right of the boundary and $\psi_g = \{\psi_{g,L}, \psi_{g,R}\}$, respectively, we set $\psi_1 = \{0.2, 0.6\}$, $\psi_2 = \{0.8, 1.6\}$, and $\psi_3 = \{-0.3, -0.6\}$. Amenity valuations are given by $\mu_g(s) = \phi_{g,0} + \phi_{g,1} s + \phi_{g,2} s^2$, and letting $\phi_g = \{\phi_{g,0}, \phi_{g,1}, \phi_{g,2}\}$, we set $\phi_1 = \{0, 1.4, 0.9\}$, $\phi_2 = \{0.6, -0.3, -0.3\}$, and $\phi_3 = \{-0.5, 0.3, -0.8\}$. Note that the levels in $\phi_g$ and the constants in $\mu_g(s)$ do not affect equilibrium prices or sorting (but they help to make the visualization clearer).

We generate these preferences on a discretized version of $\mathcal{S}$ with a partition into small bins. We then numerically find the equilibrium price $p^* = p(s)$ for all $s$ such that equilibrium conditions hold by minimizing the discretized version of \[ p^* = \arg\min_{p(s):s\in\mathcal{S}} \int_{s\in \mathcal{S}} \left(m_g f_g(s) - 1\right)^2 ds, \] which minimizes the market clearing condition in all locations. Evaluating $f_g(s)$ at $p^*$ yields the equilibrium group densities.

Estimation

Residualize and smooth prices. Since the model assumes that all location amenities other than local governments are smooth at the boundary, we residualize prices with respect to observable characteristics, in a process similar in spirit to (Rossi‐Hansberg et al., 2010). Concretely, we take real house prices values in 2010 dollars $\tilde{p}(s)$ and regress them on a vector $\mathbf{X}(s)$ consisting of boundary points, year of sale, and the same hedonic controls as those used in previous sections (lot size, indoor square footage, age, and fixed effects for the number of rooms, the number of bathrooms, and the number of stories), separately for each boundary.

Next, we take the residuals of this regression and regress them on a quadratic polynomial of the distance to the boundary and an indicator for being on the right side of the boundary, again for each boundary separately. The predicted values of this regressions are then the values for $p(s)$ used in the model estimation. Results are qualitatively similar but noisier if we use real house prices instead of residualized smooth prices instead.

Restricted MLE estimator. Define $y_g(s)$ as an indicator for the household at location $s$ being in group $g$, and assume $\mu_{g}(s)$ is a polynomial with a vector of coefficients $\phi_{g}$. The log likelihood of the parameter $\theta=\left\{ \Delta\psi_{g},\phi_{g}\right\} _{g\in\mathcal{G}}$ can then be written as: \[ \mathcal{L}(\theta)=\sum_{g=1}^{G}\int_{\mathcal{S}}y_{g}(s)\log f_{g}(s)ds \] with restriction that $\sigma_{g}=H(p(s),f_{g}(s))>0$ as explained in the next paragraph. In principle, it would be possible to include $\sigma_g$ for all $g$ in $\theta$ as part of the estimands (in the sense that $\sigma_g$ is identified from variation in prices, as in Brancaccio et al., 2020). But in practice, there are two issues with doing so. First, estimating $\sigma_g$ freely in this maximum likelihood almost always yields a negative estimate for $\sigma_g$ for at least one group, which would suggest that this group's demand curve for housing would slope up (recall that $\sigma_g$ is the negative inverse of the price semi-elasticity). Simply restricting the search domain such that it is positive often leads to implausibly large estimates of $\sigma_g$ for at least one group. Second, Monte Carlo simulations show that when group membership $y_g(s)$ is measured with noise, estimates for $\sigma_g$ tend to be biased.

Hence, we restrict $\sigma_g$ by making the assumption that the variation in $\mu_g(s)$ is small relative to the variation in $p(s)$. Using only variation from within local governments such that $\psi_g(s)$ is constant, we can write \[ \sigma_g^2 = \frac{\text{Var}(p(s))}{\text{Var}(\log f_g(s))}, \] where we estimate $f_g(s)$ using the posterior probability that the household at $s$ belongs to group $g$ and bounding the probability from below at $10^{-5}$. The variances are taken over all $s$ within local governments by subtracting the means of $p(s)$ and $\log f_g(s)$ on either side of the boundary. This produces magnitudes for $\sigma_g$ as shown in Appendix Table A5 that are economically plausible.

We estimate $\theta$ for each boundary $n=1,...,N$ separately, with the restriction that there are at least two groups with more than two households on either side of the boundary. This yields an estimate of $\Delta\psi_{g}$ for at least two groups at each $n$ that satisfies this minimum household requirement, with a total of up to $N$ estimates for each group. We then restrict results to estimates of $\Delta\psi_{g}$ that (a) are below $1M and (b) have a finite estimated standard error.
