David Schönholzer

Abstract

Local governments provide many essential public goods and services, such as education, policing, and public infrastructure. Using a sample of 1.5 million homes near thousands of local government boundaries, we estimate the value of access to these governments, separately for cities and for school districts. House prices jump discontinuously even when comparing homes on the same residential street, with cities and school districts valued jointly rather than independently. A variance decomposition attributes 12% of house price variation to local governments and 19% to neighborhoods. An equilibrium sorting model reveals substantial heterogeneity in valuations across racial groups. JEL classification: H41, H73, R23, I22. \\ Keywords: Local governments, public goods, peer valuation, boundary RD.

Introduction

Local governments provide various essential services to households, such as education, public safety, and infrastructure. A rich history of research in economics attempts to estimate the value of these local public goods, going back to studies by (Oates, 1969), (Brueckner, 1979) and (Epple and Sieg, 1999), and for schools by (Card and Krueger, 1992), (Bogart and Cromwell, 1997), and (Black, 1999). How much households value these public goods has important implications for household sorting and the efficiency of public goods provision (Samuelson, 1954; Tiebout, 1956; Bewley, 1981; Hoxby, 1999), but compelling evidence on the valuation of publicly provided goods and the local governments that provide them remains elusive.

Estimating these valuations is challenging for at least two reasons. First, it is hard to distinguish between households valuing access to local governments and valuation of desirable neighborhoods. Household sorting plays an important role even on a relatively small geographic scale, which has put into question whether comparing house prices across local government boundaries can serve as a reliable way to estimate the valuation of local public goods. Indeed, these boundaries often exhibit discontinuities in both prices as well as characteristics of nearby residents (Bayer et al., 2007; Boustan, 2013; Gibbons et al., 2013). Second, even if one can isolate local government valuation from neighborhood quality, most existing approaches cannot disentangle what households are paying for when they cross a local government boundary. In the United States, two distinct layers of local government—cities and school districts—provide largely non-overlapping bundles of services, yet their boundaries frequently coincide, making it difficult to attribute house price discontinuities to one level of government or the other. Separately identifying the value of access to cities and school districts is crucial for understanding how public policy affects household sorting and for informing models in urban economics and local public finance.

This paper makes progress on these challenges by combining a large dataset of millions of individual property sales records with new methods to estimate preferences for thousands of local governments. We begin by implementing what [page 608]Bayer_etal_2007 call the “idealized approach” to valuing adjacent jurisdictions: to “compare the prices of houses on opposite sides of a neighborhood street” that serves as a boundary between adjacent jurisdictions. In this way, “any discontinuity would be almost completely attributable to differences in valuation” of jurisdictions on either side. Augmenting the boundary discontinuity approach with this across-the-street comparison allows us to credibly insulate estimates of local government valuation from the influence of neighborhoods.

To separately identify the value of cities and school districts, we exploit the fact that city and school district boundaries frequently do not coincide. In our boundary sample, we distinguish three types of boundaries: “city-only” boundaries, where the city changes but the school district is the same on both sides; “district-only” boundaries, where the school district changes but the city is the same; and “city-district” boundaries, where both layers of government change simultaneously. Comparing discontinuities across these three boundary types recovers the price of access to cities, to school districts, and to the combination of the two. We then demonstrate that these discontinuities can be used to decompose house prices into unobserved local government and neighborhood valuations in connected networks of adjacent local governments. Finally, to interpret price and racial sorting discontinuities jointly and to recover group-specific preferences, we develop a simple equilibrium sorting model and estimate its structural parameters.

These analyses yield several new insights into the valuation of local governments. First, we show that prices jump discontinuously at local government boundaries even when comparing houses on opposite sides of the same residential street. Ordering the two sides of each boundary into lower and higher-average-price sides using a hold-out sample, the price discontinuity between these two sides is about \$18,000, of which \$2,300 cannot be explained by housing or neighborhood characteristics. This suggests that about 13% of housing costs are due to access to local governments. Second, these discontinuities are especially large at boundaries where cities and school districts are aligned: the jump at city-district boundaries is more than twice as large as the sum of those at city-only and district-only boundaries. This points to a strong complementarity between municipal services—such as zoning and policing—and high-quality schools, and it is the first piece of direct evidence that the two overlapping layers of local government are separately valued by households.

Third, we disaggregate the price discontinuities of thousands of connected boundary neighborhoods and decompose them into a neighborhood component, a local government component, and their covariance. To this end, we adapt methods from labor economics typically used to decompose variation in wages into firm and worker components (Abowd et al., 1999; Kline et al., 2020), commonly referred to as AKM, but using data on houses across space instead of workers over time. Fixed effects for neighboring houses on either side of a boundary identify neighborhood effects, and fixed effects for local governments connected by neighborhoods identify local government effects. We find that local governments explain about 12% of the variation in house prices in the connected set, and neighborhoods about 19%, with the two components slightly negatively correlated. The contribution of local governments is driven almost entirely by boundaries where city and school district lines coincide, again highlighting that the two layers of local government are separately valued and complementary in equilibrium.

Finally, to recover preferences that are heterogeneous across groups, we develop an equilibrium sorting model of households choosing residential locations near a boundary. Alongside the price discontinuities, we observe modest but systematic discontinuities in racial composition—Asian households in particular are more likely to sort onto the higher-priced side of boundaries, while White, Black, and Hispanic households exhibit smaller boundary discontinuities. The model shows that group-specific valuation of one local government over another is identified as a weighted sum of the price discontinuity and a group-specific sorting discontinuity, with weights determined by the price elasticity of residential choice. We use this relationship to estimate group-specific preferences by maximum likelihood separately at each of the 1,744 boundaries for which we observe a sufficient sample of multiple groups, and we document substantial heterogeneity in preferences across racial groups.

It is worth highlighting an important limitation. The approach of this paper is suitable to estimate the value of the excludable portion of local public goods. But many public services provided by local governments may be non-excludable. For example, crime and pollution spill across boundaries, and local government policies to combat these problems may be of considerable value to households. In this sense, estimates provided here are a lower bound of the total valuation of local governments. However, the excludable portion of local public goods, including access to high-quality schools and police departments, is important in itself, and providing estimates of their value can be useful for theory and policy.

Links to existing literature. This paper builds on a long literature studying how local public goods and property taxes capitalize in house prices (Oates, 1969; Brueckner, 1979; Brueckner, 1982; Deller, 1990; Berglas, 1984), often using boundary discontinuities (Black, 1999; Bayer et al., 2007; Boustan, 2013; Gibbons et al., 2013; Turner et al., 2014). The contribution to this literature is threefold. First, due to the large sample of house sales available in this paper, it is the first to use the across-the-street comparison at scale to study how differences in public goods are capitalized into housing prices, holding other components of neighborhood quality fixed. Second, the paper separately identifies the value of access to cities and to school districts—the two primary layers of local government in the United States—by exploiting the fact that city and school district boundaries often do not coincide. This allows us to document that the two layers are strongly complementary in equilibrium: more than twice as much of a discontinuous jump in prices occurs at boundaries where both cities and school districts change than at boundaries where only one of them changes. Third, the sorting model developed here identifies preferences for local governments even when households sort discontinuously at the boundary, extending the standard boundary discontinuity design to recover group-specific valuations.

A number of studies propose methods to capture unobserved neighborhood quality, amenities, and household sorting for valuation of local public goods (Gyourko and Tracy, 1991; Kahn, 1995; Bogart and Cromwell, 1997; Bayer et al., 2007; Caetano, 2019; Albouy et al., 2020; Laliberté, 2021). (Bogart and Cromwell, 1997) are the first to exploit the fact that city and school district boundaries do not always overlap to estimate school valuation separately from city valuation. The approach presented here is most similar to (Laliberté, 2021), who uses boundary discontinuities in student achievement across school attendance zones in Montreal combined with a student-mover design to separate neighborhood effects from school effects. In contrast, we show that connected sets of boundary discontinuities can be used directly to decompose neighborhood and local government valuation and to study how they correlate with observable characteristics. This methodology has wide applicability across many settings in local public finance, urban economics, development, and history.

Finally, this paper relates to a literature on household valuation of peers and sorting in theories of local public goods provision (Brueckner and Lee, 1989; de Bartolome, 1990; Benabou, 1993; Durlauf, 1996; Epple and Romano, 1998; Sethi and Somanathan, 2004; Calabrese et al., 2006; Bayer et al., 2022). The correlations of local government valuations with peer characteristics that we report are consistent with the view that peer composition is an important input into household residential choice (Rothstein, 2006; Abdulkadiroglu et al., 2020), though the correlational nature of our projections means we do not attempt to identify a causal role for peers separately from the bundled public services they co-consume.

Section 3 presents boundary discontinuity estimates using the across-the-street design, followed by Section 4 describing the house price decomposition into a local government and neighborhood component. Section 5 develops the equilibrium sorting model and uses it to estimate group-specific preferences. Section 6 concludes.

Setting and Data

Local Governments in the U.S.

The bulk of local public goods in the U.S. in terms of expenditures are provided by school districts and municipalities (henceforth “cities”). Unlike in most countries in the world, the vast majority of school districts and cities are governed separately in the United States, with some school districts providing services to multiple cities, such as the Los Angeles Unified district to 26 cities in the region; and sometimes a city is serviced by multiple school districts, such as Phoenix or San Jose, each having multiple elementary school districts offering education services.

The top panel of Figure 1 shows the spatial distribution of all 19,539 cities and 13,193 school districts in the United States. As we can see, the distribution differs markedly by region: In New England, there are often more school districts than cities, while in the South there are relatively more cities and generally a sparser distribution of local governments. Major metropolitan areas are often densely packed with dozens of local governments clustered around a central city.

Local governments provide a wide variety of services (see Figure A1). As shown in Appendix Table A1, both cities and school districts vary enormously in size, demographic characteristics, and per capita expenditures. School districts are primarily responsible for K-12 education provision to any household in their jurisdiction. Cities have a broad portfolio of responsibilities. Their largest expenditure item and their most important responsibility is public safety, including police and fire protection. On average, cities spend about \$300 per capita on police protection and \$100 per capita on fire protection. Local services include a number of other items too, such as street maintenance, utilities, parks and recreation, libraries, sewerage maintenance, and solid waste management.

Many of the public goods provided by school districts and cities are excludable to non-residents who do not live in the jurisdiction. While school districts sometimes have programs for non-residents to join (Bergman, 2018), residency in the district is usually required to attend a school in the district (Center, 2005). Similarly, access to services such as police, fire protection, and emergency medical services are tightly linked to residence as well: calls to 9-1-1 are routed to the jurisdiction associated with the address. Zoning, regulation, and other local ordinances are also sharply tied to residence, although they may exert local externalities in the neighborhood (Turner et al., 2014).

If a household highly values any of these excludable services, it is thus usually required to reside in the associated jurisdiction. The extent to which excludable public goods are important is then reflected in a sharp difference in capitalization of local governments at the boundary. In contrast, other local public goods and neighborhood amenities are not excludable. As a result, a household can still enjoy these amenities even when living nearby but outside of the jurisdiction, and they would be capitalized smoothly across local government boundaries.

Data

To separately identify valuations of local governments and their neighborhoods, we combine two primary datasets. First, we use fine-grained local government boundaries from the Census TIGER/Line database. Second, we combine these boundaries with a large sample of individual property assessment records from ATTOM. We supplement these data with standard records from the American Community Survey (ACS), from the Stanford Education Data Archive (SEDA), and Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR). We describe each of the primary datasets in turn.

Local government boundaries. Local governance in the U.S. consists of various entities including counties, school districts, incorporated towns and cities, townships, and special districts. We focus on school districts as well as incorporated towns and cities (jointly referred to as cities) as the two most important layers of local governance as they make up the bulk of local public employment, revenue and expenditures.

To measure the extent of these two types of jurisdictions, we use shapefiles from the Census's TIGER/Line place boundaries database. These consist of detailed boundaries for each place (i.e. cities and unincorporated Census Designated Places, CDPs), for a total of more than 19,000 cities.1 School district boundaries have been prepared by the NCES's EDGE school district boundaries program, which also draws on the TIGER/Line database. It combines elementary school districts and unified school district boundaries that jointly cover all of the U.S. with more than 13,000 districts.2

City-district pairs. We use these boundaries to create city-district pairs, which we simply call local governments. The territorial extent of such a local government is a subset of an overlapping city and district territory, and in case city and district boundaries do not coincide, the local government is a strict subset. This means that neighboring local governments are often in the same city but different school districts or vice versa. This is illustrated in Figure 2: the blue boundary in the Northwest separates the City of Cupertino in the North from the City of Saratoga in the South. The green boundary in the South divides the Cupertino Unified School District (CUSD) in the North from the Saratoga Unified School District (SUSD) in the South. Hence local government .5pt-1pt 1 consists of the Cupertino city-district pair; local government .5pt-1pt 2 of the City of Saratoga but the Cupertino school district; and local government .5pt-1pt 3 combines the Saratoga city-district pair.

ATTOM assessor records. ATTOM offers a comprehensive database of individual property assessment records drawing primarily on county assessor records and covering a total of 118 million properties over 2003-2016, with near-universal coverage of properties for a little more than half of all states. These records have been standardized and cleaned across jurisdictions, allowing us to focus on comparable properties across the country. We restrict our sample to single family residential properties since multi-unit residential properties and commercial properties are harder to compare. For each property, we have information on the sales price at the time of the most recent transaction, the most recent property tax payment, its geocoded location, age, lot size, indoor square footage, the number of rooms, the number of bathrooms, and the number of stories. We also know the last names of the owner of each property, which allows us to probabilistically infer their race and ethnicity using the Bayesian procedure in [][see Appendix ? for details]Diamond_etal_2019. To this end, we merge property records with census data assigning probabilities to last names according to race and ethnicity into five categories: White, Black, Asian, Hispanic, and Unknown. The latter category applies when no posterior probability of any race reaches 80%.

These steps result in a dataset of approximately 25 million single family homes across the 30 states shown in the bottom panel of Figure 1. As shown in Table 1, the average house price in 2010 dollars is about \$235,000 and has been last sold in 2007. Around two-thirds of buyers have White surnames, 9\% have Hispanic surnames, 5\% each have Black or Asian surnames, and for about 15\% of surnames there is no clear race/ethnicity. Houses have been built on average in 1977, have 2.25 bathrooms and 3.69 rooms in total. Neighborhood characteristics from the census show slightly higher shares of all racial/ethnic categories other than Asian, in part due to the 15\% of unknown buyer race/ethnicity. Average household income is \$66,900 in the neighborhood, with around 20% of household heads being college educated.

Boundary sample and Neighborhoods

Next, we link these 25 million houses to the nearest local government boundary, restricting the matching to properties within 500 meters (0.31 miles) of any boundary. We include only houses that are near boundaries with a sufficient number of houses on either side of the boundary. Specifically, we require at least 100 houses on each side of the boundary, with further restrictions on sufficient density of houses as detailed in Appendix ?. We define neighborhoods as 500-meter segments along these boundaries (the white diamonds in Figure 2) and associate each house with the nearest one of these neighborhoods. Neighborhoods with no properties in the immediate vicinity (100m), which may be due to a physical barrier running along the boundary, are dropped together with their associated houses. To ensure neighborhood continuity across boundaries, we drop boundary segments that cross a highway or other major roadway and keep only those that coincide with residential streets, as can be seen in the center of Figure 2. This means that neighborhoods running across major roadways such as 8 Mile Road in Detroit are excluded. This definition of neighborhoods guarantees that there is a substantial number of neighborhoods that cut across local government boundaries. If we were to use census blocks or tracts instead, we would have very little variation in local governments within census units.

This results in a boundary sample of 1.47 million properties, which are slightly more expensive and less likely to have been bought by a buyer with a White surname. Since there are many more houses near boundaries in dense urban areas, houses have on average substantially smaller lot sizes and are slightly older, although they have a higher average number of rooms. They are in neighborhoods that are less White, substantially more Hispanic and Asian, and have slightly higher than household income at around $71,100.

Clusters of connected local governments. As each local government may border several other local governments, they form metropolitan clusters in the form of a spatial network of connected local governments. These clusters arise entirely endogenously based on the availability of ATTOM property records across states as well as sufficient density of houses near boundaries. As shown in the bottom panel of Figure 1, they are similar to standard definitions of metropolitan areas such as Consolidated Metropolitan Statistical Areas (CMSAs) as defined by the census, but diverge in important ways. First, large CMSAs often have multiple clusters within them that are not connected. For example, there are two large clusters with more than 20 connected local governments each on Long Island. In total, there are 444 clusters with an average number of 4.43 local governments. The largest cluster is Los Angeles with 73 connected local governments.

As an example of a cluster, see Figure 3, the cluster around San Jose, CA (and the second-largest in the country). We can see that most of Silicon Valley is connected through residential neighborhoods as demonstrated in the Cupertino-Saratoga example in Figure 2, which can be found in the Southwest on the map in Figure 3. The red boundaries show connected local governments that are separated by stacked city-district boundaries, such as the boundary between Palo Alto and Los Altos in the West. Blue boundaries separate cities, with either side of the boundary belonging to the same school district; and green boundaries separate districts, with either side receiving services from the same city.

After connecting local governments through houses near boundaries, there are just over a thousand cities and school districts in the data, as shown in columns 4-6 in Table A1. These cities and school districts are substantially larger than the average city and school district across the country. They are also more diverse, have higher average household income, higher share of college-educated residents, and higher revenue and expenditures, as one would expect from local governments in high-density urban areas.

Discontinuities at Local Government Boundaries

We now estimate discontinuities in both prices and racial composition across local government boundaries holding neighborhood characteristics fixed, in line with the underlying assumption in the model. Hence, we narrow the comparison to similar houses bought by similar buyers located close-by on the same street but on opposite sides of a local government boundary.

Empirical Approach: Stacked Boundary Discontinuities

We begin by stacking all boundaries in our data on top of one another after orienting them such that the side with the lower average price is on the left and the side with the higher average price on the right. To avoid sorting boundary sides on the outcome of interest, we only use prices of houses in the 100 meter bin farthest from each boundary as a hold-out sample and subsequently estimate discontinuities on the stacked data from the remaining 400 meters closest to the boundary. Consequently, while house prices increase mechanically as we move from the left to the right, this setup allows us to estimate the extent to which house prices jump discontinuously at a local government boundary, in contrast to a gradual change in neighborhood quality and corresponding house prices. Concretely, we run

\begin{equation} p_{in} = \alpha_n + \psi D_{in}+g_n(S_{in})+X_{in}'\gamma+\epsilon_{in}, \end{equation}

where houses $i=1,...,I_n$ are nested in $n = 1,...,N$ neighborhoods. $S_{in}$ is the distance to the nearest boundary and $p_{in}$ is the house price at the time of last transaction. $\alpha_n$ are fixed effects for streets as well as neighborhoods. We also include cluster-by-year-of-transaction fixed effects throughout to account for different house price trends across metro areas. $g_n(\cdot)$ is a neighborhood-specific smooth function capturing local variation in neighborhood quality, and $X_{in}$ are housing characteristics, which include age, lot size, interior square feet, and fixed effects for the number of rooms, bathrooms and stories. The parameter of interest is $\psi$, the size of the average discontinuity in house prices at the boundary going from the lower to the higher average-price side, which is indicated by $D_{in} = \mathbf{1}[S_{in} \geq 0]$.

We show results for both nonparametric (Calonico et al., 2014) as well as quartic polynomial regression discontinuity estimates, to ensure results are robust to the estimation approach. All standard errors are clustered on the level of the boundary, allowing for arbitrary spatial correlation near the boundary.

Price Discontinuities

Table 1 presents a simple comparison of houses on the low and high average-price side of local government boundaries. Houses on the high side sell on average for \$284,900, which is \$20,600 higher than on the low side. Buyers on the high side are slightly more likely to be White or Asian, and slightly less likely to be Black or Hispanic. Houses on the high side are slightly newer and larger. Average neighborhood characteristics are also quite different, with households on the high side more likely to be non-minority, college educated, and high income. Since it is unclear how much of the simple price difference is due to gradually changing neighborhood characteristics or local government characteristics, we now go beyond this broad comparison using the methodology described in the previous subsection to zero in on the local discontinuity at the boundary.

Figure 4 shows nonparametric estimates of house price discontinuities according to equation (1). We use 2% sample bins along the distance to the boundary and local linear regressions with a 140m bandwidth (which is the MSE-minimizing bandwidth) and a rectangular kernel. In each of these estimates, we control for cluster-by-year fixed effects, neighborhood fixed effects, street fixed effects, race of buyer, housing-by-cluster characteristics, and a quadratic polynomial in absolute distance from the boundary. Thus, the comparison is between houses on the same street with buyers of the same race, living in observably similar houses bought in the same year, but in different local governments.

In the panel on the top left, we stack all boundaries, no matter whether they are city-only boundaries with the same district on either side; district-only boundaries with the same city on either side; or joint city-district boundaries. We can see that house prices on the low average-price side are around \$267,000 at a 400-meter distance to the boundary all the way to the boundary. Average house prices then jump to around \$269,000 as we cross to the high average-price side. This figure indicates that there are indeed small but significant price discontinuities at boundaries, even “within neighborhood”, meaning comparing observably similar properties sold in the same year on opposite sides of the same street. There is little evidence that valuation merely reflects gradually improving neighborhood quality towards the high average-price side.

We can also estimate these discontinuities separately for district-only and city-only boundaries (bottom left and top right of Figure 4). The discontinuity across district-only boundaries are about \$2,000 on average, while those for city-only boundaries show a small and typically insignificant discontinuity. Finally, the bottom right panel shows overlapping city-district boundaries only, which show a large discontinuity of around \$7,000 (from \$275,000 to \$282,000). Two features of these estimates stand out. First, city-only boundary discontinuities are small and insignificant in levels, and only marginally significant in logs (Appendix Table A2): on their own, city boundaries that do not coincide with a district change price in little additional value. Second, joint city-district boundaries show substantially larger jumps than district-only boundaries—in fact, city-district discontinuities are significantly larger than the sum of city-only and district-only discontinuities. Together, these patterns suggest that cities are valued primarily in combination with aligned school districts, rather than as independent bundles of services. Municipal services such as zoning, policing, and land-use enforcement appear to complement school quality—perhaps because the return to school quality is higher when public safety and zoning are also favorable—so that a boundary at which only the city changes produces a small jump, while a boundary at which both layers change produces a jump that exceeds the sum of its parts.

Discontinuities in Race/Ethnicity

The evidence provided so far suggests there are significant discontinuities in prices across boundaries, comparing houses on the same street bought by a household of the same race in the same year. We now investigate to what extent households of a different race/ethnicity sort across jurisdictions differently. Figure 5 shows the share of homebuyers of a certain race/ethnicity near a boundary using the same estimation approach as in Figure 4, again including neighborhood, cluster-by-year, and street fixed effects as well as property controls.

Surprisingly, there is little evidence of racial sorting across the boundary for White and Black households. The share of buyers with a White surname increases gradually from about 55% to 57% at the boundary, and then continues growing smoothly up to about 58% at 400 meters into the high average-price side. The share of Black households exhibits the exact reverse pattern: it drops smoothly from just under 6% to a little over 5% near the boundary, from where on it continues to drop smoothly to below 5%.5 In contrast, the share Hispanic and Asian households exhibit a substantial discontinuity at the boundary. The share of Asian buyers falls gradually from about 8.5% to 8% moving towards the low side of the boundary. At that point, it jumps discontinuously to more than 9% and continues growing to about 10%. Hispanic buyers exhibit the reverse pattern: Their share starts off at around 15%, gradually dropping to about 14% as we approach the boundary. It then drops and remains constant at just under 13%.

One concern is that Asian and Hispanic surnames are easier to identify than White and Black surnames, which may be driving the difference in discontinuities. But the slopes of White and Black shares are quite steep, suggesting that we are able to identify substantial sorting of White and Black households within local governments according to neighborhood quality. If differential noise were responsible the lack of jumps for White and Black shares, then identifying neighborhood sorting would be more difficult as well. To further address this concern, Appendix Figure A2 shows very similar estimates based on Census block data, which rely on self-reported race rather than race inferred from surnames.

White and Black households have precisely estimated near-zero coefficients ($-0.003$ and $-0.001$, respectively). In contrast, Asian households are 1 percentage point more likely to be on the high-price side, which is a 13% increase relative to the low-price side. Hispanic households exhibit a noisy 0.9 percentage point drop towards the high-price side, a 7% drop.

Discontinuity in Property Taxes

In addition to different bundles of public goods, local governments also offer different property tax rates. Hence, to account for the difference in housing cost beyond the transaction price on either side of a boundary, we also have to account for differences in annual property tax payments. The complete household willingness to pay to live in a local government is then the sales price plus the present-discounted value of property tax payments.

Appendix Figure A3 present discontinuities in property taxes paid in 2016 across the stacked boundaries of each type, controlling for the same set of observables and fixed effects as before. On average, these discontinuities are about \$28-\$84. Unsurprisingly, the patterns of average property taxes as a function of distance to the boundary are very similar to the patterns of house prices, as we would expect if all houses were assessed at a similar rate. The implied property tax rate would be just over 1%.6 This is slightly lower than the average rate of around 1.5% (Harris et al., 2013), which suggests that property tax rates are somewhat lower on the high-price side. Assuming households discount all future property tax payments at a rate of 0.95, the implied willingness to pay to live on the high-price side rises by \$560, for a total of about \$2,900, or 14% of the difference across the two sides of the boundary.7

Other differences in local government characteristics. Appendix Table A3 summarizes how cities and districts on the low and high house-price side of a boundary differ besides taxes. Despite higher prices, city revenue and expenditures per capita are similar on either side. This is primarily due to higher intergovernmental transfers to the low-price side as well as lower property tax rates on the high-price side. Perhaps surprisingly, public employment per capita is almost 10% higher on the low-price side across most government functions including police and administration. They also better paid: public employees on the low-price side make about 6% higher monthly salaries. Turning to demographics, low-price cities are on average substantially larger, with households that have much lower income and are less likely to be White or Asian, as opposed to Black or Hispanic. Their police departments also have lower crime clearance rates than their high-price side neighbors, although there is no difference on average in how restrictive land use regulation is.

Comparing Local Government and Neighborhood Valuation

Empirical Approach: Variance Decomposition

Stacking boundaries ordered by the average price in the 400-500m bins from the boundary allows us to detect a discontinuity, provided that the discontinuity is in the direction of the average price change. But this assumption may not be satisfied, and ordering boundaries in this way removes a lot of interesting heterogeneity across local governments. In particular, we are interested in how local government valuation compares to the valuation of neighborhoods, local public goods, and peers who co-reside in the local government.

We can avoid ordering boundaries by exploiting the fact that the sample of neighborhoods form a bipartite network of local governments in space connected by mutual boundaries, as shown in Figure 3. It turns out that this data structure can be organized along the same lines as two-way fixed effects models estimating unobserved worker and firm fixed effects in labor economics [henceforth AKM]Abowd_etal_1999, whereby we use neighborhoods “moving” across local governments through space instead of workers moving across firms over time. As we demonstrate in Appendix 11, the conditions for separate identification of unobserved neighborhood and local government effects are identical to AKM.

We can implement this strategy empirically through the regression

\begin{equation} p_{in}=\alpha_{n}+\psi_{j(i,n)}+g\left(S_{in}\right)+X_{in}'\gamma+\epsilon_{in} \end{equation}

where $\alpha_n$ is the average valuation of neighborhood $n$, and $\psi_{j(i,n)}$ is the average valuation for local government $j = j(i,n)$ associated with house $i$ in neighborhood $n$, depending on which side of the boundary house $i$ is located. $X_{in}$ are the same housing characteristics as in equation (1) as well as state-by-year fixed effects. $g(S_{in})$ are separate quartic polynomials on either side of the boundary. Identifying assumptions are discussed in detail in Appendix 11. This decomposition allows neighborhood and local government valuations to be correlated, but it assumes they are additively separable, which we can test by comparing this model to a saturated model (see Section ?).

We can then decompose the variance in log house prices into

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \text{Var}(p_{in}) & =\text{Var}(\alpha_{n})+\text{Var}(\psi_{j(i,n)})+\text{Var}(X_{in}'\gamma)+\text{Var}(\epsilon_{in})\\ & \quad+2\text{Cov}(\alpha_{n},\psi_{j(i,n)})+2 \text{Cov}(\psi_{j(i,n)},X_{in}'\gamma)+2 \text{Cov}(\alpha_{n},X_{in}'\gamma) \end{aligned} \end{equation}

where we are particularly interested in the contributions of the neighborhood component $\text{Var}\left(\alpha_{n}\right)$ and the local government component $\text{Var}(\psi_{j(i,n)})$. These components tell us how much of the variation in house prices is explained through differences in neighborhood and local government valuation. In addition, $\text{Cov}(\alpha_{n},\psi_{j(i,n)})$ indicates the extent to which homebuyers seem to treat neighborhoods and local governments as complements or substitutes, depending on whether it is positive or negative. Since plug-in estimates as in AKM or (Card et al., 2013) are known to be biased, we also present estimates using the leave-one-out estimator developed by [henceforth KSS]Kline_etal_2020, which estimates bias-corrected variance components with unrestricted heterogeneity.

In addition to estimating variance components using equation (3), we can also investigate how the estimated local government effects correlate with observed characteristics of corresponding cities and school districts. While these estimates are not causal, they demonstrate what aspects of local governments are valued in equilibrium. We study this by running \[ \widehat{\psi}_{j}=\delta_{h(j)} + Z_{j}'\beta+r_{j}, \] where $\delta_{h(j)}$ are city or district fixed effects, $Z_j$ is a vector of observable city and school district characteristics, and $r_j$ is an error term. Including city or district fixed effects restricts the comparison to cities in the same school district or vice versa.

The largest leave-one-out cluster is Los Angeles, with a total of 72 local governments connected by 73,961 houses in 510 boundary neighborhoods. The variance in log price is 0.517. We find that access to local governments explains about 11% of house price variation using the KSS estimate of $\text{Var}(\psi_j)$, which is 0.059. Neighborhoods explain only slightly more of the variance, about 13%, with an estimate of the covariance of 0.004.

Other clusters exhibit similar patterns of house price variance decompositions. The median large cluster (bottom row of Table 4) has a local government variance estimate of 0.036 relative to a log price variance of 0.4, suggesting that 9% of price variation is due to access to excludable local public goods. Neighborhoods explain a comparable share of about 10%. The covariance is small but negative, a result we find in most but not all clusters. We interpret the negative correlation we find in most clusters as evidence for households treating neighborhood quality and access to high-quality local governments as substitutes rather than complements: for instance, they trade off living in a good neighborhood but worse schools against a bad neighborhood with better schools. This can be true even if neighborhood quality and school quality are highly positively correlated.

A few clusters show patterns that diverge from the median. For example, the cluster around Boston, MA shows substantially larger estimates of the local government component than the median, making up almost a fifth of the house price variation in the cluster. These large estimates of $\text{Var}(\psi_j)$ are accompanied by large estimates for neighborhood variance $\text{Var}(\alpha_n)$ as well, and a strongly negative covariance between the two. Boston has a central city and school district that is thought of as having relatively low quality, while the connected suburbs have some of the highest-quality schools and city services in the country. This suggests that, conditional on some housing budget, households face a particularly stark tradeoff between neighborhood quality and excludable public goods quality in these clusters.

Overall, these variance component estimates confirm the important role of local governments for house prices documented in the price boundary discontinuities. The median estimate of 9% contribution of access to local governments is similar to the 13% estimate in the boundary discontinuity approach. Unobserved neighborhood characteristics make up a slightly larger portion of house price variation. In contrast to typical worker-firm settings, covariance estimates are negative on average even when bias-corrected with KSS. Given the robustness of this pattern across almost all clusters, it is unlikely to be driven by a bias specific to an individual cluster.

Connected Cluster Variance Decomposition

Estimating variance components by cluster allows us to document how much local government choice and neighborhood choice matters in individual metro clusters, but it is difficult to draw inferences about the country as a whole or for specific subsets of the data other than geographic proximity. To create connections between clusters, we first residualize house prices with respect to cluster fixed effects, so that variation is not driven by between-cluster differences in log house prices. We then normalize each cluster with respect to the largest city in the cluster, similar to using the establishments in the restaurant industry as the benchmark against which to compare other firms in a connected set in the worker-firm setting.

We begin by providing evidence for the validity of the additive model connected through the largest city per cluster as normalization by comparing it to a model with unrestricted neighborhood-government interactions in Appendix Table A4. The adjusted $R^2$ from the additive model after residualizing by cluster fixed effects is 0.80, while the $R^2$ from the match model is 0.81. This is a mere 1.3% improvement, indicating that the match model captures only marginally more variation in log house prices. Appendix Figure A4 shows mean residuals by cells defined as interactions of estimated neighborhood valuations and estimated local government valuations. Reassuringly, these mean residuals are small relative to the variation in log house prices (with variance 0.37), and there is no obvious pattern of positive or negative mean residuals.8

Projecting Valuation on Observables

We now study what characteristics of local governments are correlated with local government valuation. These correlations are not causal, but they are informative about which characteristics are associated with household valuation in equilibrium. We study local government characteristics that help us to distinguish between the direct valuation of public goods and the indirect valuation of local governments through peers. These characteristics are grouped into output, inputs, and peers. Peer characteristics are average household income and racial composition drawn from the American Community Survey, measured at the level of the local government whose valuation we are projecting—at the city level when projecting city valuation and at the school district level when projecting district valuation—rather than at the immediate neighborhood or boundary tract level.9 Input characteristics such as expenditures or public employment per capita speak to household sorting due to local public services. In contrast, peer characteristics such as average household income or racial shares point to the importance of who public services are shared with. Finally, output characteristics such as school achievement or crime clearance rates represent the idea that households value the quality of public services, which in itself may be a product both of government inputs and peers.

Estimating Group-Specific Preferences

The variance decompositions and correlations with observables in the previous section point to peers as important drivers of local government valuation. But two limitations of the AKM approach stand out. First, it relies on correlations that are difficult to interpret without a model of how prices and sorting are jointly determined. Second, households may have heterogeneous preferences for local governments, and the average valuations estimated via fixed effects cannot account for such differences. Hence, we now develop an equilibrium sorting model with heterogeneous households that allows us to estimate and interpret group-specific preferences.

Model

We develop a simple framework to make the distinction between local governments and neighborhoods precise, drawing on ideas from (Bilal and Rossi-Hansberg, 2021). On the supply side, we consider a residential neighborhood that straddles a local government boundary with smoothly varying neighborhood quality but a different bundle of public goods (and peers with whom to share them) on either side of the boundary. For simplicity, we assume that strict zoning regulation limits supply responses on either side. On the demand side, we are interested in identifying the valuation of both neighborhoods and local governments. Hence, we allow for households to sort endogenously both within and across each of the local governments on either side of the boundary. In equilibrium, patterns of household sorting and prices are pinned down by the combination of locational amenities (neighborhood and local government valuation) and household preferences.

Environment. Consider a single neighborhood with locations $s \in \mathcal{S} = [-0.5, 0.5]$ near a boundary at $s = 0$ separating two local governments. Each location has a fixed unit of uniform housing supply.10 Households are divided into groups $g\in \mathcal{G} = \{1,...,G\}$. Each group makes up a share $m_g$ in the neighborhood. We assume that each household makes a single residential choice according to the following indirect utility function: $U_g(s) = V_g(s) + \sigma_g \varepsilon_g(s)$. The systematic component of utility is given by \[ V_{g}(s)=\mu_{g}(s)+\psi_{g}(s)-p(s), \] where $\mu_g(s)$ captures group-specific average valuation of amenities that varies smoothly across the neighborhood; $\psi_g(s)$ is group-specific average valuation of local governments, which is constant within local governments but may jump at the boundary; and $p(s)$ is the equilibrium price for housing at $s$, which applies to households irrespective of their group membership. Households may value a local government $\psi_g(s)$ directly because of the local public goods it provides, or indirectly because of strategic complementarities of sharing the local government with a particular set of households residing there in equilibrium. The stochastic component of utility, $\sigma_g \varepsilon_g(s)$, consists of an identically and independently distributed type-I extreme value random variable $\varepsilon_g(s)$ and a dispersion parameter $\sigma_g$ that captures the relative weight of idiosyncratic drivers of residential choice not captured in neighborhood amenities, local governments, or prices.11

Equilibrium. An allocation of households across the neighborhood and a price for each location are a sorting equilibrium if the following two conditions hold. First, households choose locations such that they maximize their utility. Under utility maximization, the density of households of group $g$ across the neighborhood, denoted by $f_g(s)$, is

\begin{equation} f_{g}(s)=\frac{\exp\left(V_{g}(s)/\sigma_{g}\right)}{\int_{\mathcal{S}}\exp\left(V_{g}(\ell)/\sigma_{g}\right)d\ell}, \end{equation}

as shown in Appendix 13. The second condition for equilibrium is that the housing market clears in the neighborhood, which requires $\sum_{g\in \mathcal{G}}m_{g}f_{g}(s)=1$ at every location. The left-hand side is the demand for housing at a location across all groups, taking into account the size of each group, whereas the right-hand side is the unit supply of housing at each location. Intuitively, if demand exceeds supply at some location, then the price at this location has to rise to dissuade some households from choosing to live there.12

Figure 6 illustrates the relationship between preferences, prices, and residential choice in a sorting equilibrium. Three groups have preferences for amenities and local governments as shown in Panel A. In particular, households in Group 1 and 2 have a preference for the local government on the right of the boundary, whereas those in Group 3 have a preference for the one on the left. Panel B shows the corresponding equilibrium price schedule, which exhibits a discontinuity at the boundary. Finally, Panel C shows the equilibrium sorting behavior of the three groups. In line with their preferences, households in Group 2 and 3 sort discontinuously on the side of the boundary of their preferred local government, but interestingly, those in Group 1 sort on the left side even though they prefer the government on the right. The reason for this is that households in Group 2 have such a strong preference for the right government that many of those in Group 1 are not willing to pay the resulting market premium to live there.13

Identification and Estimation

The illustration in Figure 6 shows how preferences yield equilibrium prices and sorting behavior. But the econometrician solves the reverse problem: using observed prices and sorting behavior to infer preferences. To see how preferences are identified from observed prices and sorting patterns, define for any variable $x(s)$ its boundary discontinuity $\Delta x \equiv x(0) - \lim_{s\rightarrow 0^-} x(s)$. Then, we can express a group's preference for the government on the right of the boundary over the one on the left as

\begin{equation} \Delta \psi_g = \sigma_g \Delta \log f_g + \Delta p. \end{equation}

We can see that these preferences are identified as the weighted sum of the price discontinuity $\Delta p$, which applies to all groups in the same way, and the group-specific sorting discontinuity $\Delta \log f_g$, which captures a group's willingness to pay a premium above and beyond the market price.14 The weight on the sorting discontinuity is given by the dispersion parameter $\sigma_g$: if a group's preferences are more idiosyncratic (i.e. larger $\sigma_g$), then systematic sorting implies a larger willingness to pay to live in one government over another. In contrast to local government preferences, the dispersion parameter $\sigma_g$ is identified by variation in residential choices and prices away from the boundary.15

Example: Piedmont-Oakland. It is useful to see Equation (5) in action, using an example from our data. Piedmont, CA is an affluent city and school district in the San Francisco Bay Area whose residents are mostly White and Asian, whereas neighboring Oakland is much larger and more diverse, both in terms of household income and race. Figure 7 shows the price and sorting discontinuities near the boundary between the two local governments. The panel on the left shows that prices for similar housing jump by about \$172,000 at the boundary, corresponding to the $ p$ term in Equation (\ref{eq:disconts}). The right panel shows that White households sort discontinuously on the less expensive Oakland side, whereas Asian households are more likely to reside on the more expensive Piedmont side. Transforming these discontinuities in White and Asian shares at the boundary into densities and taking the difference of their logs yields sorting discontinuity estimates of $-0.154$ for $ f_White$ and $0.215$ for $ f_Asian$, respectively. Finally, price variation relative to variation in the (log) densities of the two groups yields estimates of \$123,000 for $\sigma_{\text{White}}$ and \$103,000 for $\sigma_Asian$.16

Putting these numbers together according to Equation (5), we get preference estimates of White households for Piedmont over Oakland of \$153,000 and for Asian households of \$194,000. Even though White households prefer Piedmont just like Asian households, the stronger preference of Asian households and the resulting higher price to live there dissuades some White households from residing in Piedmont, leading to a discontinuous drop of White households when crossing from Oakland into Piedmont.

We implement this procedure by residualizing house prices with respect to housing characteristics and year of transaction and estimating the model separately for each of the 1,744 boundaries in our dataset for which there are a sufficient number of households of at least two groups. We estimate local government preferences via maximum likelihood as shown in Appendix 13.

Conclusion

This paper provides precise estimates of house price and racial sorting discontinuities at local government boundaries, comparing only houses on the same street. We show that there is substantial heterogeneity in the implied preferences for local governments, both across racial groups and types of governments. We briefly discuss implications for efficiency and policy as well as areas for future research.

A fundamental debate in local public finance since its inception as a field is the question whether public goods can be efficiently provided. (Samuelson, 1954) worried that the externalities embedded in public goods would lead to freeriding and underprovision, whereas (Tiebout, 1956) suggested that household sorting across local governments would restore efficiency. However, the pervasive existence of premiums to live in one local government over another goes against the notion of efficient public goods provision (Brueckner, 1982). Furthermore, if peer characteristics play an important role in residential choice, then efficient Tiebout sorting is unlikely. (Brueckner and Lee, 1989) formalize this tension: when one group benefits from the presence of another, mixed communities can Pareto-dominate homogeneous Tiebout sorting, albeit at the cost of an efficiency loss from having to tailor public goods to average rather than individual tastes. Our findings suggest this tension is alive in the data, which in turn justifies intergovernmental transfers to local governments that benefit less from peer valuation.

The findings in this paper also highlight the potential benefits and tradeoffs of designing place-based policy to target local governments as opposed to neighborhoods. For instance, our estimates suggest Black households seem to struggle accessing high-quality neighborhoods, while Hispanic households seem to have less opportunities to access better school districts and cities. Policy may target incentives or support along these dimensions.

Finally, an important area of future research suggested by the findings of this paper is the role of non-excludable local public goods. Much of what local governments do could have substantial spillovers on adjacent neighborhoods and local governments. Quantifying the share of non-excludable public goods in household valuation makes for an interesting problem for future research to tackle.
