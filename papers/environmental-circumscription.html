Abstract

State capacity is associated with long-run development but the causes of early state formation remain debated. A prominent theory in anthropology suggests that states form due to environmental circumscription—high land productivity surrounded by an unproductive periphery. The classic, coercive version of the theory posits that circumscription accelerates organized conflict and inhibits out-migration, which fosters state formation. We propose an expanded theory that accommodates cooperative mechanisms: circumscription creates a need to coordinate public goods provision and regulate trade. We combine archaeological, historical, and environmental data to test circumscription theory and its underlying mechanisms across the globe. Circumscription is associated with early state formation and both cooperative and coercive mechanisms are at play. The pace of change after the arrival of farming is higher in circumscribed regions than in similar comparison regions. This suggests a synthesis of cooperative and coercive theories is best positioned to explain the historical roots of the state. \\ Keywords: state formation, long-run development, geography, archaeology. \\ JEL classification: N40, P16, N50, N90.

Introduction

Historical state capacity is increasingly recognized as an important precondition for long-run economic development (Besley and Persson, 2009; Michalopoulos and Papaioannou, 2013; Borcan et al., 2018; Dell et al., 2018). As a result, understanding the historical roots of the state—the extensive margin of state capacity—has become an active frontier of research in economics (Dal Bó et al., 2018; Sanchez de la Sierra, 2018; Lagerlöf, 2021; Mayshar et al., 2022; Allen et al., 2022; Mayoral and Olsson, 2022), building on a long tradition in the social sciences more broadly (Wright, 1977; Mann, 1986; Tilly, 1990; Olson, 1993). A central debate in this literature is whether cooperation or coercion is the central driving force of state formation. Government can serve to coordinate public goods provision (Allen et al., 2022), or to extract resources for emerging elites (Mayshar et al., 2022)—how important are these motives in early state formation across the globe?

We study this question in the context of a prominent anthropological theory of early state formation developed by (Carneiro, 1970): circumscription theory. The basic idea is that states form in areas with high agricultural productivity that are circumscribed by an unproductive periphery. In these areas, Carneiro observed, communities have poor outside options for emigration after losing a conflict with an opposing group in the area. Hence, they are willing to accept subjugation by the victorious group, paying tribute in exchange for being allowed to remain in the productive core of the area. While Carneiro saw his theory very much in the vein of coercive theories of state formation, circumscription could also foster cooperative mechanisms of state formation. Circumscribed areas may act as marketplaces, creating a demand for contract enforcement; or they may foster higher population density, increasing the need for public goods.

Circumscription theory has only been examined in qualitative studies or individual regions (Allen, 1997; Mayoral and Olsson, 2022). There are at least two challenges in studying circumscription theory more systematically: first, it requires data with global coverage of early state formation and circumscription; and second, it requires rich information on social and economic change to elucidate the mechanisms underlying the link between circumscription and state formation.

We overcome these challenges by building a new dataset that draws on various archaeological, historical, and environmental data. To measure early state formation, we combine a global archaeological atlas featuring thousands of sites (Bahn, 2000) with a historical database providing detailed information on social and economic change in 35 regions around the world (Turchin et al., 2015). Together, these two datasets illuminate the geographic, social, and economic context in which early state formation took place. We measure circumscription as the difference in agricultural productivity in a location and average productivity in the surrounding area. Agricultural productivity is measured using features of the environment that are slow-moving and unlikely to be strongly affected by human activity, such as seasonal temperature and soil types, allowing us to interpret circumscription as predetermined relative to state formation.

Our first contribution is to establish a strong relationship between circumscription and early state formation. This relationship is robust to various alternative factors that could explain this correlation, such as climate, topography, freshwater availability, ecological diversity, the timing of the Neolithic transition, and baseline population sizes. This evidence establishes that some features of circumscribed areas did indeed foster state formation.

Our second contribution is to investigate various mechanisms that could be driving the effect of circumscription on state formation. To this end, we investigate the sequence of change in early state formation areas relative to other areas that are similar in several ways but not circumscribed. Using this approach, we find that both cooperative and coercive mechanisms were likely at play in early state formation. Early states experienced a more rapid rise in trade and public goods than other areas, in line with cooperation. But we also observe signs of conflict emerging sooner in these circumscribed areas than others, in line with coercion. Persistent inequality seem to be primarily a consequence rather than a precursor of state formation, refining our understanding of how cooperation and coercion are at work in the state formation process.

The first part of the paper is devoted to the measurement of early state formation and circumscription, and to the estimation of their relationship. To measure early state formation around the globe, we rely on the consensus in much of the archaeological literature that there are only a few regions in which pristine states formed without the influence of existing states (Trigger, 2003). These regions are Mesoamerica, the Andes, Egypt, Mesopotamia, the Indus Valley, and China. In these regions, we then identify archaeological sites associated with state formation using the archaeological literature and the timing of the first appearance of bureaucrats or government buildings in our historical data.

Measuring circumscription requires fine-grained data on historical agricultural productivity around the globe. We rely on potential production capacity (PPC) estimates of seventeen crops from the Global Agro-Ecological Zones (GAEZ) database maintained by the FAO (Fischer et al., 2008), which we amend in two important ways. First, since the Green Revolution dramatically increased caloric yields of many crops (Gollin et al., 2021), we use historical estimates of crop calories (Chatfield, 1953) from before the Green Revolution. Second, we restrict crops to be only available either in the Americas or in Eurasia and Africa depending on their center of domestication, in line with a world before the Columbian Exchange (Nunn and Qian, 2010).

Having constructed global measures of early state formation and circumscription, we then show that early state regions are on average substantially more circumscribed than other regions. This holds on the “macro-scale” when comparing the regions of early state formation to the other areas in our historical databank of 35 regions, as well as in an expanded sample of 81 regions. It also holds on the “micro-scale” when comparing individual archaeological sites or all land cells to one another. The relationship is also robust to the inclusion of various controls that speak to alternative theories of state formation. The magnitude of the circumscription effect is large: a one standard-deviation increase in circumscription doubles the probability of early state formation.

In the second part of the paper we then seek to elucidate the mechanisms underlying the relationship between circumscription and state formation. We begin by investigating the sequence of change in early state formation areas. These sequences uncover remarkable evidence on the relative timing of social and economic phenomena. In particular, features typically understood as functions of the state, such as public goods provision, often appear before states arise. Conversely, social differentiation, such as through hereditary status, is often seen as a core element of state formation. But we find that this feature more often appeared only after state formation.

We then compare these trajectories in early state formation regions to those of comparable regions that are not circumscribed. To this end, we match each of the early state formation regions to a comparison region that is much less circumscribed but otherwise has similar characteristics. This leads to intuitively appealing comparisons such as the circumscribed Nile Valley to the non-circumscribed Mississippi valley. These comparisons uncover a substantial gap between heavily circumscribed and less circumscribed regions in the pace of change after the arrival of farming: circumscription accelerates the appearance of markets, irrigation, defensive settlements, and other phenomena, reflecting the wide array of mechanisms that may lead to early state formation, beyond the coercive mechanism originally proposed in Carneiro's work.

These findings have several important implications for the literature on early state formation. First, rather than resolving the debate on cooperation versus coercion in favor of one or the other side, the evidence presented here points towards a synthesis between these two forces. Cooperation to support irrigation was central in the most circumscribed regions such as Upper Egypt, Southern Mesopotamia, and the Kachi Plain—regions in which farming groups were particularly reliant on coordination to deal with the challenges of the arid environment. In contrast, coercion seems to have been a driving force in more mountainous regions like the Valley of Oaxaca, Cuzco, and the Middle Yellow River Valley, where signs of organized intergroup conflict appear much earlier than the state.

Second, our findings refine the mechanics of cooperative and coercive forces of early state formation. The traditional view of the cooperative mechanism is that states were created to enable public goods provision. But we show that areas whose geography had already stimulated public goods were more likely to give rise to states. States formalized and intensified existing forms of cooperation rather than being a precondition for them, as in (Allen et al., 2022). Our findings also refine our understanding of the coercive mechanism. Many theories stress the role of emerging elites seeking to extract resources from farming groups (e.g. Mayshar et al., 2022). But our evidence on the appearance of hereditary status suggests that persistent social differentiation was more likely a consequence rather than a precursor of state formation. Rather than intra-group coercion, it seems that intergroup competition was the leading channel of coercive state formation.

This paper also has implications for the broader literature on early state formation and state capacity. Seminal work by (Bates, 1987) argues that states emerge where gains from trade across ecological zones are possible. (Fenske, 2014) finds support for this argument. Similarly, (Michalopoulos et al., 2016; Michalopoulos et al., 2018) show both theoretically and empirically how Islam may have acted as a state-building force along ancient trade routes by redistributing some of the gains from trade to potential predators living in poorly endowed regions. In this sense, this literature identifies a similar geographic configuration for state formation as we do.

This paper is also connected to the literature on state capacity initiated by (Besley and Persson, 2009; Besley and Persson, 2010). Our work provides evidence that the interplay of cooperative and coercive forces advancing the development of state capacity was already at work at the inception of the state. Our work also provides direct evidence for the role of external threats for the development of state capacity, as in (Gennaioli and Voth, 2015).

Context and Theory

Historical context.

A stable climate emerged around 10000 BCE, which facilitated the domestication of plants and animals in various places around the world (Purugganan and Fuller, 2009; Matranga, 2022). Over time, farming spread from initial centers of domestication to most places across the globe. Farmers lived in small-scale, sedentary, and autonomous communities tied together through kinship (Bogucki, 1999). There was little institutionalized authority or governance beyond the village community.

Starting around 4000 BCE, some of these groups consolidated into state societies, in which centralized governance extended beyond kinship, maintained by a specialized class of bureaucrats with the power to tax. Pristine, early states formed in Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, the Yellow River Valley, Mesoamerica, and the Andes (Fried, 1960; Service, 1975; Trigger, 2003).1 State formation without the influence of existing states was rare.2

Circumscription theory.

There are many theories of state formation across the social sciences.3 One of the most influential theories in anthropology was developed by Robert (Carneiro, 1970). He proposes that intergroup conflict intensifies due to competition over scarce farming resources. Losing a conflict leaves a group with two options: migrate away or accept domination by the victorious group. Accepting domination lays the foundation for the state in the form of a bureaucracy that institutionalizes the payment of tribute from the losing group to its rulers. Circumscription accelerates the timing of resource scarcity and hence intergroup competition, and it limits out-migration options of losing groups. It follows that circumscribed regions make for environments that are particularly conducive to state formation.4

But besides accelerating conflict and limiting out-migration, circumscription could also foster state formation through alternative channels. Circumscribed areas are likely to be places where economic rents are high, as they were the richest locations in their regions. The inequality associated with circumscribed locations may have served as a basis for the rise of elites or may have raised the need for protection from raiding by surrounding groups. Circumscribed areas may also exhibit higher population density, which in turn leads to more social interactions that require dispute resolution, such as trade in need of contract enforcement. Inequality, trade, population density, and warfare may all increase the returns to public goods provision and thereby the need for cooperation through government.

Coercion versus cooperation.

Theories of state formation are often grouped in their emphasis on either coercive or cooperative mechanisms. In fact, [p. 734]Carneiro_1970 saw his theory as a rebuke of cooperative (“voluntaristic”) theories of state formation. In its classic conception, circumscription is primarily coercive—operating through population pressure, warfare, and subsequent state formation—whereas an expanded version of circumscription theory adds cooperative mechanisms—increased trade, public goods provision, and urbanization. The goals of this paper are (a) to provide quantitative evidence for the role of circumscription in state formation, and (b) to investigate whether circumscription is mediated primarily in terms of the classic or expanded version of the theory, which allows us to speak to the relative importance of coercive and cooperative forces in state formation more generally.

Data and Measurement

Archaeological and Historical Data

We rely on two sources of archaeological and historical data to measure early state formation: the Atlas of World Archaeology and the Seshat Global History Databank.

Atlas of World Archaeology.

The Atlas is a collection of detailed maps and descriptions for all major regions of archaeological research, with a curated selection of archaeological sites from specialists in each region (Bahn, 2000). It has global coverage of a total of 7,708 archaeological sites from Paleolithic to the Classical period. Despite its broad coverage, there are three important limitations worth noting. First, it is not a systematic collection of all archaeological sites around the world but rather a sample that is in part determined by the authors' own research expertise and interests. Second, even if the Atlas were comprehensive, the extent to which archaeological evidence survived and was subjected to research varies from place to place, leaving us with a selected sample of sites. And third, there has been considerable progress in identifying archaeological sites in many parts of the world since 2000. Despite these limitations, coverage for regions of early state formation is quite dense and should give a fairly representative picture of pre-state and state locations, which is the main comparison in our empirical exercises.

To identify sites that are associated with early state formation, we use the information provided on sites in (Bahn, 2000) and supplement this information with a review of the anthropological literature for each region in which early states formed. We then cross-reference this assessment with the information in Seshat (see below). This results in a total of 238 early state sites across seven clusters.5 In addition to these sites, the Atlas also shows the location of 761 Paleolithic sites (pre-agriculture), 1,538 Neolithic sites (pre-state agriculture), and 5,171 sites of successive states, associated with societies that developed states that under the influence of existing states. Figure 1 shows the location of early state sites in blue as well as sites preceding state formation in orange.

Seshat Global History Databank.

Seshat is a collaborative effort across the social sciences to collect systematic data on historical societies on a global scale (Turchin et al., 2015; Francois et al., 2016). It covers 35 regions around the world, which were selected to be geographically and historically representative.6 They are marked as squares in Figure 1. Out of these 35 regions, eight are in areas that experienced early state formation. Two pairs of early state regions (in Mexico and Southern Mesopotamia) are fairly close together, so we treat each of these pairs as one cluster. The only early state region not covered in Seshat (but in the Atlas) is the Maya region.

Each region features between two and 33 societies from distinct periods. For each society, Seshat compiles dozens of variables capturing economic activity, social practices, and military technology, as well as contextual information and references to the literature supporting the coding of variables. Figure 2 shows all 372 societal periods covered in Seshat. We identify periods of state presence in each region by the first appearance of either bureaucrats or government buildings.7 Using only these two characteristics, the timing of early state formation in regions lines up closely with the review of archaeological evidence employed for the classification of sites in the Atlas. In regions classified as having developed early states independently of the influence of existing states, the first instance of state appearance is interpreted as early state formation. In other regions, the state may still appear at a later point in time, but these are considered instances of state formation under significant outside influence.

In addition to markers of state formation, Seshat allows us to observe features like the first appearance of urbanization, organized warfare, food storage, irrigation, markets, currency, organized religion, and hereditary status. Indicators of population pressure and organized warfare may provide evidence for the classic, coercive circumscription theory. The expanded theory also considers other phenomena, in particular those that suggest the presence of cooperative forces, such as public goods provision or trade.

Extended regions.

We extend the geographic coverage of the 35 Seshat regions by sampling pre-state sites from the Atlas that (a) have the highest number of sites within the radius used for the measurement of circumscription (see below); (b) do not overlap with any other region; and (c) have a least five sites in them. This resulted in 81 regions. See the opaque circles in Figure 1. While this extended sample of regions does not have the rich historical information collected in Seshat, it allows us to study a larger sample of geographic regions from locations that had substantial pre-state activity according to the density of archaeological sites in the Atlas. Out of these 81 regions, 14 are early state formation regions. The six additional regions are based on early state sites in Guatemala, coastal Peru, Northern Mesopotamia, and the Eastern Indus valley. As before, we treat regions that were closely linked as clusters.

Environmental Data

Agricultural productivity.

As a proxy for agricultural productivity, we use data on the maximal potential production capacity (PPC) in tons per hectare over seventeen crops from the FAO's Global Agroecological Zones (GAEZ) database (Fischer et al., 2008), scaled by historical calories per ton for each crop by the FAO (Chatfield, 1953) and corrected for the Columbian Exchange (Nunn and Qian, 2010). The GAEZ database covers total crop production value for all important crops across the globe on a 112 degree grid size (i.e. a cell width of about 9km).8 Appendix Figure A3 shows a map of the resulting agricultural productivity data; details about data construction are described in Appendix ?.9

This proxy is constructed to capture stable conditions of agricultural productivity in the pre-state era. PPC is based on agro-climatic resources available in each cell that are slow-moving and unlikely to be strongly affected by human activity. These resources include temperature, precipitation, altitude, and soil conditions, accounting for the optimal conditions and growing periods of each crop. Scaling by historical calorie productivity ensures that it is similar to its productivity after domestication, rather than affected by the calorie gains many crops experienced in the context of the Green Revolution (Gollin et al., 2021). Restricting the availability of crops to the world region in which it was domesticated ameliorates the concern that a location may not have had access to the crop that would be most productive to cultivate there.10 Despite these precautions, it is likely that this proxy still suffers from measurement error, which we discuss in Section ?.

Measuring circumscription.

To quantify the intensity of circumscription between a location and its surrounding land, we compute the difference between the productivity in a given cell and the average productivity of all surrounding cells at various radii. To this end, we divide the entire landmass of the globe into cells with a width of 14 degrees (28km at the equator), which results in 176,512 cells. We compute circumscription as $a_i - \tilde{a}_{ir}$, with local productivity $a_i$ and surrounding productivity $\tilde{a}_{ir}$, where \[ \tilde{a}_{ir} = \frac{1}{N_r} \left[ \sum_{j \neq i:\text{dist}(i,j) \leq r} a_j \right] \] for radius $r$ in cell units, typically $r=10$, which is 278km at the equator with cell size 14 degree. $N_r$ is the number of cells (minus the cell at $i$) in the circle with radius $r$. In contrast to agricultural productivity per se, circumscription intensity is relatively low in large fertile expanses such as the American Midwest or Western Eurasian steppes since most cells are surrounded by equally productive cells; on the other hand, circumscription intensity is high in areas that are agriculturally productive but confined in otherwise barren land or surrounded by oceans, such as the river valleys of the Nile or the Indus, or the islands of Cuba or Sicily. Figure 3 shows a map of circumscription.

Intuitively, $a_i - \tilde{a}_i$ measures the difference between agricultural productivity in a given cell and the average surrounding cell. While this measure of circumscription is fairly crude, it is simple, transparent, and straightforward to compute. Furthermore, we can adjust cell size and radius of the measure to gauge its robustness. See Appendix ? for details on the measurement of circumscription.

Other datasets.

In addition to agricultural productivity, we include a number of other climatic, topographic, soil, and ecological factors in our dataset. We take annual mean and standard deviation of temperature from the from the WorldClim global climate database (Hijmans et al., 2005); river flow freshwater accumulation from the HydroSHEDS database (Lehner et al., 2008); measures of ecological diversity using potential vegetation from SAGE (Ramankutty and Foley, 1999); topographic slope and ruggedness (Riley et al., 1999; Burchfield et al., 2006; Nunn and Puga, 2012); and population distribution estimates from HYDE (Goldewijk, 2001).

Analysis Datasets

These archaeological, historical, and environmental datasets are combined into four distinct datasets for analysis purposes, ranging from a broad comparison between macro-regions to a fine-grained comparison of land cells on a spatial grid. The first dataset consists of the 35 regions covered by Seshat. The second dataset consists of the 81 extended regions based on the combination of Seshat and Atlas sites. The third dataset is made up of the 2,393 archaeological sites related to pre-state and state activity from the Atlas between the 60$^\text{th}$ Southern and Northern parallels.11 Finally, the fourth is made up of 176,512 land cells around the globe between the 60$^\text{th}$ Southern and Northern parallels on a 14 degree spatial grid, corresponding to a cell width of 28km at the equator.

Comparing the regions dataset to the Seshat dataset, we can see that the additional regions drawn from the Atlas sites render the dataset more comparable to the typical land cell: the set of 81 regions includes locations that are more likely in temperate climate and at a similar latitude as all land cells. Estimation Strategy

We are now interested in whether this relationship is statistically significant and robust to alternative explanations. The thought experiment we have in mind is one in which we compare two regions that are similar in many ways—such as their agricultural productivity, their climate, their topography, and so forth—but one of them is more circumscribed than the other. To this end, we run the following regression across all four analysis datasets:

\begin{equation} Y_i = (a_i - \tilde{a}_i)\beta + a_i \gamma + \mathbf{X}_i'\eta + u_i \end{equation}

where $i$ indexes observations, which could be regions, sites, or land cells. $Y_i$ is an indicator equal to one if a state site is present in the cell and zero otherwise. $a_i - \tilde{a}_i$ is circumscription, and $a_i$ is agricultural productivity. $\mathbf{X}_i$ is a vector of controls. Finally, $u_i$ is an error term that allows for spatial correlation across nearby observations.13

The coefficient $\beta$ quantifies the increase in probability of finding a state site in a cell for a one standard-deviation increase in circumscription $a_i - \tilde{a}_i$. If circumscription is an important correlate of early state formation, then estimates of $\beta$ are significantly positive even when controlling for agricultural productivity and other covariates. In contrast, if state formation is mainly driven by productivity itself, our estimate of $\beta$ may be near zero.

Measurement error in agricultural productivity.

As described in Section ?, we take several steps to ensure that our measure of agricultural productivity is a reasonable representation of historical conditions. Despite all of these cautionary steps, it is likely that it still suffers from some measurement error due to noise in the GAEZ productivity measure or due to changes in the environment over time.14 If this measurement error is uncorrelated with state formation, then it will make it generally harder to detect a correlation of circumscription with state formation. This is because the noise in the data will attenuate the productivity gradient between any location and its surroundings, and as a result, it is harder to distinguish locations that were historically circumscribed from ones that were not.

Causal interpretation of circumscription.

For the estimates of $\beta$ to be interpreted as causal, we need the degree of circumscription to be predetermined relative to state formation. If state formation itself affects the degree of circumscription, or if a third factor drives both state formation and circumscription, this may not be the case. One such possibility is that measurement error in agricultural productivity is correlated with state formation. Specifically, the historical presence of the state could lead to environmental degradation that would affect our measure of agricultural productivity. However, if early state societies were to degrade the environment, it is likely that agricultural production and hence degradation would be more intense near the center of early states rather than the periphery. As a result, this type of degradation would decrease the degree of circumscription of early states, making it harder to detect their correlation. To address the possibility that a third factor drives both circumscription and state formation, we control for a host of variables associated with alternative theories of state formation and run a placebo test using the timing of the Neolithic transition.

Heterogeneity across early state regions.

Figure A4 shows the density of circumscription across all land cells as well as averages of early state sites by region. State sites in all seven regions are substantially circumscribed, although the degree of circumscription varies considerably. The two most circumscribed ones are Mexico (including both the Basin of Mexico and the Valley of Oaxaca) and Egypt, with averages above one; the least circumscribed are the Indus Valley and China. In the case of the Indus Valley, it is likely that the changes in monsoon that shifted the regional climate (Giosan et al., 2012) make it hard to get an accurate picture of agricultural productivity at the time of the Indus Valley civilization. However, the Central Plains region of China adjacent to where the state first formed does indeed seem less circumscribed, with hundreds of kilometers of fertile land stretching eastwards from the area of initial state formation.

Additional results.

We present a number of additional results and robustness tests in the appendix, including the timing of state formation using cross-country data in Appendix 10. So far, all results presented were using a grid with cell size of 14 degrees (28km at the equator) and circumscription radius of $r=10$ (278km at the equator). Appendix Figure A5 presents results varying the cell size between 15 and 13 degrees (22km and 37km, respectively) as well as radii between 4 and 20 cell units. Each point in the graph represents an estimate from a separate regression, with corresponding confidence intervals extending vertically. We use all land cells and specification with controls from Table 2. Although the estimates are not very sensitive to the choice of radius, we see that the point estimates tend to drop off slightly with larger radii. Depending on cell size, a distance of around 100-250km is most strongly associated with state formation. This “Goldilocks” circumscription radius for early state formation is broadly consistent with the approximate reach of early states (Spencer, 1990; Covey, 2006).

Given the prominence of theories of state formation that stress the importance of alluvial agriculture (Mayshar et al., 2017; Heldring et al., 2019; Mayoral and Olsson, 2022), we investigate whether circumscription matters for state formation in large river valleys.16 Appendix Figure A6 shows estimates for top 10% and top 5% river cells by Paleolithic sites, Neolithic sites, state sites, and post-state sites. We find that circumscription is uncorrelated with the presence of pre-state (Paleolithic and Neolithic) sites and only becomes correlated for state sites. The correlation falls again slightly for post-state sites. This implies that among river cells, state formation took place in more circumscribed locations.

An important concern is that the correlation between circumscription and state formation is driven by the timing of the Neolithic transition (that is, the domestication of plants and animals). In a placebo test, we provide evidence that this timing is unrelated to circumscription using a separate dataset on the spread of agriculture in Western Asia and Europe (Pinhasi et al., 2005). This addresses the concern that early states simply formed in locations that adopted farming earlier, and circumscription may simply capture the speed of adoption. As can be seen in Appendix Figure A7, the correlation of circumscription with the timing of the Neolithic transition is small close to zero.

Mechanisms

So far, we have shown that circumscription is indeed a robust predictor of early state formation. We now turn to the question of whether the classic version or the expanded version of circumscription theory finds more support in the data, on the basis of the rich information provided in the Seshat database. To this end, we pursue three approaches. In Section ?, we investigate the sequence of events that unfolded in early state formation regions as documented in Seshat. This sequence can speak to the causal chain in effect prior to state formation. In Section ?, we then construct comparative case studies, in which we match each early state region to another region in Seshat that is much less circumscribed but similar in other ways. The comparison of “treated” (i.e. circumscribed) to “control” (i.e. non-circumscribed) timeline since the adoption of agriculture allows us to contrast the events preceding state formation with another region that was not subject to the dynamics of circumscription. Finally, in Section ? we evaluate to what extent societies in treated and control regions differ systematically along both coercive and cooperative phenomena before state formation.

Operationalizing mechanisms.

The classic version of Carneiro's theory posits that circumscription increases population pressure, which drives organized warfare and ultimately leads to state formation. To measure population pressure, we use four indicators, reflecting various aspects of the “urban revolution” (Childe, 1950). These indicators are urbanization (the largest settlement has at least 5,000 inhabitants), high population density ($>$ 1 person/km$^2$), the appearance of settlement hubs (documented as having at least three layers in the settlement hierarchy), or episodes of population in-migration. The idea is that sufficiently high population density and a network of subordinate communities provide the basis for urbanization, which is likely to be associated with population pressure. We operationalize organized warfare by the strategic placement of settlements in defensive positions, by the presence of defensive structures such as ramparts or palisades, or by the existence of a professional class of soldiers.

The expanded version of circumscription theory allows for a wider set of mechanisms to be at work. This includes cooperative channels, in particular the need to coordinate and govern public goods provision and trade. We measure public goods through the presence of food storage facilities, irrigation systems, roads, drinking water supply systems, and ports. We capture trade through the presence of markets or currency (tokens or precious metals). In addition to population pressure, urbanization may also create demand to regulate the public space in a cooperative manner. Hence, indicators of increased population can point both towards coercion as well as cooperation. We also investigate the presence of formal legal codes, organized religion, and inequality. We measure the latter in the form of hereditary status. Rather than intergroup conflict in the form of organized warfare, this phenomenon would point to internal differentiation and extraction by elites as an important driving force, especially in the absence of warfare.

Sequence of Change in Regions of Early State Formation

Figure 5 shows the sequence of social and economic change in all eight early state formation regions covered in Seshat. The timelines are sorted according to the chronology of early state formation, starting from Southern Mesopotamia in 4000 BCE at the top. For each early state formation region, we display the start and end points of each societal period. Blue triangles indicate the beginning of the period in which we first observe the state, using the presence of bureaucrats or government buildings as our markers of state formation. Other symbols indicate the first appearance of various phenomena that speak to potential mechanisms underlying state formation.

In line with the classic version of circumscription theory, we see that evidence of organized warfare typically appears before the state. However, this is not always the case: in Upper Egypt and the Kachi Plain, signs of intergroup conflict only appear after state formation. There is also evidence for population pressure appearing before state formation: this happens in all cases except Southern Mesopotamia and the Kachi Plain, where signs of urbanization coincide with state formation. Although most cases show some evidence of population pressure, it usually appears only after the appearance of organized warfare, in contrast to the causal chain proposed by Carneiro. It could be that population pressure is simply harder to pick up in the archaeological record than organized warfare. But together with the two cases that lack any signs of organized conflict between farming communities before state formation (Upper Egypt and Kachi Plain), it casts some doubt on theories of external conflict as the sole driver of state formation.

In addition to these markers associated with the classic theory, we find meaningful evidence that indicates pathways for coordination and cooperative governance. We see public goods appear before state formation in every single early state region. Remarkably, every instance of early state formation was preceded by the appearance of public irrigation systems. This is in line with a long tradition of theories on irrigation as a driver of state formation going back to (Wittfogel, 1938) due to the cooperative demands to manage these systems. We also see signs of trade appear before state formation in all but two instances, which could create demand for rule and contract enforcement by the state. Finally, we find evidence that hereditary status was present before the state in two early state regions: the Middle Yellow River Valley and the Valley of Oaxaca.

Comparative Case Studies

One important limitation of the time series analyses of the previous section is that we cannot compare the events that unfolded in early state regions to counterfactual sequences that might have taken place in the absence of circumscription. Consequently, we do not know whether these events were typical features of social and economic change of early societies, no matter whether they brought about early states or not. To make progress on this question, we now develop a design that matches early state regions to regions that were similar in some respects but were less circumscribed and did not give rise to early states.18 We can then compare the sequence of events in early state regions to these matched comparison regions to get a sense of whether their time series were typical or not. After studying these comparative case studies, we then investigate systematic differences in pre-state societies in early state regions to those in matched comparison regions to isolate which phenomena were more likely to emerge in circumscribed early state regions. In this way, we can isolate the causal pathways leading from circumscription to state formation, both via those of the classic, coercive theory, as well as mechanisms in the expanded version of circumscription theory.

Research design.

To systematically match “treated” early state regions to “control” regions that are not circumscribed and did not give rise to early states, we proceed as follows. We begin by reducing the set of 35 Seshat regions to those that did not experience early state formation yet had sufficiently long pre-state histories and low values of circumscription. Of the 27 such Seshat regions, we drop (a) the four island regions with relatively short pre-state histories (Big Island Hawaii, Iceland, Chuuk Islands, and Central Java); (b) the nine regions that are classified as regional latecomers by Seshat, which also have only short histories; and (c) the five control regions with a circumscription value above the median among remaining regions. This leaves us with a donor pool of nine regions as potential comparisons to early state regions.19

In matching early state regions to donors, we seek to make each pair of regions as similar as possible to one another on characteristics that are predictive of state formation. Given the importance of alluvial agriculture and the role of the Neolithic transition, we begin by matching on river size and absolute latitude. We match on a larger set of variables further below to ensure that the choice of matching variables does not meaningfully change our results. Let $\mathbf{X}_i$ be a vector of matching variables for region $i$ and $m(i)$ be the comparison region matched to $i$. For a set $\mathcal{T}$ of indices of early state regions, we seek to minimize $\sum_{i\in\mathcal{T}} || \mathbf{X}_i - \mathbf{X}_{m(i)} ||$ for some distance metric $||\cdot||$.20

To ensure the best unique match, we match without replacement so that every comparison region is only used once and search for the set of matches that minimizes the sum of differences across all matched pairs.21 Letting $\mathcal{C}$ be the indices of comparison regions, we can define $\tilde{m}:\mathcal{T}\rightarrow\mathcal{C}$ as a potential mapping of early state regions to comparison regions. The optimal mapping can then be defined as \[ m(\cdot) = \arg\min_{\tilde{m}(\cdot)} \sum_{i\in\mathcal{T}} || \mathbf{X}_i - \mathbf{X}_{\tilde{m}(i)} ||. \]

Egypt vs Cahokia.

The first comparative case study juxtaposes societal change in Upper Egypt in the Nile Valley to Cahokia in the Mississippi Valley. While this comparison has a number of drawbacks—for example, the two regions are not on the same continent—its appeal lies in the fact that both of them are in fertile river valleys with large, navigable rivers, but only Upper Egypt is circumscribed, whereas productive land stretches for hundreds of kilometers in all directions around Cahokia.

This juxtaposition shows a striking contrast in the first appearance of phenomena long before state formation: irrigation emerges in Upper Egypt only about 1,000 years after the arrival of crops, whereas it takes around 3,000 years in Cahokia and appears only after signs of organized warfare and population pressure. Even if one were to treat the arrival of maize and other cultural knowledge from Mesoamerica roughly 2,000 years after the original domestication of chenopods in Cahokia as the relevant starting point, the comparison of these two regions would still suggest that non-circumscribed regions are more prone to organized warfare than circumscribed regions, given the appearance of conflict before trade and public goods provision in Cahokia. In sum, what stands out in this case is the earlier appearance of public goods and trade in the circumscribed region as well as the speed of societal change after the Neolithic transition.22

Kachi Plain vs Middle Ganga. The second case compares the Kachi Plain to Middle Ganga, two regions in South Asia at the confluence of several rivers. This comparison is appealing because of the relative geographic proximity of the two regions (in addition to their similarity in terms of latitude and river size). At the same time, they are strikingly different in terms of environmental circumscription: the Kachi Plain lies at the edge of the Indus river valley, which cuts through an arid plain between the Iranian Plateau and the Thar Desert, whereas the comparison region lies at the Ganges, which is one of the most fertile plains on the planet.

This comparison again shows an accelerated pace of social and economic change before state formation in the treated region relative to the comparison region. In the Kachi Plain, public goods and trade activity emerge only a few hundred years after the arrival of crops, whereas it takes more than 3,000 for these phenomena to appear in Middle Ganga. When they do, it is together with signs of organized conflict and inequality in the form of hereditary status. Here again, the pace of social change seems to be accelerated, leading with signs of cooperation.

Differential Characteristics of Pre-State Societies

Overall, these case studies suggest that the pace of social and economic change is typically faster in circumscribed areas. Relative to less circumscribed comparison regions, this change is often driven by public goods and trade. We now generalize these examples to all societies that emerged before the state. To this end, we apply the timing of state formation since the arrival of crops in the treated region across all pre-state societies in both the treated and the comparison region of each matched pair. For example, since the state emerged in Upper Egypt around 1,800 years after after crop arrival, we only include societies from Cahokia within that timeframe.

Figure 8 shows the share of treated and control societies that experienced our phenomena of interest before the arrival of the state in treated regions. One way to interpret these differences is in the form of a pre-state balance test, which allows us to test to what extent societies in circumscribed regions that ultimately gave rise to early states were already on a different trajectory than similar regions that were less circumscribed. Indeed, we see striking differences along several dimensions: pre-state societies in circumscribed regions were more likely to exhibit signs of organized warfare, public goods, and trade. The evidence is particularly strong for differences in the presence of public goods. Indicators of population pressure are also slightly elevated in circumscribed pre-state societies, but not significantly so.23 In contrast, there is little evidence that these societies experienced more social stratification, at least in terms of hereditary status.

Mechanisms: summary. Overall, the time series of events in early state regions and the matched comparison of circumscribed early state regions to less circumscribed comparison regions suggest that there is a spectrum of mechanisms underlying the link between circumscription and early state formation. Some regions do indeed exhibit the pattern of population pressure driving competition over arable land, leading to war between neighboring farming communities, and eventually giving rise to the state—exactly as predicted by the classic, coercive version of circumscription theory. But other regions exhibit very different patterns of societal change, both in the time series as well as in comparison to similar regions. These cases conform much better to a cooperative theory of how circumscription facilitated state formation whereby the need or opportunity for coordination fostered state formation. The evidence for many regions falls in between these two extremes, suggesting that both cooperative and coercive forces are at work, in line with an expanded theory of circumscription.

Conclusion

This paper provides quantitative evidence that environmental circumscription facilitated early state formation, both through coercive and cooperative channels. On the basis of two archaeological datasets, we identify the geography and history of early state formation on a global scale. We measure circumscription as the agricultural productivity gradient for each location relative to surrounding areas, using global environmental data on historical potential production capacity of relevant crops. The first contribution is to show that circumscription is correlated with early state formation, even after controlling for a number of alternative factors that could be driving their relationship. In a second contribution, we show that organized warfare, public good provision, and trade may all contribute to accelerated societal change resulting in state formation.

Our updated circumscription theory affirms that coercion is an important component of early state formation. But circumscription did not exclusively operate through the coercive lock-in of farming groups. Rather, it precipitated a number of social and economic phenomena, including cooperative mechanisms that go beyond Carneiro's classical theory. We also recognize the limits of the explanatory power of circumscription in the state formation process: circumscription is neither necessary—as the weakly circumscribed Middle Yellow River Valley exemplifies—nor sufficient—as heavily circumscribed Sogdiana in Central Asia illustrates.

These findings prompt several research questions that call for quantitative analysis. Even though pristine state formation was rare, states came to dominate almost all societies. How quickly did the spread of statehood take place? Which places were more susceptible to states and why? What role did the interaction of incipient states with each other and with nearby pastoralists play? And what legacy did the first generation of states leave in the subsequent development of statehood? Providing answers to these questions in future research can help explain the divergent development of states around the world that is still evident today.

Overview of Bahn_2000 . The Atlas of World Archaeology is a collection of maps and descriptions summarizing archaeological research across a global scale, from the origins of the human species in the Paleolithic up to the Early Modern era (c. 500 CE) in the Old World (Europe and Asia) and up to the arrival of Europeans in Africa, the Americas, and Australia. The Atlas is organized in three parts: the first part studies the development of Homo Sapiens and its spread across the globe; the second part summarizes major achievements by humans in the 10,000 years since the end of the last Ice Age; and the third part proceeds to documents these achievements in greater detail across five world regions (Europe and Western Asia; Central, South, and East Asia; Africa; the Americas; and Australia and the Pacific). There are a total of 73 maps showing the location of important archaeological sites as point features, approximate areas of cultural activity as polygon and line features, and various sources of commodities as point features. An example can be seen in Figure A9.

While any archaeological atlas is subject to the basic distortions of all of archaeology [p. 13]Bahn_2000, the Atlas makes a conscious effort to offer a global representative sample of across place and time. Despite the historical focus on Europe and Western Asia in Archaeology, more than two-thirds of the Atlas are devoted to other regions. The key theme is the rise of civilizations, covering pyrotechnology (kiln and forge), the Neolithic transition (i.e. the domestication of plants and animals), the emergence of cities and states, the development of writing, and the creation of early empires.

Processing and classifying Bahn. All maps except the one devoted to the Neandertals were scanned and georectified. Then, all point features (sites and sources) were digitized, with information on the type of site according to the legend, the site name (whenever available), and whether it is a source of commodity or not. No polygon and line features were digitized. In total, the 72 maps include 8,599 point features, 7,708 of which are archaeological sites (the rest are either sites of other species of Homo, shipwrecks, or sources of commodities). A detailed overview of all maps as well as a shapefile with all point features is available from the author.

To identify sites relevant to early state formation, we proceed as follows. First, maps were broadly categorized into three broad stages: (1) pre-state maps: maps depicting cultures with evidence on paleolithic or neolithic activity preceding state societies. These may occasionally include simple chiefdoms. (2) state maps: maps showing cultures experiencing state formation for the first time in the region. (3) post-state maps: these show cultures that emerged after early states had already formed in the region. In line with the consensus in the archaeological literature (Childe, 1950; Trigger, 2003), seven regions experienced pristine early state formation, with little or no contamination from earlier experiences of state formation. In each case, only the first page of each region with sites associated with state formation was chosen, as later pages in the same region would typically fall into the post-state category.

Second, after cross-referencing the information with the presence of bureaucrats or government buildings in Seshat, we selected the following sites from each early state region:

  • Mesopotamia: Uruk sites on page 61. In Seshat, the evidence on the presence of bureaucrats and government buildings is controversial, in part because the buildings associated with this period may have also taken religious functions, as opposed to exclusively administrative functions. Given the consensus in the anthropological literature that Uruk was indeed a state society (Trigger, 2003), we code it accordingly.
  • Indus: All sites on page 115 except contemporary non-Indus sites. There is not much ancillary information on the exact set of sites first associated with state formation, so we use all Indus sites listed on this page.
  • China: Shang sites on page 117, restricted to approximate Xia dynasty borders. Seshat identifies Erlitou as the first period of state formation, which is consistent with the extent of the Xia dynasty.
  • Egypt: Naqada II or III culture sites from page 140. Seshat identifies Naqada II as the first period of state formation. We cannot distinguish between Naqada II and III in the map, so we choose both categories.
  • Mexico: Major Classic sites on page 168. Other Classic sites are described as having considerable influence from major Classic sites, so we restrict early state formation to major Classic sites. These sites cover the most important sites both in the Basin and Mexico and the Valley of Oaxaca.
  • Maya: Late Formative period sites on page 170. There is no information on the Maya in Seshat, but Bahn (p. 171) describes evidence of “concentrated political authority” at Late Formative period sites.
  • Peru: Moche, Tiwanaku, and Wari sites on page 179. Seshat covers only Cuzco, which was eventually part of the Wari state.24 Stanish_2001 (Stanish (2001), p. 55) carefully evaluates the evidence on state formation in South America, identifying the three cultures as the origin of the state in the region.

It should be noted that results are robust to making changes to the inclusion rule of state sites. The goal was to identify the exact locations that first underwent the transition to state societies. Most results are unchanged if simply using all sites from the state maps, and most sites that were classified as post-states but could arguably be considered still in the general phase of early state formation would strengthen the results.

Seshat: Global History Databank

The Seshat Global History Databank brings data together, in a structured format, for historical societies from the neolithic revolution onwards. Currently data for approximately 2000 variables pertaining to a range of topics, including social complexity, warfare, ritual and religion, equality, and norms and institutions, are being captured. In addition, to the data itself the Databank also captures contextual information, references to the literature supporting the codes, and information on levels of uncertainty and scholarly agreement. Seshat thus not only offers a vast browsable comparative dataset but also allows users to test hypotheses quantitatively with historical data. In a first phase of the project data was gathered for a spatial sample of 30 time series, centered around a Natural Geographic Area (NGA). In the current phase of the project additional time series are being added and areas in between time series are being filled in.

More information and replication files for Seshat can be found on the Seshat website. The database is documented in (Turchin et al., 2015), (Francois et al., 2016), and (Turchin et al., 2017).

Environmental and Geographic Data

Agricultural productivity proxy.

Agricultural productivity is the maximum potential production capacity in tons per hectare over the seventeen crops (buckwheat, barley, chickpea, foxtail millet, groundnut, maize, oat, pearl millet, wetland rice, rape, rye, sunflower, soybean, sweet potato, sorghum, wheat and white potato) after correcting for the Columbian Exchange (e.g. no wheat in the Americas, no potatoes in Eurasia). See the GAEZ website for the database.

We use the earliest data available (baseline period 1961-1990), with intermediate input level and irrigated water supply. While the data is also available for rainfall water supply, it renders areas that were very productive but relied on river flooding and alluvial agriculture completely unproductive if they are in arid environments. Note that most early civilizations substantially relied on irrigation based on freshwater delivered via rivers. As such, the data with rainfall water supply does not represent a good proxy for agricultural land quality when other water sources are available.25 Concerning the input level, the intermediate level is the lowest for irrigated water supply.

Next, we apply restrictions from the Columbian Exchange on which crops are available to a given region. To this end, we divide the crops into Old World crops, applied to Africa, Asia and Europe, and New World Crops, applied to the Americas. New World crops are maize, sweet potato, white potato and sunflower; the others are Old World crops.

In the next step, each crop is scaled using the historical Food Composition Tables by the FAO (Chatfield, 1953), using calorie values per 100 grams for each crop. Finally, we take the maximal calorie amount across all crops available in a region as the land quality proxy.

Circumscription.

To compute circumscription, we subtract average agricultural productivity in all cells within radius $r$ (without the cell of interest) from the value at the cell of interest, as described in the main text and illustrated in Figure A1.

Ecological diversity.

To estimate ecological diversity, we use data from the Center for Sustainability and the Global Environment (SAGE) in the Nelson Institute at the University of Wisconsin-Madison. The data consists of a global map of natural vegetation at a 5 min resolution classified into 15 vegetation types. The data is representative of the world's “potential” vegetation (i.e., vegetation that would most likely exist now in the absence of human activities). See (Ramankutty and Foley, 1999) for further details. The data is here.

As described in (Fenske, 2014), the ecological diversity index is the Herfindahl index constructed from shares $s^t$ of various ecological types $t=1,...,T$. We use SAGE's potential vegetation as the ecological types. In the case of our grid cell data set, we compute for each cell $i$: \[ \text{EcoDiv}_{ir} = 1 - \sum_{t=1} ^T \left( s_{ir} ^t \right)^2 \] where \[ s_{ir} ^t = \frac{1}{N_r} \sum_{j: \text{dist}(i,j) \leq r} n_j ^t \] with $n_i ^t$ being an indicator for cell $i$ having ecological type $t$ and $N_r$ as the sum of cells within $r$ as before. As usual, we use $r=10$ as our default radius. Similarly, ecological polarization is \[ \text{EcoDiv}_{ir} = 1 - \sum_{t=1} ^T \left(1 - 2s_{ir} ^t \right)^2 s_{ir} ^t. \]

Mean and standard deviation of temperature.

Data on the annual mean and standard deviation temperature are from the WorldClim global climate database. We use current condition (1960-1990) 5 minute resolution data for mean monthly temperature. This is averaged over the year to get annual mean temperature for each cell. The standard deviation is taken across months for each grid cell. See here for the data source.

Ruggedness and slope.

The Terrain Ruggedness Index was developed in (Riley et al., 1999). The data on ruggedness and slope used here the grid-cell level data prepared for (Nunn and Puga, 2012). The data is described in detail on their data repository here.

River flow accumulation.

River flow accumulation data is from the Hydrological data by the SHuttle Elevation Derivatives at multiple Scales (HydroSHEDS) project, which offers hydrographic information between the 60$^\text{th}$ Southern and Northern parallels. River flow accumulation is derived primarily from elevation data of the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM) at 3 arc-second resolution and measures the number of cells of drainage accumulation due to the elevation data. See here.

HYDE 3.2 historical population estimates.

The History Database of the Global Environment (HYDE) is an internally consistent combination of historical population estimates and allocation algorithms with time-dependent weighting maps for land use (Klein Goldewijk et al., 2017). Population is represented by maps of total, urban, rural population, population density and built-up area, with a resolution of 112 degree resolution. The period covered is 10000 before Common Era (BCE) to 2015 Common Era (CE). All data can be downloaded from here.
